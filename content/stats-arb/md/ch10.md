Here is the curve that gets a strategy funded. Take the HDFCBANK / KOTAKBANK pairs trade the previous chapter built - the same frozen hedge ratio, the same z-score, enter at plus-or-minus two standard deviations, exit at the mean, hard-stop at four - run it over 2019 to 2023, and it prints a **net Sharpe of 1.01**, a total return of **+110.5%**, and drawdowns shallow enough to sleep through. Twenty-two clean round trips. It looks like money. This chapter takes that exact curve and dismantles it the way a risk committee would before a single rupee is committed, because a backtest is a hypothesis wearing the costume of a result. Between the pretty chart and a real profit-and-loss sit four leaks, and we are going to open each one on the same pair, with the real numbers, in this data window.

Two honesty rules are baked in before we start, so we are not fooling ourselves on the first page. The hedge ratio `b = 1.0545` is estimated on the **train window only** and then frozen - the test period never touches it. The z-score is **trailing** (a rolling 60-day window, about two half-lives), so the signal at any point uses only past data. The fill is **next-bar**: the signal forms at today's close and the trade is executed on the next bar. Costs are delivery (CNC) costs - a round trip is **0.474% of notional** - because a roughly 29-day half-life means positions are held for weeks, not squared off intraday. With those rules fixed, we open the leaks.

## Teardown one: the edge does not survive a blind window

The single most important question about any backtest is whether the rule works on data it was not built on. We froze the hedge ratio and the thresholds on 2019 to 2023 and applied them, completely unchanged, to **2024 to now** - data the model has never seen.

{{example: ch10/02_cell11.py | In-sample net Sharpe 1.01 collapses to 0.34 out of sample | RISK}}

The in-sample **net Sharpe of 1.01 falls to 0.34** out of sample - **about two-thirds of the edge gone** - leaving +11.5% over two and a half years across just 11 round trips, with a -13.8% drawdown. The shape of the curve changes too: the smooth in-sample climb becomes a sideways grind that happens to end up. That is not a strategy that "still works a bit"; it is a strategy whose headline number was mostly an artefact of the window it was tuned on.

A fairer test re-estimates the hedge ratio every quarter on the trailing two years and trades the next quarter forward - still strictly out-of-sample, but allowed to keep up with drift. That walk-forward lands at **Sharpe 0.62** overall and **+22.3% across 2024 onward** (the only genuinely recent slice). It recovers some of the loss, but it sits far below 1.01, and every refit is one more chance to overfit. There is no free lunch in re-estimation.

:::warn
The in-sample curve was a hypothesis, not a result. Held out blind, the net Sharpe fell by roughly 66% in this window. Any single backtested Sharpe you are shown without an out-of-sample number beside it should be treated as the optimistic end of a range, not the answer.
:::

## Teardown two: costs are not a footnote

A pair round trip is not one trade - it is **eight fills**: two legs, each bought and sold, on entry and on exit. Every fill pays statutory charges (securities transaction tax, exchange and regulator fees, GST, stamp duty), plus half the bid-ask spread it crosses, plus its own market impact. For a multi-week delivery hold the dominant term is the **0.1% transaction tax on the sell side** - there is no cheap intraday square-off to dodge it.

{{example: ch10/04_cell16.py | Costs are a haircut: gross Sharpe 0.90 down to net 0.77 | NSE}}

Over the full sample that is **33 round trips times 0.474% = 15.9% of capital paid away as costs.** Gross Sharpe **0.90 becomes net 0.77**; total return **+175% becomes +135%.** In this window costs are a haircut, not a guillotine - precisely because a plus-or-minus-two-standard-deviation rule trades rarely. But that verdict depends entirely on the cost you assume, and the half-spread and impact are the two numbers you can least defend in advance: they move with your size, the time of day, and how the order book looks when you actually trade.

So instead of trusting one number, sweep it. As the assumed round-trip cost rises, the **out-of-sample net Sharpe reaches zero at about 1.9%** round trip - and the out-of-sample line sits much closer to that zero axis than the in-sample line the whole way. The edge that has a comfortable cost cushion in-sample does not have the same cushion once it is out of sample. Your break-even is not a distant cliff; it is roughly four times the cost you hope to pay, and a bad fortnight of fills can spend that margin.

:::info
The cost verdict is a function of an assumption you cannot pin down before trading. When a strategy's sign flips somewhere between a 0.5% and a 2% round-trip cost, "is it profitable?" is the wrong question. The right one is "how confident am I in the 0.474%, and what does the answer look like if I am wrong by a factor of two?"
:::

## Teardown three: the relationship was never stable

Teardown one showed the edge fades out of sample. This is *why*. The whole strategy assumes one stable cointegrating relationship - a single hedge ratio, a spread that always comes home. We test that assumption directly with a rolling Engle-Granger p-value: is the pair cointegrated *right now*?

{{example: ch10/06_cell21.py | The pair fails the cointegration test in 90% of rolling windows | STAT}}

The rolling p-value runs from a min of 0.005 to a max of 0.978, and it sits at **0.734 in the latest window.** The pair **fails the 5% cointegration test in about 90% of rolling windows.** Reversion is the exception for this pair, not the rule - the clean, tradeable cointegration the build chapter found was one lucky stretch of data, not a permanent property of the two names.

The breakdown has a mechanical cause: the hedge ratio is not a constant. The rolling 120-day `b` swings from **-0.312 to 1.744**, a mean of 0.649 with a standard deviation of 0.417 - it wanders by **317% of its own mean and even turns negative**, which means the "hedge" briefly inverts and both legs point the same way. The frozen train value of 1.0545 sits about **+63% above the test-period average.** Out of sample the spread we trade is therefore **mis-hedged by construction**: it carries directional bank-sector risk that has nothing to do with mean reversion, and that leaked directional risk is exactly what shows up as the out-of-sample drawdowns.

:::key
De-cointegration is not a separate problem from the out-of-sample collapse - it is the engine of it. A fixed hedge ratio trades a snapshot of a regime that has already ended. When the spread fails the cointegration test in nine windows out of ten, you are not trading a tether that occasionally stretches; you are trading a directional bet that occasionally happens to revert.
:::

## Teardown four: leaks that flatter quietly

The first three leaks are about the future being unknowable. The fourth is about the future quietly leaking *into the past* inside your own code - and about the pairs you never see. The most dangerous version is a normalisation leak: standardising the spread with the **whole sample's** mean and standard deviation instead of a trailing window. It looks like innocent scaling, but the scaling factor secretly contains the test period's own range, so the held-out test is no longer blind.

The lesson is not that look-ahead always flatters; it is that a one-day shift in an assumption moves the result by more than the entire edge, in either direction.

Then there is the pair you are looking at. We did not stumble onto HDFCBANK / KOTAKBANK - the build process **scanned ten bank pairs and crowned the one that tested most cointegrated** in 2019 to 2023. That is selection, and it has a price.

{{example: ch10/10_cell29.py | Survivorship: 5 of 10 bank pairs lose money net out of sample | STAT}}

Our pair ranked **first of ten in-sample**; run the identical net strategy across all ten same-sector bank pairs and **5 of the 10 actually lose money net out of sample.** We did not find a good pair so much as run a beauty contest in-sample and crown a winner whose crown is worth little once the window changes. And these are only the banks **still in the index** - the pairs that merged or delisted are not on the chart at all, which means even this humbling picture is the flattering version.

:::warn
A low p-value pulled from a scan of many pairs is a hypothesis, not a discovery. Scan ten pairs and the best one looks special by construction; scan a hundred and a few will pass any test by chance alone. The honest number is not the winner's in-sample Sharpe - it is how the whole cohort, winners and losers and the delisted ghosts, did out of sample.
:::

## Where this breaks

The strategy did not fail because of a bug. It failed because a backtest measures how well a rule *fit the past*, while trading needs how well a rule *survives the future*, and the gap between those is everything we just measured.

- **The in-sample curve was a hypothesis.** Held out blind, the net Sharpe fell from 1.01 to 0.34 in this window. Re-fitting the hedge ratio recovers part of that to 0.62, but lands far below the headline and adds its own overfitting risk.
- **Costs are not a footnote.** Eight fills per round trip, dominated by delivery transaction tax, turned a comfortable gross 0.90 into a marginal net 0.77 - and the out-of-sample sign flips somewhere around a 1.9% round-trip cost you cannot pin down in advance.
- **The relationship was never stable.** The pair failed the cointegration test in about 90% of rolling windows and the hedge ratio swung 317% of its mean, even going negative. A fixed spread trades a regime that has already ended.
- **Leaks flatter quietly.** A one-day shift in the fill, or a full-sample normalisation, moved the result by more than the entire edge - in either direction - and we crowned the in-sample winner of ten survivors while the broken and delisted pairs stayed invisible.

The bottom line: naive pairs trading on HDFCBANK / KOTAKBANK, net of realistic NSE costs and out of sample, is **at best marginal** - a low single-digit Sharpe and a near-flat equity curve, before borrow, financing, slippage beyond our estimate, and the short-leg problem. A statistical relationship existed in one window. That is genuinely not the same thing as a tradable edge, and the difference between the two is the price of admission to everything that follows. This is not a counsel of despair; it is the standard every later technique - dynamic hedge ratios, baskets, cross-sectional neutrality, proper sizing, and validation that does not fool you - must clear before it earns a place in the book. Every one of them has to pass the same four leaks measured here. Educational content only, not investment advice.
