Every other chapter in this course opens a price panel in a fraction of a second and gets straight to the statistics. That speed is a lie of omission. Somebody had to pull ten years of daily bars for fifty names plus the index, once, and pin them to a local database so that nobody ever has to ask the broker for them again. This chapter is that somebody. It is the unglamorous plumbing under the whole module: where the data comes from, how it is stored, and - the part most tutorials skip - how you prove the cache is actually complete before you trust a single spread built on top of it. Get this wrong and every elegant cointegration test downstream is computing beautiful statistics on quietly broken data.

## Download once, read fast

The most common way to waste a research afternoon is to re-download history on every run. A single ten-year daily series is a few hundred kilobytes over the wire - trivial. But fifty of them, pulled repeatedly across a session of iterating on ideas, is thousands of networked round-trips you never needed to make, against an API that is rate-limited and will eventually push back.

The fix is a local cache. You pay the download cost exactly once with `source="api"`; from then on every `source="db"` read is a local columnar scan with no network anywhere in the path. OpenAlgo's store is a DuckDB file on disk, and the two regimes look like this - the slow, networked first run, and the instant steady state every run after.

## The fifty names and the index

The universe is the fifty current Nifty 50 constituents plus the NIFTY index itself, grouped by sector so that same-sector pairs - the natural hunting ground for a mean-reverting spread - sit next to each other. In this build that is fifty equities across fourteen sectors: five private banks, the NBFC and insurance block, IT, metals, energy, autos, pharma, FMCG, materials, infra and the rest. The equities live on exchange `NSE`; the index lives on its own exchange, `NSE_INDEX`, which matters because you will fetch it with a different argument and it is your calendar ground truth later.

One word of warning you should hear now and again at the end: this is *today's* membership, cached backwards. That single fact is the most flattering bias in the entire course, and we return to it with teeth in the final section.

## db vs api, measured

An expert does not take "the cache is faster" on faith. Time the identical call both ways on one liquid name, a few repetitions each, and report the measured ratio. The `api` path crosses the network to the broker and re-serialises the response; the `db` path is a local DuckDB query. Absolute numbers depend on your machine and the broker's mood, but the shape never changes.

{{example: ch02/01_cell7.py | One ten-year read: db cache vs api broker | DATA}}

In this data window the `db` read came back in a median **54.7 ms** against the `api` path's **346.0 ms** - the cache is **6.3x faster** on a single ten-year daily series. Multiply that by fifty names across a session of dozens of re-runs and the difference stops being a convenience and becomes the difference between an idea you can iterate on and one you abandon out of boredom.

:::key
The speed-up is not the point - reproducibility is. A `source="db"` read returns the *same bytes* every time, so a backtest you ran last month reruns identically today. A `source="api"` read can drift as the vendor revises history underneath you. Cache first, then research.
:::

## Inside the cache: the market_data table

OpenAlgo stores every bar in a DuckDB table called `market_data`, one row per `(symbol, exchange, interval, timestamp)`, where `timestamp` is a Unix epoch in seconds - integer keys are small, sort fast, and never trip over a timezone. A companion `data_catalog` table summarises each series (first timestamp, last timestamp, record count, last download time) so coverage can be checked without scanning the bars themselves. Writing a downloaded OHLCV frame into that schema is a tidy transform - cast the index to epoch seconds, coerce volume and open interest to integers - followed by an insert. Reading the catalog back out is a single `GROUP BY symbol` that any verification routine can trust.

:::info
The catalog is not decoration. Treat it as the cache's manifest: the cheapest possible "what do I actually have?" query. Every coverage check below could be run straight off `data_catalog` without touching a single price bar - which is exactly how a nightly job confirms a download finished before research opens the file.
:::

## Verifying coverage: rows, spans, and gaps

A download that *ran* is not a download that *succeeded*. Before trusting fifty series for research you verify every one - row count, first date, last date - and flag anything short or missing. Crucially this verification uses `source="db"` only: a pure local scan, no further downloads. The first view is simply how many daily bars each name carries.

{{example: ch02/02_cell19.py | Daily bars cached per symbol | NSE}}

A healthy daily cache is a flat block of bars sitting against the same ceiling, with a handful of short stubs. In this window the index calendar prints 2,477 sessions and the full-history names sit right up against it; the stubs - JIOFIN, ETERNAL, TMPV, SHRIRAMFIN - are genuinely younger series, recent listings or symbol renames, not data errors. You confirm that by looking at *when* each series starts, not just how many rows it has.

{{example: ch02/03_cell21.py | Cache coverage span per series | NSE}}

Row counts tell you how many bars; the span view tells you which window. Each bar runs from a series' first cached date to its last. Across this cache the earliest start is **2016-01-01** and every series ends on or around **2026-06-25** - the last common trading day. That alignment on the right edge is the thing you are checking for. A bar that stopped early would be a stale series nobody topped up, and on a two-legged spread a stale leg is not a small problem - it silently freezes one side of the relationship while the other keeps moving.

But row counts and spans can both look healthy and still hide internal holes - a missing Tuesday here, a skipped week there - that quietly corrupt a spread without ever showing up as a short history. To find them you need the set of dates the market was actually open. Rather than hardcode a holiday list that rots, use the NIFTY index series as the ground-truth NSE session calendar: the index prints on every trading day, by definition. For each symbol, within its own active window, a gap is any NIFTY session it is missing.

{{example: ch02/04_cell23.py | Internal missing sessions vs the NIFTY calendar | STAT}}

The verdict in this data window: against a 2,477-session calendar, **10 of 50** names are perfectly clean, and the worst single name is missing just **3 sessions**. Three. Those are almost certainly real trading halts or suspensions, not corruption - the kind of thing a sane cache lives with. But notice what this check buys you: it is the difference between "the file loaded" and "the file is right." If one name had been missing forty sessions, the row count alone might never have flagged it, and you would have discovered the hole the hard way, as a fake jump in a spread weeks later.

:::warn
A single bad or missing bar on one leg is far more dangerous in stat-arb than in directional trading. A spread is a *difference*; a hole on one side fabricates a move that never happened. Always re-run gap detection after any bulk download - the verification pass, not the download log, is your proof the cache is sound.
:::

## Keeping it current without re-downloading

Once the cache exists, keeping it current must be cheap. The wrong way is to re-download ten years every morning. The right way is to ask the cache for each symbol's last date, fetch only `[last+1 .. today]` from the API, and append with de-duplication so an overlapping re-fetch costs nothing.

:::tip
Build the loop to over-fetch on purpose - re-pull the last few weeks every night, not just yesterday. The de-dup throws away what you already have, and the overlap quietly heals any bar the vendor revised after you first cached it. The cost is a few extra kilobytes; the benefit is a cache that self-corrects.
:::

And because a real download loop hits the API hundreds of times, wrap every fetch in exponential backoff - on a timeout or a *too many requests*, wait, then double the wait a few times before giving up. It is ten lines of code and the difference between a download that survives a flaky network and one that dies half-written at symbol thirty.

## Where this breaks

This cache is a convenience, not a source of truth. The honest failure modes:

- **Rate limits and partial downloads.** A fifty-symbol pull is hundreds of API calls, and a mid-run *429* or timeout can leave a series half-written. The retry wrapper helps; the real guard is the verification pass above. Never assume a bulk download finished - re-verify coverage and gaps every time.
- **Adjustment is baked in at download time.** The bars are whatever the broker served *on the day they were fetched* - typically split- and dividend-adjusted to that date. A later corporate action retro-adjusts history at the vendor, so an old cached series can silently disagree with a fresh pull. The cached `close` is for *measuring* relationships, not for reconstructing the exact rupee you could have transacted at.
- **Vendor gaps and bad prints.** Missing sessions, zero-volume days, the odd spike or stale carry-forward come straight from the upstream feed. Gap detection finds the holes; it does not fix a wrong price. A spread is unforgiving of a single bad bar on one leg.
- **Point-in-time and survivorship.** This is *today's* Nifty 50, cached backwards. Names that were dropped, merged or delisted are simply absent, and recent renames - ETERNAL, TMPV, JIOFIN, SHRIRAMFIN - have discontinuous histories. A backtest on this universe is biased toward survivors. A true point-in-time database with delisted constituents is out of scope here, but it is the limitation that most flatters every result downstream, and you should never forget it is there.

With the database built, verified and kept current, the rest of the course simply reads it with `source="db"`. The next chapter assembles the clean research panel from this cache and states the trading-versus-adjusted-price caveats in full.
