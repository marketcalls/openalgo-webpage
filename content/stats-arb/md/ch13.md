Every strategy in this course so far has traded **one relationship at a time** - a pair, a Kalman-tracked pair, a Johansen basket. They all share the same ceiling: a single spread can only absorb so much capital, and the day it de-cointegrates you are left holding nothing but the wreckage of a hedge ratio that stopped working. This chapter builds the version of statistical arbitrage that does not have that ceiling - the one that runs across an entire universe at once, market-neutral by construction, and is the literal workhorse of every equity market-neutral desk on earth. It is the most *scalable* idea in the book. It is also, in this window and universe, **the cleanest demonstration of the course's central thesis: a statistical relationship existing is not the same thing as a tradeable edge existing.** The gross signal here is real, clean, and genuinely market-neutral. The book just turns over so fast that realistic NSE costs do not dent the edge - they **invert its sign**.

## Strip the market, keep the wiggle

The whole idea fits in one sentence: most of a stock's daily move is just the market (and its sector) moving, so strip that out, and what is left - the idiosyncratic residual - tends to over-react and snap back. The mechanics are a regression. Take any one name and regress its daily return on the NIFTY return; the slope is its beta, and the scatter around the fit line is the residual, the part of the day the market does not explain.

{{example: ch13/01_cell8.py | One stock vs NIFTY: the market explains 51% of AXISBANK's daily variance | STAT}}

For AXISBANK the market alone explains **R-squared of 51% of its daily variance**, and the residual standard deviation is **1.48% per day against a raw 2.11%**. Read that the right way round: when you hold a single stock long, you are mostly holding the market with a small idiosyncratic rider attached. A market-neutral book is only allowed to bet on the rider. Across the whole 50-name universe, removing the market strips out a median **31%** of each stock's daily variance; also removing the same-sector mean residual lifts that to a median **61%**. So roughly **two-thirds of a typical stock's daily wiggle is market plus sector**, and the residual book trades only the remaining third.

Two honesty rules are wired in from the start. The beta is a **120-day rolling estimate** - the beta on day `t` uses only returns up to `t`, never the future. And the sector neutralisation subtracts the *same-day* cross-sectional sector mean, so two banks both jumping on a banking-policy headline do not manufacture a fake signal between them. The notebook's phrase is the right one: "optionally remove sector" is not optional in practice - it is where most of the clean edge lives.

## From residual to a market-neutral book

The residual is the raw material; the signal is the negative of each name's last `k`-day cumulative residual, then demeaned across the universe. A stock whose residual has fallen hard over the last couple of days gets a **high** signal and becomes a buy candidate; one whose residual has run up gets a **low** signal and becomes a short. Demeaning - subtracting the cross-sectional average - is what makes the book dollar-neutral by construction: the longs and shorts have to balance.

Turning that signal into a book is three lines of arithmetic: rank the names each day, demean the ranks so longs and shorts offset, and scale so the gross book is 1 (about 0.5 long, 0.5 short). Because the weights sum to zero and the residuals are already beta-stripped, the resulting book is dollar-neutral *and* approximately market-neutral - the realised book beta in this window comes out at a **mean of +0.006 with a standard deviation of 0.058**, which is as close to zero as a real book gets. On a representative recent day the longs were the residual losers (BHARTIARTL, BEL, BAJAJ-AUTO, SUNPHARMA, ONGC, ETERNAL) and the shorts the residual winners (INDIGO, M&M, RELIANCE, MAXHEALTH, TRENT, ADANIENT). Nothing in that list is a directional call on the market; every name is a bet that its own residual over-stretched and will revert.

## The edge is real - the decile test says so

Equity curves can flatter a strategy, so before plotting any P&L, ask the cleaner question: does the signal actually *sort* forward returns? Each day, bucket every name into deciles by signal - decile 0 is the strongest short, decile 9 the strongest buy - and average each decile's next-day residual return.

{{example: ch13/05_cell19.py | The decile test: forward residual return climbs broadly with the signal | STAT}}

It climbs, broadly, left to right, and the top-minus-bottom spread is **+3.89 basis points per day gross**. Note the shape the notebook is honest about: it is *broadly* monotonic with a noisy extreme decile, not the immaculate staircase a curve-fitted result would draw. That noise is the honest signature of a **real but thin** edge.

The factor model genuinely matters here - this is not a place where extra modelling is wasted polish. Removing only the market gives a gross Sharpe of **0.51**; also removing the same-sector mean residual lifts it to **1.34** - the within-sector co-movement was adding noise to the ranking, and deleting it roughly **doubles the clean edge**. A gross Sharpe a little above 1.3 on a genuinely market-neutral book is exactly the low-volatility profile a desk is built to harvest. So we have a real signal that sorts returns and a real factor improvement that nearly doubles it. Everything to here is the good news.

:::info
The gross result is not a fluke or a leak. The beta is rolling and backward-looking, the fill is next-day (the signal forms at today's close and the book is held over tomorrow), and the signal still sorts forward residuals with a positive top-minus-bottom spread. In this window and universe, factor-neutral residual reversion is a genuine phenomenon. Hold that thought for exactly one section.
:::

## Then you try to harvest it

Here is the entire argument of the chapter on one chart. The gross curve is that clean residual-reversion edge grinding upward with a shallow drawdown. Then we subtract realistic NSE costs.

{{example: ch13/04_cell17.py | Gross is real; net is the problem - costs invert the sign of the strategy | RISK}}

The same signal, on the same days - the only thing that changes is whether you pay to trade it. The gross Sharpe of **1.34** becomes a net Sharpe of **-1.99** under intraday (MIS) costs. Under overnight (CNC) costs, which carry the heavier delivery transaction tax, the curve does not merely roll over - it falls off a cliff. This is not a haircut. In the pairs chapter, costs turned a gross 0.90 into a net 0.77 - a trim, because a plus-or-minus-two-sigma rule trades rarely. Here, costs take a positive 1.34 and hand back a *negative* 1.99. The edge is not reduced; its sign is reversed. A strategy that made money before costs reliably loses money after them.

:::warn
A net Sharpe of -1.99 is not "marginal" or "needs work". It means that if you traded this exact signal and paid realistic costs, you would lose money with the same statistical reliability that the gross book made it. The cost line is not eating into the edge from the side; it is sitting on top of it, larger.
:::

## Turnover is the whole story

Why does a 1.34-Sharpe signal die this badly when a pairs trade survived? One number: the book turns over close to its **entire gross value every single day**, which annualises to roughly **235 times a year**. The signal lives at a one-to-three-day horizon and flips constantly, so almost the whole book is replaced daily.

{{example: ch13/06_cell21.py | Turnover near 1.0x per day; the cost line sits above the edge line | RISK}}

Now do the arithmetic the desk does. The gross edge is about **2.48 bps per day**. The MIS cost drag, at the realised turnover, is about **6.45 bps per day**. You are paying roughly two and a half times your edge to capture it. The red shaded region - costs above edge - is most of the chart.

The one lever that genuinely helps is trading less. Sweeping the lookback `k` from 1 to 20 days slows the signal and drops turnover from 1.34x to 0.31x per day - and the net Sharpe climbs from **-3.83 at k=1 toward -0.16 at k=15**. It climbs, but it **never crosses zero**. Even at the slowest, stalest setting, the residual edge is too thin to clear the toll. You cannot out-clever turnover with a better factor model, and you cannot out-wait it with a longer holding period. The cleanest way to see the verdict is to invert the question and ask how cheap you would have to be.

{{example: ch13/09_cell27.py | Breakeven is 2.66 bps per side; realistic NSE cost is 6.9 (MIS) to 11.9 (CNC) | NSE}}

The breakeven per-side cost is **2.66 bps**. Realistic intraday cost is **6.9 bps** and overnight **11.9 bps** - the cheapest you can plausibly trade is more than **two and a half times** the cost at which the strategy stops making money. That gap, not any modelling flaw, is the precise reason this is a research model and not a strategy you switch on.

:::tip
When a strategy's breakeven cost sits at a fraction of the cost you can actually achieve, stop tuning the signal. No lookback, no factor, no risk model closes a gap of that size. The only things that can are structurally lower cost (institutional execution, internalisation, patient limit fills instead of crossing the spread) and a far larger, more liquid universe to dilute impact - levers that live outside the backtest entirely.
:::

## Where this breaks

This signal is real and it is uneconomic, and those two facts live together. The verdict for this window and universe: a clean, genuinely market-neutral residual-reversion edge with a gross Sharpe a little above 1.3 once the sector is removed, that is **decisively unprofitable after realistic NSE costs** in the form shown.

- **Turnover and costs dominate, full stop.** The edge is a few bps per day; realistic per-side cost is several bps applied to ~1.0x turnover daily, so net inverts the sign from a gross Sharpe of 1.34 to a net of -1.99. The breakeven of 2.66 bps is a fraction of the 6.9-11.9 bps you can actually trade at. No factor-model polish fixes an edge smaller than its own toll.

- **Capacity is tiny and impact is assumed away.** The cost model uses a flat 2 bps impact, but a residual-reversion book demands liquidity exactly when it is scarcest - it buys what just fell and shorts what just ran. Real impact rises with size, so the modest gross edge shrinks as you scale while costs do not. Real capacity needs hundreds of names and hard per-name limits, not 50 large caps.

- **The short side may not be implementable.** Half the book is short. In India that means intraday cash short (no overnight hold, so you forfeit the overnight residual), borrowed stock (limited inventory and a fee that eats the thin edge), or single-stock futures (only F&O names, subject to position-limit bans and roll costs). If you cannot short a name cleanly, the book stops being market-neutral. **Treat the short leg as a research abstraction unless you have a real implementation path.**

- **Factor crowding and decay.** Cross-sectional reversal is one of the most well-known, most-arbitraged signals on the planet; the edge is thin *because* it is crowded. In a deleveraging event it can flip violently to momentum as every desk unwinds the same residual losers at once - the August 2007 quant quake is the textbook case. A backtested gross Sharpe is an upper bound that crowding erodes in real time.

- **Beta-estimation error.** "Market-neutral" is only as good as the rolling beta. A 120-day beta is stale after a regime change, noisy for short-history names, and wrong around corporate actions - so the residual carries leftover market and sector exposure, and the supposedly neutral book quietly takes directional risk it never priced.

- **Survivorship and a point-in-time universe.** This is *today's* index looking back. A genuine cross-sectional book trades the changing membership, including names later dropped or delisted, which lowers the gross signal below what this flattering panel shows.

This is the most scalable idea in the course and, in the same breath, its clearest lesson. The signal is real; the residual genuinely reverts; the book is genuinely neutral. And after the toll of harvesting it at realistic NSE cost, it is a clean research signal with no tradeable edge. It becomes interesting only if you can simultaneously drive per-side cost below ~2.66 bps and implement the short side for real - and until both are true, the honest description is exactly that. The next chapter turns to the risk and sizing machinery that decides whether any of these signals, gross edge and all, survives as a real book. Educational content only, not investment advice.
