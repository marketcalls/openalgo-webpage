Every chart, test and Sharpe ratio in this course is computed on a long/short equity spread that buys one stock and shorts another on the close. That trade is a research abstraction. It exists cleanly in a notebook and almost nowhere else. The long leg is trivial - you buy the stock for delivery and hold it. The short leg is where the abstraction walks out of the notebook and into an Indian trading account, and meets compulsory intraday square-off, borrow scarcity, futures basis, position-limit bans, margin calls, price bands and physical settlement. None of those frictions live inside a close-price series, which means none of them live inside any backtest built on one. This chapter is the lens you carry into every later chapter, and it compresses to a single sentence: a statistical relationship existing is not the same as a tradable edge existing - and almost the entire distance between the two is the short leg.

## The backtest makes three quiet assumptions

When a later chapter buys stock A, shorts stock B on the close, and tracks the spread back to its mean, it is leaning on three assumptions it never states out loud. First, that **you can stay short B for as long as the reversion takes** - days, sometimes weeks. Second, that **both legs fill at the price you measured**. Third, that **carrying the position costs nothing**. Every one of those is false in an Indian account, and each falsehood has a name. You cannot hold a naked cash short overnight. You measured an adjusted close and you fill on the next bar at a raw price with a spread and impact. And the short leg drags a borrow fee, a futures basis, or a roll cost that appears nowhere in the price series. The diagram is the mental model for the whole chapter: a clean research lane on top, and the messy real-market lane the short leg falls into underneath.

## Why we learn on the close and can never trade it

The statistics in this course are built on daily closing prices, and that choice is correct for learning and wrong for executing at the same time. The closing print is the cleanest, most comparable, least-noisy point of the day: one auction-settled number per name, split- and dividend-adjusted so a relationship is *measured* rather than shattered by a corporate action, and aligned across all fifty names onto a single date index - exactly the matrix a cointegration test wants. It is the right learning signal. It is also a price you can never transact at. There is no bid-ask inside an adjusted close, no depth, no impact, and the closing auction may not absorb your size. Your signal fires at the close and your real fill lands on the *next* bar at a raw price. Holding both ideas at once - the close is the right thing to model and the wrong thing to trade - is the discipline the whole module is built on.

:::info
An adjusted close is a measurement instrument, not a tradable quote. Use it to decide *whether* a relationship exists; never assume you could have transacted at it. The series you model and the series you fill on diverge most violently at exactly the corporate actions that adjustment papers over.
:::

Carrying a short in India is not one decision; it is a choice among four instruments, each swapping the clean equity exposure of the backtest for a different vehicle and a different bag of frictions.

Read the rows honestly. An **intraday cash short** lets you sell a stock you do not own, but only intraday - the broker force-squares it near the close, which makes overnight mean-reversion pairs impossible to carry this way and the spread-plus-impact bill payable twice every day. **SLB**, securities lending and borrowing, is the cleanest economic match to a true short: you borrow the stock, sell it, return it later, and pay an annualized borrow fee. But Indian SLB is thin and lumpy - many names have little or no borrow, the fee spikes precisely when everyone wants the same short, and the lender can recall mid-trade. **Stock futures** are the realistic overnight default: a single deeply liquid instrument in large names, naturally short-able, where the price you pay is the basis, a roll cost every expiry, lot-size granularity that stops you sizing to the rupee, margin, and ban risk. A **synthetic short** through options replicates the exposure but adds two option legs, Greeks and assignment risk, and for a linear spread it is rarely worth the complexity. The uncomfortable part: the hedge ratio you so carefully estimated on equity closes is now implemented in a *different instrument*, and that mismatch is tracking error you simply did not have in the backtest.

:::tip
The backtest shorts an equity; you will most often short a future. That substitution is invisible in the curve but real in the account - a future has its own basis, its own roll, and a lot size you cannot trim. Decide the short vehicle for each pair *before* you trust the backtest, because the vehicle, not the signal, sets the cost.
:::

### Which names can you actually short-size?

F and O eligibility is necessary but not sufficient. A name can be in the derivatives segment and still be too thin to take a real position without moving its own price, so the first filter is liquidity, not statistics. Ranking the universe by average daily traded value - close times volume, in rupees crore - gives a blunt proxy for how much size each leg can absorb.

{{example: ch04/01_average_daily_traded_value_adv_over_the_.py | Average daily traded value across the universe | NSE}}

In this data window the richest name, HDFCBANK, turns over about **Rs 2,479 crore a day** against the thinnest, TATACONSUM, at about **Rs 164 crore** - roughly **15x** apart inside a single index, with a median name trading near **Rs 399 crore/day**. Notice what this does and does not buy you. Because the universe is fifty large caps, *zero* names trade under a third of the median - there is no genuinely untradeable name here, so the filter is discipline rather than rescue: it tells you which pairs will cost more in spread, not which are impossible. The red, thin end is still where the short leg quietly breaks - wider spreads, shallower depth, a less active futures contract, scarcer SLB borrow - and a pair that marries a liquid name to a thin one inherits the thin one's execution problems on *both* legs.

## Eligibility, bans, margin and settlement

Even with a liquid name and a futures contract, four exchange mechanics that appear in no price series decide whether you can hold the trade. Stock futures only exist for names in the F and O segment, and that list is actively curated on liquidity criteria - names enter and exit it, and a stock leaving the list kills its futures short outright. Layered on top is the **Market-Wide Position Limit**: when aggregate open interest across *all* participants in a stock crosses 95% of its MWPL, the stock enters a ban period in which only position-reducing trades are allowed. You cannot open or add, and trying draws a penalty. For a market-neutral book this is the worst possible failure mode - your pair can be forced into a one-sided exit precisely when the name is most crowded.

:::warn
An MWPL ban does not arrive when convenient. It triggers when a name is crowded - often the same moment your spread is most stretched and most attractive to add to. If you cannot open or add the short leg, you are holding half a hedge, which is naked directional risk wearing a market-neutral label. Check eligibility and ban status *before every entry*, not once at backtest time.
:::

The same futures short drags in margin, bands and settlement. A short future ties up SPAN plus exposure margin - often a fifth to a third of notional, *per leg* - so a pair is two margined legs marked to market daily, and a gap day calls for more cash. In practice it is margin, not your signal, that caps position size. Price bands and circuits can lock a name so you cannot exit one leg, and half a hedge is again naked risk. And settlement differs by instrument: cash equity settles T+1, but stock futures are *physically settled* if carried into expiry, so holding a short future to the last day means owing delivery of shares. The roll calendar therefore becomes part of the strategy - you must roll or close before every expiry, paying the roll each month.

### The basis you never modelled

When you short a stock through its future you do not trade the cash price at all - you trade the futures price, which sits away from spot by the basis. Under simple cost-of-carry the fair future is `F = S x exp((r - q) x T)`, with `r` the financing rate, `q` the dividend yield and `T` the time to expiry. There is no futures series in this database, so the chart below is a *model* of the fair basis off a real spot, not a market quote - just enough to make the magnitude concrete.

{{example: ch04/02_cost_of_carry_basis_sketch_real_spot_mod.py | Modelled cost-of-carry basis on a real spot | EXEC}}

On the real RELIANCE spot in this window, with an illustrative `r = 6.5%` and `q = 0.6%`, the modelled 30-day fair basis is about **Rs 6.41**, or roughly **48.6 bps of spot**. Small - but not zero, and that is the point. The basis changes every day, it converges to zero by expiry (a mild tailwind for a short when carry is positive), and it appears nowhere in the equity close series your statistics were built on. So the spread you actually trade on futures is not the spread you cointegrated on cash, and that gap is tracking error. Worse, real quotes embed demand, borrow scarcity and roll pressure on top of textbook carry, so the basis can move against you exactly when a name is hard to borrow - noise your cash-based hedge ratio never saw.

## A relationship is not an edge

This is the doctrine of the course in one picture. Thousands of pairs co-move. Far fewer are cointegrated in-sample. Fewer still *stay* cointegrated out-of-sample. Fewer again survive realistic costs on two legs traded twice. And only what is left at the bottom - cheap to trade, short-able, stable, net-positive after every layer above - is an actual edge. Each chapter from here is one more layer of this funnel.

The gates are not equally famous, but they are equally fatal. Gates 1 to 3 decide whether the trade is even *possible* - size, short access, and segment-and-ban status. Gates 4 and 5 decide whether it is *profitable* once the market takes its cut on two legs traded twice, with next-bar fills and the genuine risk that one leg fills and the other does not. Gates 6 to 9 decide whether you can *survive* it - margined capital with a gap-day buffer, monitoring that surfaces a broken leg the moment it happens, a kill switch decided before you need it, and an after-tax view, since futures and cash are taxed differently and the difference comes straight out of net edge.

## Where this breaks

- **The short leg is assumed, not guaranteed.** Every backtest in this course shorts freely. In a real account the short may be un-borrowable, un-shortable overnight, or in an F and O ban exactly when you need it. No short, no market-neutral trade - and that single gate invalidates more "edges" than any statistical test.
- **Instrument mismatch is silent tracking error.** Hedge ratios are estimated on cash closes but implemented in futures, with their own basis, roll and lot-size granularity. The thing you trade is never quite the thing you modelled, and the difference does not show up until real money is on it.
- **Costs and carry are off-page.** Closing prices contain no spread, no impact, no borrow fee, no basis, no roll, no margin cost. They all subtract from the curve; this chapter only *names* them, and a later chapter quantifies the damage.
- **Eligibility and limits move underfoot.** F and O membership, MWPL bans, price bands and margin rates all change over time. A relationship that was tradable last year can be administratively un-tradable today, with no change in the statistics at all.
- **This chapter proves nothing on its own.** It is judgement and diagrams, not a result. The liquidity numbers are illustrative *in this data window* and the basis figure is a model, not a market quote. Carry it as the lens for every later chapter, not as evidence of an edge - and the next chapter starts drawing the family tree of the strategy now that you know which parts of it are research and which parts are real.
