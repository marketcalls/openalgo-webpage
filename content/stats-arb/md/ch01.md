"Arbitrage" is a promise of a free lunch: the same cash flow trading at two prices, bought cheap and sold dear in the same breath, with no risk carried in between. **Statistical** arbitrage keeps the spirit and quietly deletes the guarantee. Nothing forces convergence here - no shared coupon, no settlement, no law. There is only a statistical *relationship* between two assets that has reverted often enough, fast enough, in the past that betting on it again has positive expectancy: after costs, most of the time, on average. Every one of those qualifiers is a place the trade can die. This first chapter is about learning to see them - and to tell a relationship that is real from one your eyes invented.

We build from the floor up: a clean synthetic example you can stare at until the mechanics are obvious, then the same picture on a real NSE pair the data itself nominates - where, fair warning, it does not behave. Then the family tree of how the idea scales, what "market neutral" actually buys you, and an unsentimental look at why most of the historical edge is gone. Every number below is computed from the data in front of us, true *in this data window* and nowhere promised beyond it.

## Trade the relationship, not the price

A single stock price is, to a very good first approximation, a **random walk**: tomorrow is today plus noise, with no centre of gravity pulling it back. Forecasting the *level* is forecasting that noise - a fool's errand dressed up as analysis. So statistical arbitrage refuses to forecast a level at all. It hunts instead for a **combination** of two (or many) prices whose *difference* is **stationary**: it wanders away from a mean and gets dragged back. When two names share the same underlying drivers, each price inherits the random walk, but the shared trend **cancels in the spread**, leaving only a mean-reverting wobble. That wobble - not the price - is the thing you trade.

Before touching a real ticker, let us manufacture the ideal case in numpy so the mechanics are unambiguous. We build a shared stochastic trend - a random walk standing in for "whatever moves the whole sector" - and a mean-reverting wobble (an AR(1) process that always leans back toward zero). Price `X` is the trend plus tiny noise; price `Y` is the trend plus the wobble. By construction they are **cointegrated**: each wanders like a random walk, but `spread = Y - X` is the stationary wobble alone. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test - the workhorse stationarity test of the whole series - puts a number on it.

{{example: ch01/01_cell5.py | The synthetic ideal: a price you cannot forecast, a spread you can | STAT}}

Read the printout, not the picture. The ADF p-value on the price `X` is **0.465** - high, so we *cannot* reject the random-walk null; the level is genuinely unforecastable. The ADF p-value on the spread is **0.000** - it emphatically rejects the random walk and declares the spread stationary. The spread's **half-life of mean reversion is 8.3 days**: that is how long it takes, on average, to close half a deviation, and it is the natural holding period of the trade. The top panel is hopeless to forecast - both lines climb, fall and drift with no anchor. The bottom panel is a different kind of object: it breathes around a fixed mean, and the dotted bands are where a trader would fade it. Everything else in this course is engineering around finding, trusting and trading that bottom panel.

:::key
Statistical arbitrage trades a **stationary relationship**, not a price. You give up forecasting the level - which is a random walk - in exchange for forecasting a spread that has a centre of gravity. The entire edge lives or dies on whether that spread is *actually* stationary out of sample, not just in the sample you measured.
:::

## The same picture on real NSE data

Synthetic data was built to cooperate. Real prices were not. Rather than cherry-pick a pretty pair by hand, we let the data nominate one: among names **in the same sector** - a real economic reason to co-move - with a long overlapping history, which pair's daily returns are the most correlated? High return-correlation is *necessary but nowhere near sufficient* for a tradable spread, but it is the honest place to start looking.

The data's pick is **BAJFINANCE vs BAJAJFINSV** - two NBFC/financial names, return-correlation **0.824** over a **2473-day** window from mid-2016 to mid-2026. Same group, same sector, deeply intertwined businesses. If anything has a clean spread, surely this does.

{{example: ch01/02_cell9.py | The pair the data chose - and the spread that refuses to behave | NSE}}

It does not. The two names clearly travel together - that is the rebased top panel - but the spread in the bottom panel is nothing like the synthetic one. It has fat excursions, the mean visibly drifts across the window, and the verdict is brutal: the **ADF p-value on the spread is 0.199**. That is far above 0.05; we **cannot reject the random walk**. The most return-correlated same-sector pair in this universe does *not* produce a cleanly stationary spread in this window. And the **half-life is 104 days** - not the synthetic's tidy eight. A trade in this spread is a position you would carry for *months*, during which the relationship has ample time to quietly break.

:::warn
High return-correlation and a tradable spread are different altitudes. BAJFINANCE and BAJAJFINSV correlate at **0.824** in daily returns, yet their level spread fails the stationarity test (ADF p = **0.199**) in this window. Two names can move together day-to-day while their *spread* drifts off without ever coming back. Correlation tells you they wiggle in sync; cointegration - covered properly later in the series - tells you whether the spread comes home. Confusing the two is the single most common way a "pairs idea" turns out to be a slow directional bet in disguise.
:::

:::tip
The **half-life** is the trade's natural holding period and a risk gauge in its own right. A half-life of **104 days** means a deviation takes months to revert on average - months of margin, financing, borrow and headline risk on two legs. Short half-lives are tradable and forgiving; long ones ask you to be right *and* patient *and* solvent the whole time. Always read the half-life before you fall in love with a spread.
:::

**Single-name reversion** trades one series against its own moving average or fair level - fragile, because a lone price has no economic anchor forcing it back. **Pairs** add a second name and a single hedge ratio, with an economic story (same sector, shared input costs) supplying the anchor. **Baskets** trade one name against a *weighted basket* of peers - a whole cointegrating vector instead of a single ratio - and are more robust, because an idiosyncratic shock to any one peer washes out. **Cross-sectional / factor-neutral** stat arb ranks the *entire* universe by a reversion signal, goes long the cheap tail and short the rich tail, and hedges out sector and market factors. That last form is what the large quant books actually run; pairs is the teaching case because you can see every moving part.

**Dollar-neutral** means equal rupees long and short - net cash exposure zero. But if the long leg has a higher market beta than the short leg, the book still rises and falls with the index; you carry a residual market beta you did not intend. **Beta-neutral** sizes the short leg by the *ratio of betas* so the market sensitivities cancel, leaving only the spread's own behaviour - the alpha you actually wanted. Let us put numbers on it for our real pair.

{{example: ch01/03_cell15.py | Two neutralities on the same pair: dollar vs beta | RISK}}

In this window, BAJFINANCE has a market beta of **1.41** and BAJAJFINSV **1.33**. Sizing the short at **1.06x** the long notional makes the book beta-neutral. The honest detail: because these two betas are close, the naive dollar-neutral book (+1 / -1) carries a residual net beta of only **+0.09** here, while the beta-neutral book sits at **+0.00** by construction. That small gap is *luck of this particular pair* - when the legs have betas that diverge, dollar-neutral leaks real index risk, and the residual can be large enough to swamp the spread you were trying to trade. And neither neutrality is permanent: betas drift, so a book that is neutral today leaks exposure tomorrow unless it is re-hedged - and every re-hedge pays costs.

:::info
On NSE the **short leg is a research abstraction** in this course unless you have a genuine implementation path. Shorting equity in the cash market beyond intraday is not free or automatic: it requires stock futures, SLB-borrowed stock, or an intraday square-off, and each choice changes costs, margin, borrow availability, taxes and slippage. We use price series to teach the *statistics*; turning the short leg into a real position is a separate engineering problem the later chapters take seriously. This is education, not investment advice.
:::

## Where the edge came from - and why it decayed

Statistical arbitrage was never magic. Historically it was paid for providing a **service** the market needed. When a large buyer pushed one name above its peers, the arbitrageur sold the rich name and bought the cheap one, absorbing the imbalance and earning the gap as prices realigned - liquidity provision dressed as a strategy, the bread-and-butter of the pioneering quant desks of the late 1980s and 1990s. News diffused slowly: it hit the obvious name first and its peers lagged, and trading the lag earned the difference. And structural flows - index rebalances, fund redemptions, tax-driven selling - moved spreads for non-fundamental reasons that someone got paid to revert.

Then the edge decayed and crowded. Faster data and execution collapsed the diffusion lag. Thousands of funds now run the same cointegration screens, so deviations are arbitraged away in minutes, not days. Exchange-traded funds and electronic market makers supply much of the liquidity pairs traders once sold. More capital chasing the same finite, shrinking spread means thinner gross returns and brutal sensitivity to costs. The infamous **August 2007 "quant quake"** - when crowded stat-arb books deleveraged into one another over a few days - is the canonical proof that the edge is shared, correlated and fragile. The chart below makes the point in miniature: the "relationship" you are leaning on is not a constant.

{{example: ch01/04_cell17.py | The relationship is not a constant: rolling correlation drifts | STAT}}

Even our headline pair, supposedly co-moving by nature, has a 1-year rolling return-correlation that **ranges from 0.71 to 0.92** across this window, with a standard deviation of **0.05**. A pair that looks "cointegrated on average" can be materially less coupled for quarters at a time - and those quarters are exactly when an open position bleeds. The single average correlation of 0.824 we started with hid all of that variation. Averages flatter; the timeline tells the truth.

## Honest expectations before you go further

Set the dials correctly now. **This is hard, and it is supposed to be** - the easy spreads are gone, and what remains is thin, fast and contested by better-capitalised, lower-latency players. **Costs dominate**: every pair round trip pays statutory charges, spread and impact on *two legs, on entry and exit*, and a gross edge of a few tens of basis points per trade is routinely *negative* after costs at retail size. **The short leg is rarely free**, as noted above. And a statistical relationship existing is **not** the same as a tradable edge existing - that bright line is the whole reason this module is more than a notebook.

What this series is: **research mechanics first** - cointegration, spread construction, signal design, honest backtesting, multiple-testing discipline - and **execution realism second**, layered on so you can watch how much of every paper edge survives contact with reality. The goal is not to sell you a strategy; it is to make you dangerous enough to tell a real one from a backtest artefact.

## Where this breaks

- **A stationary *past* spread guarantees nothing about the future.** ADF and cointegration describe the sample you ran them on. Relationships break on mergers, regulation, capital-structure changes and regime shifts - often precisely when your position is open. Our chosen pair already failed the test (p = 0.199) in-sample.
- **The hedge ratio is estimated, not given.** The OLS beta of **1.153** is noisy, look-back dependent and drifts; a wrong or stale ratio turns a "market-neutral" spread into a directional bet.
- **Return correlation is the wrong altitude.** It is necessary, not sufficient. Two names can correlate at 0.824 in returns and still have a non-stationary, untradeable level spread. We use it here only to *start* looking; later chapters replace it with a proper cointegration test.
- **Survivorship and point-in-time bias.** The universe is today's index members looking back. Pairs that blew up and were delisted never enter the search, flattering every statistic.
- **Crowding makes the edge correlated across funds.** When deleveraging hits, "market-neutral" books fall together (August 2007). Neutral to the *index* is not neutral to *other arbitrageurs*.
- **Neutrality leaks.** Betas drift, so dollar- or beta-neutral today is not neutral next month without re-hedging - and every re-hedge pays costs.

The bottom line: statistical arbitrage is the discipline of trading a stationary *relationship* you have measured honestly, sized for the risk you chose to keep, in full knowledge that the relationship is estimated, decaying and shared with everyone else running the same screen. The next chapters draw the bright line between a statistical relationship and a tradable edge, and turn the loose word "related" into the precise, testable machinery of cointegration.
