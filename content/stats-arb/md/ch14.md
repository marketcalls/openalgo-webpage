A single pair is a coin with a hidden bias and a habit of breaking. The previous chapters built one - HDFCBANK / KOTAKBANK - and then dismantled it: held out blind, net of realistic costs, its out-of-sample Sharpe was **0.34**, an honest near-flat curve. The beginner's instinct is to hunt for a *better* pair. The desk's instinct is the opposite: stop betting the book on any one relationship, and build a **portfolio** where no single pair can sink you. This chapter takes six same-sector, cointegrated-ish pairs and does the real engineering - neutralise what you do not want, size each to a risk budget, weight by risk rather than gut, and put hard rules underneath. The honest headline up front: six pairs lift the out-of-sample net Sharpe from **0.34 to 0.43** and shrink the worst drawdown from -13.8% to **-10.0%** in this data window. That is a steadier ride on the same marginal edge - not a new edge. Everything that follows is about earning even that.

The premise of bundling them is correlation, so measure it: the **average pairwise correlation of the six gated pair P&Ls is just 0.04.** They are nearly orthogonal - each earns in bursts and sits flat in between, and they do not all earn at once. That near-zero correlation is the engine that makes a book smoother than any line inside it.

:::key
A pairs book is not a collection of bets - it is one bet on a *correlation structure*. At an average pairwise correlation of 0.04 the diversification is real, but it is also the most fragile assumption in the building: correlations that are near zero in calm regimes snap toward one in a crisis, exactly when you need them apart. The whole book leans on a number that is least reliable when it counts.
:::

## Sizing: volatility targeting and the shrunk covariance

The six pairs are wildly unequal in risk. A one-unit M&M / MARUTI spread, with a hedge ratio above 2, is several times more volatile than a one-unit HDFCBANK / KOTAKBANK spread. Drop one raw unit of each into a book and **M&M alone owns the risk** - "diversification" becomes a slogan. Volatility targeting fixes it: scale each pair by a constant `leverage = target_vol / its_own_vol`, estimated on the train window and frozen, so every pair contributes the same standalone risk (8% annualised). The leverages tell the story - M&M / MARUTI gets just **0.14** while TCS / HCLTECH gets **0.41** - the loudest pair is turned down the most.

{{example: ch14/02_cell10.py | Volatility targeting lifts the book Sharpe from 1.10 to 1.34 | RISK}}

Equalising risk lifts the book Sharpe from **1.10 (naive, one raw unit each) to 1.34 (vol-targeted)**, both scaled to the same 10% target so the comparison is purely about balance, not leverage. But vol targeting only fixes each pair's *standalone* risk; it is blind to how pairs co-move. That co-movement - the shared-leg correlations - lives in the covariance matrix, and a covariance matrix is *estimated*, which is where the trouble starts.

{{example: ch14/03_cell12.py | Ledoit-Wolf shrinks the 63-day condition number from 6.9 to 2.3 | STAT}}

With six assets and a short window, the sample covariance is noisy and ill-conditioned, and an optimiser fed such a matrix produces garbage weights - it amplifies noise in the near-singular directions. The diagnostic is the **condition number** (ratio of largest to smallest eigenvalue; higher means closer to singular). On the realistic 63-day windows a desk actually re-estimates on, the **sample matrix has a median condition number of 6.9 (max 16.7)**; **Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage cuts that to 2.3 (max 8.2)** - roughly a 3x improvement in stability, trading a little bias for a lot of conditioning. The heatmaps make the shared legs visible: the two IT pairs sit at a strong negative correlation through TCS, the metals pairs through TATASTEEL.

:::warn
Ledoit-Wolf makes the matrix better *conditioned*, not *correct*. It buys you stability, not foresight. The correlations that drive every weight below - especially the shared-leg ones - drift over time, so a covariance optimised on 2019-2023 is a snapshot, not a law. Shrinkage stops the optimiser from exploding on noise; it does not stop the world from changing.
:::

## Weighting by risk, not by gut

With a trustworthy covariance you can weight pairs by risk. Two classic long-only schemes, weights summing to one, under a hard **concentration cap of 35%** so no pair can dominate: **minimum variance** (put weight where it lowers total book variance most - it loves the negatively-correlated IT pair that hedges its sibling) and **risk parity / equal risk contribution (ERC)** (every pair supplies the same share of book volatility). Minimum variance gives the lowest model vol by construction (2.55% vs 2.77% for equal weight); ERC sits a touch higher at 2.60%, buying balance with a sliver of variance. The reason ERC matters is the gap between equal *capital* and equal *risk*.

{{example: ch14/05_cell16.py | Equal capital is not equal risk - one pair quietly carries 26% | RISK}}

Under naive equal weighting the risk contributions are **lopsided: HDFCBANK / KOTAKBANK supplies 26.0% of book volatility and M&M / MARUTI 23.7%, while the two IT pairs supply only 9.7% and 8.6%.** Two pairs out of six carry half the risk. After vol targeting the diagonals are roughly equal, so this imbalance comes almost entirely from the **correlations** - the IT pairs hedge each other and contribute little net risk, while the standalone bank and auto pairs contribute a lot. Risk parity flattens every contribution to exactly **16.7% (1/6)**, and the concentration cap is the backstop underneath: no pair exceeds 35% of capital, so one broken pair can never take the book down alone.

## Rupee-neutral is not beta-neutral

Now the exposures. A long/short book has two size numbers: **gross exposure** (sum of the absolute legs - what you actually trade, pay costs on, and margin against) and **net exposure** (the signed sum - your directional tilt). Gross *breathes* with how many pairs are firing (mean **1.08x**, peaking at **2.80x**) while net hugs zero (mean **+0.017x**), because every pair is internally long-short. That looks market-neutral. It is not. "Equal money long and short" is **rupee-neutral**; "equal market sensitivity long and short" is **beta-neutral**, and the two diverge the moment two legs have different betas to NIFTY. The pair beta is `beta_A - hedge_ratio x beta_B`, so a large hedge ratio amplifies any mismatch.

M&M / MARUTI is the cautionary tale: its pair beta to NIFTY is **-1.11**, while every other pair sits between -0.17 and +0.27. Summed across active pairs, the **book's residual beta has a mean of just +0.008 but a standard deviation of 0.095 and peaks at |0.28|** - a "market-neutral" book quietly carrying a quarter-unit of market risk whenever the high-hedge-ratio pair is on. The fix is a **NIFTY overlay**: short the lagged residual beta in the index each day, and the realised rolling 120-day beta collapses toward zero. Sector neutrality is cleaner by construction - every pair lives inside one sector, so net tilts stay tiny against gross (IT: 0.464 gross, 0.019 net; Metals: 0.372 gross, -0.003 net) - though the metals pairs sharing TATASTEEL are where a small net tilt can leak in.

Assemble it all - vol-targeted returns, risk-parity weighted, scaled to 10% portfolio vol, charged realistic delivery (CNC) costs of about **47 bps per leg-notional** on every position change - and the full-sample net Sharpe is **0.94**. Out of sample it is **0.43**, total **+9.0%**, max drawdown **-10.0%**: steadier than the single pair, but the out-of-sample haircut is the same brutal fact it always was.

:::info
Rupee-neutral is the exposure you can see; beta-neutral is the one that hurts you. The overlay zeroes the *in-sample* residual beta - out of sample the betas drift and the book carries market risk again, worst from the high-hedge-ratio pair, exactly when the market moves most. You are not removing the risk; you are removing the part of it you could measure on history.
:::

## The stop-loss-on-a-spread dilemma

On a single instrument a stop-loss is plain risk control. On a mean-reverting spread it is a paradox: a spread stretched to `|z| > 3` is, *by the strategy's own logic*, more likely to revert - so the stop fires precisely when expected forward return is highest. Cut it and you bank a loss right before the snap-back; hold it and a truly de-cointegrated pair runs against you without limit. This is not a tuning problem; it is structural.

{{example: ch14/10_find_the_most_painful_real_stop_in_the_b.py | The stop fires at the extreme, then the spread comes home | RISK}}

The most painful real instance in this book (excluding the COVID crash, a genuine break) is concrete: HDFCBANK / KOTAKBANK, short the spread, **entered 2020-10-07, stopped out two days later on 2020-10-09 for -6.4%.** Had you held, the spread reverted to the mean by **2021-01-05 for +21.4%.** The stop did not save you - it **cost 27.9% of the missed reversion**, cutting at the worst possible moment.

The same tension runs through **drawdown control**. A causal throttle - cut book exposure to half once it is 5% below its high-water mark, restore above -2% - clips the tail from **-10.0% to -8.6%**, but it is not free: Sharpe falls from **0.94 to 0.76** and total return from **+89% to +60%**. It cannot prevent the first leg of a drawdown and it can whipsaw. You are buying a smaller tail with a lower Sharpe.

:::key
No stop threshold and no drawdown throttle resolves the dilemma - they only choose which error to make. The stop that costs you a 21.4% reversion is the same stop that caps an unbounded loss on a pair that has genuinely de-cointegrated. Tuning the threshold on history just overfits which of the two mistakes you happened to make last time. Risk control here is the deliberate purchase of one regret to avoid a worse one.
:::

## Retiring a broken pair before it bleeds you

The hardest risk rule in stat-arb is also the most important: **drop a pair when its cointegration dies.** A spread only reverts while the relationship holds; once it breaks, the z-score machine cheerfully keeps generating entries into a trend that never comes home. We monitor each pair with a rolling 252-day Engle-Granger p-value and apply hysteresis to avoid flip-flopping - **retire** a pair after its p-value sits above 0.10 for three consecutive readings, **re-admit** only after it drops below 0.05 and holds.

{{example: ch14/12_cell31.py | A rolling cointegration screen retires pairs - median tradeable: 0 of 6 | STAT}}

The result is humbling, and it is the truest number in the chapter. Each pair passes the rolling screen only a sliver of the time - TCS / HCLTECH the most at **13%**, INFY / TCS the least at **2%** - and the **median number of pairs passing the screen is 0 of 6.** A disciplined book, obeying its own retire rule, sits empty more often than not. Read plainly: these relationships are cointegrated *occasionally*, not *durably*, and a static six-pair book that ignores the screen is mostly running on faith - trading regimes that have already ended because the entry logic never asked whether the tether still held.

:::warn
Retiring is late by construction. A 252-day rolling p-value confirms a break only after enough damage accumulates to move a year-long window - by the time the rule retires a pair, most of the loss is already taken, and re-admission can whipsaw you back into a still-broken relationship. Detection lags the event it detects. This is the best honest tool available and it is still a rear-view mirror.
:::

## Where this breaks

A portfolio layer makes a stat-arb book *survivable*; it does not make a weak edge strong, and every tool above leans on estimates that are themselves fragile.

- **The covariance is estimated, and it moves.** Ledoit-Wolf cut the 63-day condition number from 6.9 to 2.3 - better conditioned, not correct. The shared-leg correlations that drive the weights drift, so a weight optimised on 2019-2023 can be wrong out of sample. Shrinkage buys stability, not foresight.
- **Neutralisation is backward-looking.** The leg betas, hedge ratios and the NIFTY overlay are all fitted on history. The residual beta we "hedged to zero" is zero only in-sample; out of sample the book carries beta again, and the high-hedge-ratio M&M / MARUTI pair (beta -1.11) leaks the most exactly when it hurts.
- **Stops and throttles are double-edged.** The stop that cost a 21.4% reversion is the same one that caps an unbounded loss on a truly broken pair. No threshold resolves the trade-off; tuning it on history overfits last time's mistake.
- **Retiring lags.** A rolling cointegration screen confirms a break only after a 252-day window moves - the loss is largely taken before the rule fires, and re-admission can whipsaw. By that screen the median tradeable count was 0 of 6.
- **Six survivors, weighted in hindsight.** This book is built from today's index names that happened to test cointegrated on the train window - the same survivorship bias the single-pair teardown quantified, now compounded across six pairs, with the delisted and merged pairs invisible.

The honest summary: this is research-grade portfolio construction on a research abstraction. It is the *right* way to turn signals into a book, and it materially improves the ride - out-of-sample net Sharpe from 0.34 to 0.43, worst drawdown from -13.8% to -10.0%. But it sits on top of the same marginal, cost-laden, drifting edge, and it inherits every caveat of the equity-short problem, which on Indian markets may require stock futures, borrowed stock, or intraday square-off - each changing costs, margin, borrow availability and risk. A statistical relationship existing is not the same as a tradable edge existing. The portfolio layer is what keeps you in the game long enough to find out which one you have.
