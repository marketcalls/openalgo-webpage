The last chapter ended on a verdict: this pair is cointegrated, that one is not. That verdict is a single bit of information, and it is the least useful thing the machinery produces. The interesting questions all live downstream of it. *How many* units of the second stock hedge one unit of the first - and is that number even well-defined? What does the spread you would actually trade look like, and how do you prove it reverts rather than just hope it does? Once it reverts, *how long* does it take to come home, because that number is your holding period and your stop-loss horizon at once? This chapter opens the Engle-Granger engine and walks every moving part on one real cointegrated NSE pair, computing each number from the data and telling you, at each step, exactly where the number is lying to you.

## The two-step machine, and a pair the data chooses

Engle-Granger is two steps and nothing more. Step one is an ordinary regression of one log price on the other; its slope is the hedge ratio and its residual is the spread. Step two tests that residual for stationarity. If the residual is stationary, the two prices are cointegrated and the residual is the asset you trade. Hold this picture, because every code cell below is one box in it.

Fix one estimation window - **2019-01-01 to 2023-12-29, 1,239 trading days** - hold it for the entire chapter so every number is comparable, and run the Engle-Granger `coint` test on all ten same-sector pairs among the five Nifty bank names. Holding the window fixed matters: cointegration is a statement about a particular stretch of data, and quietly re-selecting the window per pair is one of the easiest ways to fool yourself. In this window exactly **one of the ten** clears the 5% bar: **HDFCBANK against KOTAKBANK**, `coint` p = **0.0306** (t = -3.521 against a 5% MacKinnon critical value of -3.341). The runner-up, HDFCBANK against SBIN, sat at p = 0.096 - on the wrong side of the line, and an expert does not negotiate the threshold after seeing the number. That single survivor out of ten is your first warning about selection bias, which the next chapter attacks; for now we call HDFCBANK our `A`, KOTAKBANK our `B`. Everything works in **log prices**, because a slope on logs is a unit-free return-for-return relationship - exactly what cointegration theory and the half-life assume.

:::info

Keep it in your pocket - in a moment a naive test on the same spread will hand you a far prettier p-value, and you need to know which one to believe.
:::

## Three hedge ratios, and why OLS lies

Step one's slope is the hedge ratio: how many units of `B` neutralise one unit of `A`. The obvious way to get it is `sm.OLS(A, B)`. The trap is that OLS treats the right-hand variable as measured without error and minimises only the *vertical* distances to the line. But both stocks are noisy, so the answer changes depending on which one you put on the right.

Regress `A` on `B` and you get b = **1.0545**. Regress `B` on `A` and you get b = **0.7859**, which flipped back into A-on-B terms is **1.2724**. If the hedge ratio were a clean physical constant, those two would be exact reciprocals and agree. They do not: they straddle the truth, **20.7%** of the OLS value apart. The reason is exact and worth memorising - the product of the two slopes equals the regression R-squared: 1.0545 x 0.7859 = **0.8288 = R-squared**. Since R-squared is below one, the two OLS slopes can never be reciprocal, and each is pulled toward zero. That is errors-in-variables, or attenuation, bias, and it is not a rounding artefact - it is a 21% disagreement about your position size.

The geometry tells you exactly when this matters. OLS `A~B` minimises the *vertical* scatter, implicitly assuming all the noise lives in `A` and `B` is pristine; OLS `B~A` assumes the opposite. Both legs are noisy stocks, so neither axis is the clean one, and the gap between the two slopes is governed entirely by R-squared - at R-squared = 1 the lines coincide, and as R-squared falls they fan apart until the choice of `x` dominates the answer. Our tight 0.83 makes the 21% gap the *mild* version; on a noisier pair the same mechanism can hand you two hedge ratios that differ by half, at which point your position is a directional bet wearing a spread's clothing.

## Total least squares: the honest middle line

The fix is to stop pretending either axis is clean. Total least squares - orthogonal distance regression, available directly as `scipy.odr` - minimises the *perpendicular* distance to the line, treating `A` and `B` symmetrically. The result does not depend on which series you called `x`, and it sits, by construction, between the two OLS slopes: **1.0545 <= 1.1751 <= 1.2724**. The TLS hedge ratio of **1.1751** is **11.4% higher** than the OLS `A~B` value we will actually use.

So which do we trade on? Engle-Granger is *defined* on the OLS residual, and with R-squared this high the OLS and TLS spreads behave almost identically, so the rest of this chapter builds the spread from **OLS `A~B`** to match the textbook two-step. The point of TLS is not that it is magic. It is that the hedge ratio is an *estimate* carrying a direction-of-regression ambiguity, and an expert states which convention they used instead of pretending the number fell from the sky.

:::key
There is no single "correct" hedge ratio for two noisy series - there is a range, and you pick a convention. OLS `A~B` gives 1.0545, the flip gives 1.2724, TLS splits them at 1.1751. On a tight pair the choice barely matters; on a loose one it is the difference between a hedged spread and a directional bet wearing a spread's clothing.
:::

## Building the spread, and the formal test that it reverts

With the convention fixed, the spread is just the Step-1 residual: `s = A - (alpha + beta.B)`. Subtracting the fitted `beta.B` strips out the shared sector and market trend, leaving a residual that - if the pair really is cointegrated - oscillates around a constant. OLS forces the mean to essentially zero (we measure **-0.00000**) with a standard deviation of **0.06982** in log units, and the series **crosses its own mean 59 times** in 1,239 days. Frequent mean-crossing is the visual fingerprint of reversion: a random walk would wander off and cross rarely.

{{example: ch07/02_cell19.py | The spread: residual of HDFCBANK on KOTAKBANK, with sd bands | STAT}}

Visual oscillation is suggestive, not proof. The formal test is the Augmented Dickey-Fuller test, which asks whether the spread has a unit root - a random walk - against the alternative that it is stationary. Here is the expert nuance that separates a careful researcher from an optimist. Run `adfuller` straight on the OLS residual and you get a t-statistic of -3.519 and p = **0.0075**: a strong, confident rejection of the unit root. But that number is *flattering*, because the hedge ratio was fitted on the very data being tested - chosen, in effect, to make the residual as stationary as possible. The honest number is the Engle-Granger `coint` p of **0.0306** from the scan, which uses the correct MacKinnon critical values for an estimated cointegrating vector. Both clear 5%, so the verdict holds - but notice the gap. The naive ADF p (0.0075) is four times stronger than the honest p (0.0306) on the identical spread. Report the conservative one, always.

:::warn
The naive ADF p-value on a fitted residual is biased toward "stationary" by construction - you optimised the residual, then tested it. On a marginal pair this is exactly how a non-reverting spread gets waved through. When you have an estimated hedge ratio, the Engle-Granger `coint` p is the number that counts; the residual ADF is a diagnostic, not a verdict.
:::

The spread's distribution is roughly bell-shaped and tight - skew **-0.02**, excess kurtosis **-0.52** - which is what lets the standard-deviation bands later become entry and exit thresholds. Near-zero skew means it is not lopsided toward one stock; the slightly negative excess kurtosis means thinner tails than a normal, so the one- and two-sd bands actually mean what they say. A later chapter turns those exact lines into a rule - short the spread above the upper band, long below the lower one - and that rule only makes sense on a distribution this symmetric and tight. "Mean-reverting" should be something you can see, and here you can.

## How fast it comes home: the OU half-life

Stationarity tells you the spread *will* return to its mean. It says nothing about *when*, and "when" is what you trade. Model the spread as an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process - the continuous-time spring that pulls a deviation back toward the mean - whose discrete version is an AR(1). Regress the daily change `ds` on the lagged level `s(t-1)`; the slope is `phi`, the pull-back speed is `kappa = -phi`, and the **half-life = ln(2) / kappa** in trading days: the time to close half of any gap.

On our spread the AR(1) fit gives `phi` = **-0.0237**, so `kappa` = **0.0237**, strongly significant (t = -3.83, p = 0.0001). Read `kappa` as a decay rate: roughly **2.4% of any gap closes per trading day**, compounding to half the gap gone in **ln(2) / 0.0237 = 29.3 trading days** - about **1.4 months**, just **2.4%** of the sample. The significance matters as much as the estimate: a `kappa` indistinguishable from zero is no reversion at all, only noise leaning the right way - the t of -3.83 is what lets us call this reversion real rather than an artefact of the fit.

Read the half-life as a holding period: a typical deviation is half-gone in roughly six weeks, so a position is held days-to-weeks, not minutes and not years. That one number sets your trade horizon and your patience before you call a position broken rather than merely slow. A half-life that is a large fraction of the sample is a warning, not a signal - you have not seen enough complete cycles to trust the reversion is stable rather than a single lucky round trip.

{{example: ch07/04_cell28.py | The AR(1) reversion: a negative slope is mean reversion | STAT}}

The downward regression line *is* the mean reversion. Above-mean spreads tend to fall next; below-mean spreads tend to rise. A flat line would mean a random walk; an upward line would mean an explosive series running away from its mean.

## The half-life mirage

Here is the trap that catches careful people. The half-life formula always returns *a* number - even when the spread does not revert at all. A finite, healthy-looking half-life is not evidence of anything on its own. The gates must be applied **in order**: first confirm the spread is stationary with the ADF test, *then* read the half-life. Skip the first gate and the second lies to you.

To prove it, contrast the real spread against three synthetic controls of the same length. A pure random walk reports `kappa` = 0.0025 and a half-life of **280 days** - but its ADF p is 0.157, so it is not stationary and that half-life is meaningless. The explosive series (rho > 1) gives `kappa` = -0.0100, negative, so its half-life is simply undefined. And the dangerous one, the **near-unit-root drift**: `kappa` = 0.0108, positive, with a half-life of **64 days** that looks perfectly tradeable - and its ADF p is **0.106**, so it fails the stationarity gate outright. That 64-day half-life is a mirage. If you had trusted the half-life without testing stationarity first, you would have sized a position into a series that never comes home.

{{example: ch07/05_cell32.py | Real spread vs three controls: only one keeps coming home | STAT}}

Standardised side by side, only the real HDFCBANK-KOTAKBANK spread keeps returning to its centre; the random walk and the near-unit-root drift wander off and stay there. The near-unit-root case is the one you meet in the wild - a pair that is *almost* cointegrated, drifting slowly enough that the half-life returns a plausible 64 days while the relationship is quietly broken. The ADF test is the only thing between you and that trap, and only if you run it *before* the half-life seduces you.

:::warn
A positive `kappa` and a short half-life are necessary, not sufficient. The near-unit-root control here had both - `kappa` 0.0108, half-life 64 days - and still failed stationarity at ADF p = 0.106. The order of operations is non-negotiable: stationarity gate first, speed gate second. A half-life read off a non-stationary series is a number with no meaning attached.
:::

And there is one more crack even in the genuine pair. We estimated a *single* beta over the whole window, but a trailing 120-day OLS hedge ratio swings from **-0.312 to 1.297** - a range of **258% of its own mean** of 0.624. The "constant" cointegrating vector is anything but, which is the entire motivation for the dynamic Kalman hedge ratio a later chapter builds: a fixed-beta spread fitted in-sample drifts out of its bands out-of-sample for reasons that have nothing to do with reversion.

## Where this breaks

Every box in the Engle-Granger diagram is an assumption that can fail. In rough order of how often it kills a real pair:

- **Hedge-ratio instability is the big one.** The rolling beta wandered across a 258%-of-mean range. Engle-Granger assumes *one* cointegrating vector for all time; when beta drifts, the fixed-beta spread is mis-hedged, leaks directional risk, and breaches its bands for reasons unrelated to reversion. Rolling-OLS or Kalman can chase the drift, but every added degree of freedom is a fresh chance to overfit.
- **The x-versus-y ambiguity is real money.** OLS `A~B` gave 1.0545, the flip gave 1.2724 - a 20.7% disagreement on a *tight* pair. On a looser pair it is far worse, and your P&L then depends on a modelling convention rather than on the market. TLS is more honest but still assumes a single constant ratio.
- **In-sample cointegration is an overfit waiting to happen.** We scanned ten bank pairs and reported the one that passed at p = 0.0306. That is the best of ten tests dressed up as a single result - classic selection bias. The next chapter puts numbers on how many spurious "cointegrated" pairs pure noise manufactures once you scan a universe.
- **The half-life is fragile out-of-sample.** The 29.3-day figure is an in-sample point estimate with wide error bars. A value that looks tradeable in this window can lengthen, vanish, or go negative in the next - and a spread whose half-life blows up is a position that simply does not come back. Re-estimate out-of-sample, every time.
- **ADF on a fitted residual flatters you.** The naive ADF p (0.0075) was four times stronger than the honest `coint` p (0.0306) on the identical spread, precisely because the residual was optimised. Report the conservative number.
- **Stationary in logs is not tradeable in rupees.** This entire chapter lives in adjusted log prices. Costs, the bid-ask crossed on *two* legs, borrow for the short, and the gap between adjusted and tradable prices are all still ahead of us. Treat the short leg as a **research abstraction** until you have a real implementation path - in Indian markets that may mean stock futures, borrowed stock, or intraday square-off, and each choice changes costs, margin, borrow availability, taxes, slippage and risk. A statistical relationship existing is not the same as a tradable edge existing.
