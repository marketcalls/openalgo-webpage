A stock falls for three weeks, and somewhere a trader says the words that have funded more blown accounts than any other: *"it's oversold, it has to revert."* Stop there. Before you can claim a series *reverts*, you have to prove it has something to revert *to* — a fixed mean and a stable variance that the next observation is tethered to. Most price series have neither. The whole edifice of statistical arbitrage rests on one property that decides whether "it will revert" is a tradeable statement or a sentence you are projecting onto a chart: **stationarity**. This chapter shows, on real NSE data, that a single stock price almost never has it — and that its returns almost always do. That asymmetry is not a curiosity. It is the reason stat-arb never trades a price, only a spread it has engineered to be stationary.

## The one idea: I(1) prices, I(0) returns

A series is **integrated of order `d`** — written `I(d)` — if you have to difference it `d` times before it becomes stationary. Two orders matter for everything that follows.

An `I(1)` series — **a price** — needs differencing *once*. The level itself carries a **unit root**: it wanders with no mean to return to, and its variance grows without bound as time passes. Ask "where should this be?" and there is no honest answer. An `I(0)` series — **a return** — is *already* stationary: a fixed long-run mean (near zero for daily log returns), a finite and roughly stable variance, and an autocorrelation that dies almost immediately. Deviations from its mean are temporary by construction. The single operation that turns the first into the second is the log difference, `r_t = log(P_t) − log(P_{t-1})`. That is the entire reason returns, not prices, are the well-behaved object — and the reason a tradeable mean-reverting object in this series is never a price, but a constructed spread forced to be `I(0)`.

## A random walk and a mean-reverter, built from scratch

Before touching market data, build both objects yourself so there is no ambiguity about what "stationary" looks like. A **random walk** is `x_t = x_{t-1} + e_t`: each shock is added into the level *forever*. This is the textbook `I(1)` process and the canonical model of an efficient price. A **stationary AR(1)** is `y_t = phi * y_{t-1} + e_t` with `|phi| < 1`: a shock decays geometrically at rate `phi`, so the series is pulled back toward zero. That is `I(0)` and the canonical model of a mean-reverting spread.

{{example: ch05/01_cell5.py | Random walk versus a stationary AR(1), built in numpy | STAT}}

With a seeded generator the numbers are exact. Over 1,500 steps the random walk drifts to a level mean of −31.88 with a standard deviation of 24.16 — and *both numbers are meaningless*, because they depend entirely on where the window happens to end. The AR(1) with `phi = 0.85` sits at a mean of −0.40 with std 1.74: a real, stable centre of gravity at zero. The single parameter `phi` is the whole story, and the cruel detail is the boundary: at `phi = 1` the AR(1) *becomes* the random walk. Mean-reversion and non-stationarity are separated by a razor's edge, which is exactly why testing for the difference is hard — a theme that comes back to bite us at the end.

The clearest fingerprint is what happens to the **variance over time**. Simulate many independent paths and watch the cross-sectional spread at each step. For the random walk, theory says `Var(x_t) = t * sigma^2` — it grows linearly *forever*; in a 400-path run the cross-sectional variance reaches 391.9 by step 399 and is still climbing, the cloud fanning out with no anchor. For the AR(1) the variance converges to `sigma^2 / (1 − phi^2) = 3.60`, and the simulation settles at 3.56 — the paths stay inside a band. **That band is the thing mean reversion trades.** A random walk has no band, so there is nothing to trade.

:::key
"Mean-reverting" is a property of the *process*, not a hope you attach to a chart. The test is whether the variance is bounded. If the spread of possible future values keeps widening with the horizon, there is no fair value, no anchor, and no edge — only a story.
:::

## The same split in a real NSE stock

Now the claim that matters: a real stock price behaves like the random walk, and its log returns behave like the stationary process. Take one liquid name over this data window — RELIANCE, 2,477 daily closes from June 2016 to June 2026 — and plot the level against its log returns.

{{example: ch05/03_cell9.py | RELIANCE: an I(1) price sitting over a stationary I(0) return | NSE}}

The price chart has no horizontal line you could honestly draw as "fair value" — wherever it is, it could be twice or half that in a few years. Split the sample in half and the log-price mean shifts from 6.272 to 7.156: the centre of the level *moves*, which is precisely what non-stationarity looks like. The returns chart is the opposite. Its mean is pinned near zero in both halves (+0.00124 then +0.00023) and its standard deviation is stable (0.0195 then 0.0141). One caveat we will not bury: returns are stationary in *mean*, but their variance clusters — calm stretches and violent ones. That is *conditional* heteroskedasticity, not non-stationarity; the long-run variance is still finite and stable. It matters enormously for risk sizing, and we flag it now so it is not a surprise later.

## The ADF test, read the way it is meant to be read

Eyeballing charts is not evidence. The **Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF)** test is the workhorse. It fits `delta y_t = alpha + beta*t + gamma * y_{t-1} + (lagged differences) + e_t` and tests `H0: gamma = 0` (a unit root — non-stationary) against `H1: gamma < 0` (mean-reverting). Two specification choices a professional never leaves on autopilot. First, **lag length**: the lagged-difference terms soak up short-run autocorrelation so the statistic is valid; too few biases the test, too many wastes power. Use `autolag='AIC'` to let an information criterion choose. Second, **regression specification**: `'c'` includes a constant (reverts to a non-zero level), `'ct'` adds a linear trend, `'n'` has neither. And the part most people skip entirely — the test is one-sided and the statistic is *negative*. You do not compare a p-value to 0.05 and stop; you compare the **statistic to the critical values**. More negative is stronger evidence against the unit root.

{{example: ch05/05_adf_statistic_vs_critical_values_as_a_ch.py | ADF statistic versus its critical values | NSE}}

Read it out loud. For RELIANCE's **log price**, the statistic is −2.234, sitting *above* (less negative than) even the 10% critical value of −2.567 — we **fail to reject** the unit root. For its **log returns** the statistic is −15.241, far below the 1% critical value of −3.433 — we **reject** decisively. That is the `I(1)`-price / `I(0)`-return result, on real data, with the test read correctly. The p-values (0.194 versus 0.000) tell the same story, but the statistic-versus-critical-value framing is the one that survives contact with a borderline series.

Now the discipline most people skip: does the verdict survive different specifications? Re-running the price test across the grid, `'c'` with AIC gives −2.234, `'ct'` gives −1.997 (and its critical value tightens to −3.412), and the no-constant `'n'` spec gives a *positive* +1.825. Every one says "do not reject" — the non-stationary verdict for this price is robust here. But notice how much the statistic moved, and that the `'n'` spec flipped its sign entirely. That robustness is the **exception**. For the borderline, slow-reverting spreads stat-arb actually finds, the choice of spec routinely flips the answer, and it is tempting to pick the one that tells you what you want.

:::warn
Never choose the regression specification or lag length *after* seeing the result. With a borderline series you can almost always find a `'c'`/`'ct'`/`'n'` combination that crosses the 5% line, and the temptation to keep the one that "works" is how stat-arb research quietly fits noise. Fix the spec on theory before you run the test, and report what you fixed.
:::

The ADF has a notorious weakness — it under-rejects — so it is worth running a test whose null is the mirror image. The **KPSS** test takes `H0: stationary`; rejecting it is evidence *for* a unit root. For RELIANCE's log price the KPSS statistic is 7.216, blowing past the 1% critical value of 0.739 — it **rejects** stationarity. For the returns it is 0.317, below the 5% value of 0.463 — it **fails to reject**. Both tests now agree from opposite ends, which is the strongest possible verdict.

## One number on the line: the Hurst exponent

ADF and KPSS hand you a yes/no. The **Hurst exponent `H`** gives a *position on a line* and a feel for the strength of the behaviour. Estimate it from how the typical size of moves scales with the horizon: measure the root-mean-square change over lag `k` and fit `rms(k) ~ k^H` on a log-log scale. Then `H ~ 0.5` means the series diffuses like a random walk; `H < 0.5` means anti-persistent and mean-reverting (the shape of a tradeable spread); `H > 0.5` means persistent and trending. Validate the estimator on series whose answer we already know: the synthetic random walk returns 0.502, the AR(1) returns 0.049, white noise −0.004, and a pure drift-plus-trend 0.992. The estimator works.

{{example: ch05/06_hurst_across_real_nse_log_price_series_w.py | Hurst exponent across NSE log prices | NSE}}

Run it across real NSE log prices and the result is unambiguous: in this window the Hurst exponents land in a tight band of **[0.46, 0.56] with a mean of exactly 0.50**. No single stock price sits convincingly below 0.5. Not one name is a mean-reverter. This is the same fact the autocorrelation function tells: RELIANCE's log *price* has a lag-1 autocorrelation of 1.000 and an ACF that decays agonisingly slowly (today looks exactly like yesterday, because shocks never leave), while its log *returns* have a lag-1 autocorrelation of −0.009 — essentially zero memory from the very first lag. There is nothing in the daily return series to exploit directly, and nothing in the price to revert against.

:::info
Read the Hurst exponent as a continuous diagnostic, not a verdict. ADF and KPSS answer "unit root: yes or no"; Hurst tells you *where on the line* a series sits and roughly how strong the behaviour is. A spread worth trading should land clearly below 0.5 — and the further below, the faster it covers less ground than a random walk would, which is the same as saying it reverts harder.
:::

## The whole cross-section agrees

One stock could be a fluke. Sweep 24 liquid NSE names, run ADF (`regression='c'`, `autolag='AIC'`) on each name's log price and its log returns, and collect every verdict in one grid.

{{example: ch05/08_heatmap_of_adf_p_values_low_green_statio.py | ADF p-values across the NSE cross-section: prices red, returns green | NSE}}

The split is near-total. **Every single price fails to reject the unit root** — 100% of names, with p-values spread from 0.13 (HINDUNILVR, the most borderline) up to 0.95 (BHARTIARTL, M&M, SUNPHARMA). **Every single return rejects it** — 100% of names, with p-values that all round to 0.000. KPSS, with its opposite null, mirrors the verdict exactly: 100% of names reject stationarity on the price, and only 4% — a single name — reject it on returns. There is no ambiguity in this window. Prices are `I(1)`; returns are `I(0)`; and that is why you cannot mean-revert a single stock. The mean-reverting object has to be *manufactured* — a spread between two co-moving series, engineered so the spread itself is `I(0)` even though neither leg is. That construction is the subject of the chapters that follow.

## Where this breaks

Stationarity testing feels like a measurement and is anything but. The honest caveats each come back to bite real research.

**ADF has low power near a unit root**, and this is not a footnote — it is the single most important caveat in applied cointegration work. A genuinely stationary AR(1) with `phi` close to 1 (slow mean reversion) is one the ADF test routinely *cannot* distinguish from a random walk in a few thousand observations. Make it concrete: simulate series that are stationary *by construction* and ask how often ADF correctly rejects.

{{example: ch05/10_demonstration_adf_s_low_power_against_a_.py | ADF power collapses as phi approaches 1 | STAT}}

At `phi = 0.80` the test correctly rejects ~100% of the time. At `phi = 0.99` — still perfectly stationary — it rejects only ~61%. Roughly two in five times it declares a unit root that is not there. The brutal consequence: *"ADF didn't reject"* is **not** proof of a unit root. It is very often just weak data. And the spreads stat-arb actually finds sit exactly in this dangerous region — slow mean-reverters with `phi` near 1, where the tests are least reliable and where half-lives are long enough that costs eat the edge before reversion arrives.

The other failure modes compound it. **Specification sensitivity:** `'c'` versus `'ct'` versus `'n'`, and the lag length, can flip a borderline verdict — so decide the spec *before* you look, never after. **Structural breaks:** every test here assumes one fixed regime. A pair that was stationary for three years and broke after a merger or an index reconstitution will still test "stationary" on the pooled sample while being untradeable now; stationarity over the *whole* window says nothing about stationarity *going forward*. **Finite samples and fat tails:** critical values are asymptotic, daily equity returns have heavy tails and volatility clustering, and the KPSS p-value is clipped to a small lookup table — so treat every p-value as a fuzzy guide, not a number. And the deepest caveat of all: **stationary is necessary, not sufficient**. Even a perfect `I(0)` spread is not a strategy. It still has to revert faster than costs accrue, survive out of sample, and be implementable on both legs in the Indian market — the gap this whole series exists to expose.
