A static hedge ratio is a photograph of a relationship that refuses to sit still for the camera. You fit one number - the slope of HDFCBANK on KOTAKBANK, say - on five years of data, and from that moment you act as if it were a law of physics: 1.055 units of KOTAKBANK short for every unit of HDFCBANK long, in 2019 and still in 2026. But two banks are not a law of physics. Their relative sensitivity moves with rate cycles, loan books, index reweightings and sentiment, and the moment that slope drifts, your "hedged" spread quietly stops being hedged - it leaks directional risk and breaches its own bands for reasons that have nothing to do with mean reversion. The obvious fix is a hedge ratio that updates every day, and the Kalman filter is the elegant way to do it. This chapter builds one from scratch - and then, with the same real data, shows you that the elegant thing **lost the backtest to the dumb flat number**. That result is the whole point.

## The number that pretends to hold still

Start with the diagnostic that exposes the lie. Instead of one OLS fit over the whole window, re-fit `A = alpha + beta * B` every single day on a trailing 120-day window using only past data, and watch `beta`. If it sits still, the static number is defensible. If it wanders, the static spread is structurally mis-hedged.

On HDFCBANK / KOTAKBANK over 2018-2026 - a pair the cointegration test actually picked in-sample at p = 0.0306, so this is a *real* relationship, not a fabricated one - the rolling beta is damning. The static in-sample fit hands you **beta = 1.055**. The 120-day rolling beta over the same series ranges from **-0.312 to +1.744**, with a mean of 0.625 and a standard deviation of 0.413. Its full swing is **329% of its own mean**, and it spends **7.5% of all days negative**. Negative. For two banks. A negative hedge ratio says you should hedge one lender by going *long* the other - economic nonsense for two names that rise and fall together. The static 1.055 is not a stable truth; it is the average of a quantity that genuinely moves.

But notice the trap on the *right* of that picture. The rolling beta confirms the static number is a fiction - and it also shows the naive fix is its own disease. A 120-day window is so jumpy it manufactures hedge ratios no risk desk would ever trade, including the negative ones. What we actually want is something **smoother than rolling OLS but still adaptive** - precisely the gap a Kalman filter is built to fill.

:::key
The static beta is provably wrong on this pair - the trailing estimate swings across 329% of its mean and even goes negative. But "the static number is wrong" does not imply "any adaptive number is right." Rolling OLS over-reacts; the question is whether a *smoother* adaptive estimate earns its complexity. Hold that question - the backtest answers it, and not the way you would guess.
:::

## A hidden state, estimated one bar at a time

Think of the hedge relationship as a hidden state you never observe directly and that drifts slowly over time. You only ever see prices. The Kalman filter is the optimal linear machine for exactly this situation: estimate a hidden, slowly-moving state from noisy observations, updating one bar at a time and using only the past.

The state we track is the pair `(intercept, hedge ratio)`, written `x = [alpha, beta]`. We assume no structural reason for it to move in any particular direction - only that it can wander - so the transition is a pure random walk, `x_t = x_{t-1} + w`, where `w` has covariance `Q`. The observation links today's price of A to the state and today's price of B: `A_t = alpha_t + beta_t * B_t + v`, with observation noise variance `R`. Then the filter runs two steps, forever:

The beautiful part is that the filter hands you the tradeable spread for free. The **innovation** `e_t = A_t - (alpha + beta * B_t)` - the part of today's A the filter did *not* expect from yesterday's state and today's B - is the dynamic spread. It uses only the past state plus today's B, so it is genuinely out-of-sample at every bar. The Kalman gain `K` decides how much of each surprise to believe, and it is governed entirely by two knobs.

We parameterise the process noise the Chan (2013) way, with a single dial `delta`: `Q = delta / (1 - delta) * I`. As `delta -> 0`, `Q -> 0` and the state freezes into a static beta with extra steps; as `delta` grows, the state sprints and re-hedges on every wiggle. `R` is the skepticism dial - large `R` says "most of today's move is noise, barely update," small `R` says "lunge at every print." Crucially, they only ever matter as a **ratio**: large `Q/R` trusts new data, small `Q/R` trusts the prior.

:::info
There is no market-given value for `delta` or `R`. The market does not tell you how fast beta is "really" allowed to drift. **We pick them** - and that act of picking is a fit. A static OLS beta has essentially zero discretionary knobs once you fix the window; the Kalman adds two continuous ones whose only ground truth is "whatever made the curve look good." Remember that the next time a dynamic-hedge backtest looks gorgeous.
:::

We start with a deliberately ordinary choice, `delta = 1e-4` and `R = 1e-3`, and stress-test it later.

## Three betas on one axis

With those settings the filter produces a beta that is everything we hoped for as a *description*. Over the full series its standard deviation is **0.087** - smoother than rolling OLS by a factor of nearly five (rolling sd was 0.413) - it ranges only from 0.635 to 1.067, and it **never goes negative**. It tracks genuine drift without the rolling estimate's nervous breakdowns.

Look at where the lines sit. The static beta is pinned flat at 1.055, but the market spent most of the sample with a true ratio nearer **0.73** - the Kalman line tracks down toward it while the static line stays stubbornly high. As a picture of "what was the hedge ratio actually doing," the Kalman wins outright. The static line is a single in-sample number that is wrong about most of the rest of the series. If description were the goal, we would be done. It is not. The goal is P&L.

## The dynamic spread you actually trade

We trade the Kalman spread with the *identical* z-score machinery the static-beta backtest uses: standardise by a trailing 60-day rolling mean and standard deviation (no look-ahead), enter when `|z|` exceeds 2.0, exit near 0.5. Keeping the z-score machinery identical means the **only** thing that changes between the two backtests is the spread itself - a clean, single-variable comparison.

{{example: ch11/02_cell16.py | The Kalman dynamic spread and its trading z-score | STAT}}

The top panel shows the dynamic spread with the filter's own `+/- sqrt(S)` uncertainty band; the bottom shows the rolling z-score crossing the entry and exit thresholds. It looks tradeable. It crosses zero, it stretches to the bands, it comes back. Every visual instinct says this should work at least as well as the crude static spread. Now we let the cash flows vote.

## The honest backtest: the simple beta wins

Same rules for both models, no cheating: signal at the close, fill at the *next* bar (positions shifted one day); the beta used to size day `t` is the beta known at `t`, lagged into the P&L; net subtracts a full round-trip cost on both legs every time the position changes, with the B-leg cost scaling by `beta`; and in-sample and out-of-sample are always reported separately, because the IS curve is a hypothesis and only the OOS curve is evidence.

{{example: ch11/03_cell20.py | Static vs Kalman equity: gross and net, in-sample and out | STAT}}

Here is the table the whole chapter has been walking toward. Net of costs:

| Model | IS net Sharpe | OOS net Sharpe | Trades |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| **Static beta** | **+1.12** | **+0.76** | 41 |
| Kalman beta | -0.53 | -1.50 | 78 |

The thing we spent two sections proving was "wrong" - the frozen 1.055 - earns a healthy **+1.12 Sharpe in-sample** and, the part that actually matters, **survives out-of-sample at +0.76 net**. The Kalman beta, with all its adaptive sophistication and its lovely smooth tracking, posts **-0.53 in-sample and -1.50 out-of-sample**, and trades nearly twice as often (78 versus 41) - paying costs on both legs each time to lock in a worse result. More flexibility did not buy more edge. It bought more ways to be wrong, and a bigger commission bill for the privilege.

:::warn
Adaptivity is not free. On this real, genuinely cointegrated bank pair, in this window, the static hedge ratio beat the Kalman hedge ratio by **1.65 Sharpe points net out-of-sample**, while trading roughly half as often. The dynamic model was a strictly better *description* of the relationship and a strictly worse *trade*. Never assume the more sophisticated estimator wins the P&L just because it wins the chart.
:::

## The knobs are a fit, not a discovery

A defender will say we just picked bad knobs - tune `delta` and `R` and the Kalman will shine. So we tested that directly. Sweep `delta` across two orders of magnitude at fixed `R` and watch the net out-of-sample Sharpe.

{{example: ch11/04_sweep_delta_at_fixed_r_report_is_and_oos.py | Net Sharpe swings with delta - and never clears the static bar | STAT}}

The OOS net Sharpe wanders from -0.08 at the gentlest setting down to -1.50, with no stable plateau - a tiny change in a knob we invented flips the result by more than a full Sharpe point. And the punchline: **no value of delta clears the static OOS bar of +0.76.** The best the Kalman manages is roughly break-even at the very smallest `delta` - which is the filter quietly *turning itself back into the static beta*. The closer it gets to the static number, the better it does.

Then the full two-dimensional `delta x R` grid, because the personality is the ratio, not either knob alone:

{{example: ch11/05_the_full_q_x_r_surface_net_oos_sharpe_ov.py | The full Q x R surface: 0 of 20 cells beat the static beta | STAT}}

Of the **20 cells** in the grid, the number that beat the static OOS net Sharpe of +0.76 is exactly **0**. Not one. The surface is a landscape of hills and pits, not a number - and picking the prettiest cell after seeing the out-of-sample result is curve-fitting, not edge. An honest forward test would have to fix `delta` and `R` *before* the OOS window exists, and any cell you fix on this evidence is a coin you watched land heads.

:::tip
If you must run a dynamic hedge, freeze `delta` and `R` on in-sample data and never touch them again, then judge the model only on the OOS window. The moment you re-tune after peeking at out-of-sample performance, your "result" is the maximum of a noisy surface - guaranteed to disappoint forward. The static beta's one virtue is that it has nothing left to tune.
:::

## Where this breaks

The Kalman filter is a genuinely better *description* of a drifting relationship than a static line. Whether it is a better *trade* is a separate question, and here, on a real cointegrated pair, in this window, net of costs, the answer was no. The ways it breaks:

- **The tuning is a fit, full stop.** `delta` and `R` have no market-given value. The grid swings the net OOS Sharpe across a wide range and sometimes flips its sign; any single reported number is one cell of an overfitting surface. The static beta has no such surface to get lost on.
- **More flexibility, more ways to overfit.** Every degree of freedom the Kalman adds is a degree the in-sample optimiser can exploit. The price of adaptivity is a model that fits noise as eagerly as signal - and out-of-sample, noise does not repeat.
- **A free intercept eats the very signal you trade.** Letting `alpha_t` drift means the filter can quietly absorb a real, persistent mis-pricing into a "new mean" instead of flagging it as a deviation to fade. The dynamic spread can look mean-reverting on the chart while the tradeable edge has been filtered away into the moving intercept.
- **The filter chases noise and pays for it twice.** A larger `delta` re-hedges on day-to-day wiggle, churning the book (78 trades versus 41), paying costs on both legs each time, and often locking in a worse beta just as the relationship wanders back. The net curve sits well below the gross curve for a reason.
- **A dynamic hedge can still de-cointegrate.** Tracking `beta` bar by bar does not guarantee the spread is stationary; an adaptive ratio can follow a relationship right out of its mean-reverting regime, leaving you with a smooth, well-tracked, non-reverting series that is worse than useless.

> **Real-life implementation note.** Everything here uses real adjusted equity closes to learn the statistics. The short leg is a research abstraction - shorting one bank against the other in Indian markets needs a real vehicle (intraday square-off, borrowed stock, or a stock-futures proxy), and each choice changes costs, margin, borrow availability, taxes and slippage. A statistical relationship existing - even a Kalman-tracked one - is not the same as a tradable edge existing. On this pair, the most sophisticated estimator did not even win the research backtest, let alone the implementation one.
