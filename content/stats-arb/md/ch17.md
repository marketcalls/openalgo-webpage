Sixteen chapters ago we made a single promise: to draw one bright line, between a statistical relationship and a tradable edge, and to never let a pretty number talk us across it. Everything since has been the machinery for holding that line - cointegration tests, spread construction, signal design, cost models, out-of-sample discipline, and a parade of techniques that looked brilliant in-sample and humiliating out of it. This final chapter does not teach a new trick. It assembles every earlier piece into **one disciplined workflow** you can run end to end, then reads back the honest scorecard of what actually survived. The arc has been deliberately brutal because the subject is. What is left at the end is small, hard-won, and real - and knowing exactly how small is the whole point.

## One chain, six links

A stat-arb programme is not a strategy; it is a pipeline, and every link can quietly break the one after it. Get the universe wrong and your cointegration test is mining noise. Trust correlation instead of cointegration and you select pairs with no anchor. Freeze a hedge ratio and the spread mis-hedges the moment the regime turns. The workflow below is the same chain the companion notebooks walk, in order, with no step skippable.

## What each link actually taught

**Data and universe** come first because every statistic downstream inherits their flaws. We restricted the search to liquid, F&O-eligible names in the same sector - an economic reason to co-move - and used point-in-time, split- and dividend-adjusted closes. The closing price is the right *learning* signal (one clean, comparable, auction-settled print per name per day, exactly what a cointegration test wants) and the wrong *execution* price (you cannot transact at an adjusted close, and your real fill lands on the next bar). Holding both ideas at once is the discipline the whole course is built on.

**Cointegration, not correlation,** is the entry exam. Correlation is a returns-level statistic about today; cointegration is a price-level statement about the long-run tether. They are not two flavours of one idea - across 75 same-sector pairs the rank correlation between return-correlation and the cointegration p-value was **-0.04**, statistically indistinguishable from zero. The most correlated pair in the table drifted apart and never came back; weaker-correlated pairs reverted cleanly. Screen on the wrong one and you systematically select spreads with no centre of gravity.

**Finding pairs without fooling yourself** is mostly defence against your own search. Run a cointegration test over hundreds of pairs and a handful pass at 5% by pure chance - the same spurious-regression machinery that makes 90% of *unrelated* random walks look "significant" on price levels. The honest screen starts from an economic prior, controls the false-discovery rate, and treats any low p-value pulled from a big scan as a hypothesis, never a discovery.

**Spread and signal** is the only genuinely mechanical step: an estimated cointegrating hedge ratio (not the price ratio, which silently assumes a beta of one), a *trailing* rolling z-score windowed to the spread's own half-life, and a four-zone rule - enter in a band, exit at the mean, hard-stop beyond it - filled on the next bar, never the same one. Assembled cleanly, this prints a gorgeous in-sample curve: on HDFCBANK against KOTAKBANK, **+98% gross at a 0.99 Sharpe, a 72% hit rate, and zero stops triggered.** Every number true, in that window, in-sample, gross. None of it a forecast.

## The honest scorecard

Then comes link five, where most ideas die, and where the course earns its keep. We took every seductive in-sample result out of sample, subtracted realistic NSE costs on two legs traded twice, and asked whether the relationship was even stable. Here is the whole arc on one page.

| Technique | Headline (in-sample / gross) | Honest verdict (net, out-of-sample) |
|---|---|---|
| Naive pairs (HDFCBANK / KOTAKBANK) | net Sharpe **1.01** | net Sharpe **0.34** - two-thirds gone |
| Dynamic hedge (Kalman beta) | promised adaptivity | OOS net **-1.50** vs static **+0.76**; **0 of 20** grid settings beat the static beta |
| Basket (Johansen, 5 banks) | **94%** of sub-baskets stationary | **0%** still stationary out-of-sample |
| Cross-sectional factor-neutral | gross Sharpe **1.34** | net Sharpe **-1.99** under turnover |
| Portfolio of 6 vol-targeted pairs | net Sharpe **0.94** | net Sharpe **0.43** out-of-sample |

Read that table slowly, because it is the course. The naive pair was not a bug - it was a clean, correct build whose edge was mostly a property of the window it was tuned on; held blind it kept barely a third of its Sharpe, and the pair failed the cointegration test in about **90% of rolling windows**. The Kalman filter, the "obvious" fix for a drifting hedge ratio, did not just underperform the frozen beta - it *lost money* out of sample, and no setting on a 20-point grid cleared the static bar, because a hedge ratio that chases noise mis-hedges more than a stale one. The Johansen basket was the sharpest trap: 94% of candidate baskets tested stationary in-sample and exactly none survived, because a `k`-leg vector has `k-1` free weights and estimation error is where baskets go to die.

The flagship pair makes that deflation concrete - every honest step, taken in order, knocks the seductive number down toward zero. Nothing here is a leak; it is the same strategy seen through progressively less flattering glass.

{{example: ch17/r_11_pull_together_every_number_into_one_hone.py | The seductive in-sample Sharpe, deflated one honest step at a time | RISK}}

The cross-sectional book is the most instructive failure of all, because its signal was *real*.

:::warn
The cross-sectional, factor-neutral book ranks the whole universe and is the form the large quant desks actually run. Its gross edge here was genuine - a broadly monotonic **+3.89 bps/day** top-minus-bottom decile spread, a Sharpe of **1.34 gross**. And it still lost: net Sharpe **-1.99**, because the signal demanded roughly **235x** annual one-way turnover, and a gross **2.48 bps/day** cannot out-run a **6.45 bps/day** cost. The breakeven per-side cost was **2.66 bps** against a realistic **6.9 bps**. A real edge that inverts under its own turnover is not an edge - it is a toll you pay the market.
:::

:::key
Four families of technique, one verdict: a statistical relationship existing is not the same as a tradable edge existing. Naive pairs were marginal net and out of sample; the dynamic hedge lost to the static beta; the basket overfit to zero; the cross-sectional gross edge inverted under turnover. The risk-managed six-pair book is the closest thing to a survivor - a net out-of-sample Sharpe near **0.43** - and even it is a marginal, fragile, costs-and-borrow-sensitive number, not a money machine.
:::

This is the funnel every notebook from chapter three onward is really one layer of: thousands of pairs co-move, fewer are cointegrated in-sample, fewer still stay cointegrated out-of-sample, fewer again survive realistic costs, and only what is left at the very bottom is implementable and net-positive.

## What would make a model genuinely tradable

The scorecard is not a counsel of despair - it is a specification. A model that clears every bar above is rare, but it is not mythical, and the four things that separate it from a backtest artefact are concrete.

**A real short-leg vehicle.** Every backtest in this course shorted freely; that is the single largest abstraction. In Indian cash equities an intraday short is force-squared off near the close, so overnight reversion cannot be carried that way. The realistic default is the **stock future** - liquid in large names, naturally short-able - but it drags in basis, a monthly roll cost, lot-size granularity, margin on each leg, and the Market-Wide Position Limit: cross 95% of it and the name enters a ban period where you cannot add, often exactly when the spread is most crowded. SLB borrow is the cleanest economic match but is thin, lumpy and recallable. No short, no market-neutral trade.

**Costs modelled net, on two legs, both ways.** A pair round trip is eight fills, each paying statutory charges plus half the spread plus impact. The honest number ranges from tens of basis points for a delivery hold to **over 120 bps** once you model real effective spreads and impact on two legs intraday. The edge must clear all of it before a rupee is yours - and the cross-sectional book proves a real gross signal can sit entirely below that line.

**Capacity you can actually fill.** A signal that needs more size than the thinner leg can absorb is a paper edge. Liquidity is the first filter, not an afterthought - the span across even a blue-chip universe is wide, and a pair that yokes a liquid name to a thin one inherits the thin one's execution problems on both legs.

{{example: ch17/01_average_daily_traded_value_adv_over_the_.py | Liquidity is the first filter: the traded-value span across a blue-chip universe | NSE}}

**Validation that does not flatter.** Naive cross-validation leaks through overlapping labels (Sharpe 0.78); purged-and-embargoed CV stripped that to **0.50**. Deflate the best-of-many-trials Sharpe for the search itself and the Deflated Sharpe sits at **0.96** with a **54%** probability of backtest overfitting - barely better than a coin flip that the search found anything real. The out-of-sample Sharpe of 0.34 carries a 95% bootstrap interval of **[-0.47, 1.15]** that comfortably contains zero, with a **20%** probability the true Sharpe is negative. A single backtested number without that uncertainty beside it is the optimistic end of a range, not the answer.

:::tip
The honest workflow inverts the usual leaderboard. Start from an economic prior, not a scan. Pre-register the rank, the vector and the parameters before touching out-of-sample data. Prefer the fewest legs that capture the relationship - every added weight is estimation variance and cost. Walk forward, re-estimate, and accept the turnover and overfitting that re-estimation brings. Then validate against the search, not just against the past. The technique that survives all of that earns a small allocation; the one that merely won the in-sample beauty contest earns a place back in research.
:::

## Where this breaks

Even this synthesis is provisional, and the honest caveats are the same ones that have shadowed every chapter.

- **Every number is in-this-window.** Different universes, windows and cost models give different verdicts. The scorecard is a faithful account of one decade of NSE data, not a law of markets. Re-run it on yours.
- **The short leg is still assumed.** The whole course shorts freely on equity closes. Real implementation needs a futures, SLB or intraday vehicle, each with basis, roll, borrow, margin and ban risk the backtests never paid.
- **Survivorship flatters all of it.** The universe is today's index members looking back; the pairs that merged or delisted never entered the search, so even the humbling numbers are the flattering version.
- **The survivor is marginal, not safe.** The risk-managed book's out-of-sample Sharpe near 0.43 is the best honest result in the course, and it is still thin, costs-sensitive, borrow-dependent and one regime shift from breaking. A small allocation, watched closely, with a kill switch - not a livelihood.
- **Crowding makes the edge shared.** Thousands of desks run the same screens; market-neutral to the index is not neutral to other arbitrageurs deleveraging into you.

If you take one sentence from seventeen chapters, take the one the whole course was built to earn: **a statistical relationship is not a tradable edge.** Educational content only, not investment advice.
