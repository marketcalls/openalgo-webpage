Every earlier chapter handed you a *research object*: a cointegrated spread, a trailing z-score, a net-of-cost Sharpe that survived an honest out-of-sample window. None of those is a position. The gap between a surviving hypothesis and a managed book on NSE is not one decision - it is a pipeline of them, and most of the ways to lose money hide in that pipeline, not in the signal. This chapter is the unglamorous machinery: which vehicle carries each leg, what legging actually costs when two orders cannot fill at the same instant, how wide the spread and impact really are when measured from bars instead of assumed, and the monitoring, kill switch and retirement discipline that decide whether an edge survives contact with reality. Nothing here sends an order - this is education about implementation, not a recipe for placing trades. The whole point: a statistical relationship is not a tradable edge, and even a tradable edge dies without this scaffolding.

## The pipeline is a loop, not a launch button

Beginners build the left half of the system - data, signal, backtest - and skip the right half, which is the half that keeps you solvent. Implementation is a loop: a net-positive hypothesis chooses a *vehicle* for each leg; the vehicle dictates *execution* (legging, slippage, participation, square-off); execution feeds *monitoring* (the running spread, z-score, exposure, PnL); and monitoring either keeps the trade on or **retires** it and sends the idea back to research - never straight back to the order book.

## Picking the vehicle: the holding-period crossover

The long leg is trivial - buy the cash name for delivery and hold. The implementation problem lives in the **short leg**, where there are three honest vehicles, each a trade-off you choose rather than a default. **Cash intraday** carries no overnight cost but forces a square-off near 3:20pm - so you re-cross the full spread every day just to stay in. **SLB-borrowed stock** pays a borrow fee that is lumpy and scarce: many names have no borrow at all, and the fee can spike to double digits exactly when everyone wants the same short. **Stock futures** are the workhorse - one entry and exit cost, a small running carry, a roll every expiry, and a basis that wobbles around spot.

The decisive variable is **how long you must hold**. Intraday re-crossing is a straight ramp; the once-paid vehicles start higher but barely grow. Price the crossover directly from the cost model and the line is stark: with a per-round-trip transaction cost of about **28 bps** (four fills - two legs, entered and exited), intraday re-crossing overtakes futures after roughly **one day** of holding.

{{example: ch16/01_carry_cost_crossover_total_round_trip_co.py | Intraday re-crossing overtakes futures after about one day held | EXEC}}

:::key
The vehicle is dictated by holding period, not preference. A sub-day mean-reversion signal can use cash intraday; anything held for days or weeks - which is most cointegration trades, with their multi-day half-lives - must use futures or SLB, because paying the full spread twice a day to *stay* in a multi-week trade is ruinous. The futures line embeds a roll every roughly 30 days, and its real enemy is not this transaction cost but basis wobble and lot-size granularity that the price series never showed you.
:::

That last point is the quiet one: you estimate the hedge ratio on cash closes but trade it in futures that sit off spot, round to fixed lots, and price an expected dividend the cash series already removed. Each is a small, structural tracking error between the spread you backtested and the one you trade - always there, not noise you can diversify away.

## Legging risk: two orders that must both happen

In a backtest both legs fill on the same bar at the same price. In reality you send leg A and leg B, one fills first, and in the gap before the other fills, the market moves. That gap is **legging risk**, and it produces four distinct failures: slippage on the second leg as it drifts away while you chase it; partial fills that leave you off-ratio; **hedge-leg failure**, where one leg fills and the other is rejected or untradeable and you are now naked directional - the exact risk the pair was built to remove; and outright order rejection from margin, a price band, a freeze quantity or an F&O ban.

Put a number on it with a Monte Carlo calibrated on real intraday data. Measured **5-minute return volatility is 13.1 bps**, which works out to about **0.757 bps per second** of unhedged exposure; simulate thousands of legged entries against a spread-only floor of **5.2 bps** and the distribution is not the floor. The **median legged entry costs 7.8 bps**, the **95th-percentile fill costs 16.2 bps** - a bad-gap fill at multiples of the spread - and **24% of entries cost more than twice the spread floor**. The histogram is right-skewed because the gap drift is random but you systematically chase *into* the move. And this is entry only: the exit legs the same way, doubling the legging drag over a round trip.

{{example: ch16/02_two_leg_fill_slippage_simulated_and_cali.py | Legged entry: median 7.8 bps, 95th-percentile 16.2 bps, 24% above 2x the spread floor | RISK}}

The two defences pull in opposite directions. Market orders guarantee both legs fill but pay the full chase; limit orders cap the slippage but raise the chance leg B never fills - the worse failure, because it leaves you naked. And "naked" is not a rounding error: at a generous **95% per-leg fill probability you still end up unhedged about 9.5% of the time** - one trade in ten - rising to 18% at 90% per leg. That is not a small directional bet; it is a full single-stock position the strategy never sized for.

:::warn
The most expensive outcome in pairs trading is not slippage - it is being unhedged. A 95%-reliable fill on each leg leaves you naked roughly one entry in ten, and that tail is not in any slippage histogram. The legging simulation prices the gap; it cannot price the regime where leg B is simply unavailable - no borrow, an F&O ban, a locked circuit - and you sit directional in a position you never wanted.
:::

## Costs you can least defend: spread and impact

The cost model has been carrying *assumed* values for the half-spread and impact - the two numbers you can least defend in advance, because they move with your size, the time of day and how the book looks when you trade. So measure them. There is no quoted bid-ask in OHLCV, so estimate the effective spread with the **Corwin-Schultz high-low estimator**, which recovers the bid-ask from consecutive bar ranges. Across the liquid large-caps the effective spread clusters tightly - **4.6 to 5.8 bps**, a representative median around **5.3 bps**. The naive fallback, when intraday is unavailable, is the daily high-low range - and it is wrong by an order of magnitude, **about 33 times** the effective spread, because it bundles the whole day's volatility into "spread".

{{example: ch16/04_visualise_the_measured_effective_spread_.py | Effective spread about 5.3 bps; the daily-range fallback overstates it roughly 33x | NSE}}

Impact is the other half. Model it as square-root in participation - the price moves roughly as the square root of how much of the bar's volume you take. Calibrated on each name's daily volatility, that is about **8.3 bps at 1% participation, 18.5 bps at 5%, and 26.1 bps at 10%**. The curve is convex-down: doubling your size does not double the impact, but it never stops growing either.

{{example: ch16/05_square_root_market_impact_bigger_orders_.py | Square-root impact: 8.3 bps at 1%, 18.5 bps at 5%, 26.1 bps at 10% participation | EXEC}}

That convexity is the entire argument for a **participation cap** - a hard rule never to take more than X% of a bar's volume. A reusable slippage function that charges spread plus impact per fill prices the trade-off: about **10.4 bps per fill at a 1% cap, 27.3 bps at 10%**. A tight cap (2-5%) protects your price but stretches the exit across more bars - and a long exit is exactly when a circuit, a gap or a borrow recall can trap you mid-unwind; a loose cap (10-20%) gets you flat fast but pays the convex impact and leaks information. No setting is both cheap and fast.

Now assemble one honest number. A pair round trip is four fills, and each pays statutory charges, half the measured spread, and the modelled impact at the participation cap. Stack them and the **total round-trip cost is about 123 bps - 1.23% of one leg's notional.** That is the hurdle every signal must clear before there is a single rupee of edge. If your backtested edge per trade is, say, 30 to 50 bps, this one number decides whether the strategy lives or dies.

:::info
Every cost here is an estimate, and estimates flatter. The Corwin-Schultz spread is inferred from OHLC, not quoted; the impact model is a square-root assumption with a chosen coefficient; the legging vol is one name's recent tape. When the assembled hurdle is 123 bps and a plausible per-trade edge is 30 to 50 bps, the right question is not "is it profitable?" but "how wrong can my cost estimate be before the sign flips?" - and the answer is: not very.
:::

## Monitoring and the kill switch

A managed pair is not fire-and-forget. The minimum viable system is a small pipeline: a data feed drives a signal engine; every order passes a risk-and-limits gate before it reaches the order path; and a monitoring loop watches the running state - spread, z-score, exposure, PnL, a both-legs-on check, margin headroom - with a kill switch wired straight to a flatten action.

Before any of this touches a broker, the entire loop runs in **sandbox trading (analyzer mode in OpenAlgo)**, where the same code path simulates fills and returns order objects without sending anything. Conceptually the order path has two shapes: a discrete order per leg, carrying a `product` (`MIS` intraday or `NRML` carry) and a `price_type` (`MARKET` or `LIMIT`); and a smart, idempotent order that reconciles a symbol to a *target* position size - the natural fit for a pair that must hold a neutral ratio and be flattened to zero. We describe the signatures only; nothing here is an instruction to place a trade.

:::key
A kill switch is only as good as the feed and the discipline behind it. A stale tick, a missed alert, or a single human override "just this once" defeats the whole apparatus. The intraday square-off clock is the same kind of hard rule: flatten on *your* schedule before the auto-square-off window, never at the market's worst liquidity on the broker's clock. The switch must be mechanical and pre-committed, or it is decoration.
:::

## Retiring a strategy when the edge dies

The hardest gate has no dramatic trigger: a slow death. An edge erodes as others find it, as the relationship weakens, as costs rise. Take a real pair, fix the hedge ratio on the early window (here **beta = 1.08** on the first two years), and watch the rolling co-movement and spread stability degrade. The diagnosis is blunt: the **120-day return correlation spends 48% of the history below 0.5** - nearly half the time, the two names are not even moving together at the level the trade assumed. When co-movement spends long stretches under your threshold and the spread keeps widening instead of reverting, the relationship that justified the pair is decaying.

{{example: ch16/08_edge_decay_on_a_real_pair_rolling_co_mov.py | Edge decay: 120-day co-movement sits below 0.5 in 48% of windows | RISK}}

The correct action is not to size up and "wait for reversion" - that is the gambler's move, doubling into a dead edge while it bleeds 123 bps every round trip. It is to retire the strategy and send the idea back to research; the data, not your hope, makes the call. That is the loop closing - monitoring detected decay, the retire gate fired, and the idea returns to research, not to the order book.

## Where this breaks

- **Every cost here is an estimate, and estimates are optimistic.** The Corwin-Schultz spread is inferred from OHLC, not quoted; the impact model is a square-root assumption with a chosen coefficient; the legging Monte Carlo is calibrated on one name's recent 5-minute vol. Real fills in thin names, fast tape or a stressed market are worse - sometimes far worse - and the 123 bps hurdle is the floor, not the ceiling.
- **The legging simulation assumes you always get both legs.** It prices the slippage of a gap, not the regime where leg B is simply unavailable - no borrow, an F&O ban, a locked circuit - and you sit naked. A 95% per-leg fill still leaves you naked roughly one trade in ten, and that tail is not in any histogram above.
- **The carry-cost crossover hides a scarce-borrow reality.** The SLB line uses one tidy fee; in practice borrow is lumpy, recallable mid-trade, and absent for exactly the names you most want to short. The futures line ignores margin financing and gap-day margin calls that can force a de-gross at the worst moment, plus the structural tracking error of basis, lot rounding and dividends.
- **The kill switch and monitoring are only as good as the feed and the discipline behind them.** A stale tick, a missed alert or a human override defeats the whole apparatus. The switch must be mechanical and pre-committed, or it is decoration.
- **No backtest, however honest, equals a real track record.** The research-to-reality gap - queue position, partial fills, rejections, latency, basis, recalls, regime change - only reveals itself with small size, in sandbox first. Sandbox results are a hypothesis about execution, not proof of it.

The final, honest word: a statistical relationship is not a tradable edge, and a tradable edge, once found, is not permanent. What stands between a clean research curve and a surviving book is the unglamorous machinery of this chapter - the right vehicle, controlled legging, measured costs, hard participation caps, square-off discipline, monitoring, a pre-committed kill switch, and the maturity to retire a strategy when its edge is gone. Build that machinery, or do not trade the edge at all. Educational content only, not investment advice.
