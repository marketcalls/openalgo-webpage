A pair is a quiet confession that you only trust yourself to estimate one number. But five bank stocks do not move in pairs - they are all dragged around by a single shared force (rates, the credit cycle, the banking factor), and the cleanest mean-reverting object hiding inside a sector is usually a **basket** of three to five names, not a hand-picked twosome. The promise of the basket is real: pool the reversion across several legs and no single bad quarter can snap the whole spread. The cost is brutal and badly advertised - instead of one hedge ratio you now estimate an entire **vector** of weights, and a vector fitted to one window is a fragile, high-dimensional object that overfits in ways a pair never could. This chapter builds the basket honestly with the Johansen test, then tears it down with its own out-of-sample numbers.

## Why one pair is leaving information on the table

Engle-Granger handles exactly two series: pick a dependent variable, regress, test the residual. That is a deliberate simplification, and it throws away structure. The five large-cap banks we will use here - HDFCBANK, ICICIBANK, KOTAKBANK, AXISBANK, SBIN - share **one** dominant common factor. A pair of any two of them captures only a slice of that shared cycle, and the moment one name de-couples (a bad result, a regulatory action on a single bank) the two-leg spread breaks with nothing to cushion it. A basket spreads the bet across more legs, so a single name leaving the relationship does proportionally less damage.

We fix one estimation window for the whole chapter, **2019-01-01 to 2023-12-29** (1,239 trading days), and work in log prices throughout - Johansen, like Engle-Granger, is defined on `I(1)` levels, and a vector on logs reads as a unit-free return-for-return relationship. Rebased to 100 at the window start, the five banks fan out and wander back: the cross-sectional spread of the rebased lines ranges from 0 to 137 index points across the window. That co-movement is the *necessary* visual precondition. Whether a **fixed combination** of them is pinned to a mean is the sufficient part, and only a test can answer it.

:::key
A basket pools the mean-reversion bet across several names, so no single de-coupling breaks the whole spread. That is the entire upside. The entire downside is that you have traded one well-pinned hedge ratio for a vector of `k-1` free weights estimated from the same fixed sample - and estimation error is where baskets go to die.
:::

## What the Johansen test actually asks

Engle-Granger needs a left-hand-side variable; with `k` names there is no natural one, and there may be **more than one** stationary combination living inside the prices. The Johansen procedure treats all `k` series symmetrically. It fits a vector error-correction model and asks: of the `k` possible directions through these prices, how many are **stationary** (mean-reverting) rather than random walks? That count is the cointegration **rank** `r`.

The eigenvectors that fall out of the procedure *are* the basket weights, ordered by how strongly each combination reverts. We run it with `det_order=0` (a constant in the cointegrating relation, appropriate for drifting price levels) and one lag (`k_ar_diff=1`). Both are modelling decisions, not defaults handed down from nature.

:::info
Change `det_order` or the lag count and the chosen rank can change. An expert states these choices out loud rather than letting a library pick them silently - because two analysts who disagree on the deterministic terms can get different rank verdicts on the *same* prices, and neither is "wrong."
:::

## Running the test: trace and max-eigenvalue disagree

Johansen returns two sequences of statistics, each compared against tabulated 95% critical values. The **trace** statistic tests `H0: rank <= r` against `rank > r`; you walk `r` up from zero and stop at the first rank you cannot reject. The **maximum-eigenvalue** statistic tests `rank = r` against `rank = r+1`, a sharper one-step-at-a-time test. On our basket, in this window, they do not agree.

| H0 | trace stat | trace 95% | reject? | max-eig stat | max-eig 95% | reject? |
|---|---:|---:|:--:|---:|---:|:--:|
| rank <= 0 | 75.18 | 69.82 | yes | 29.61 | 33.88 | no |
| rank <= 1 | 45.56 | 47.85 | no | 24.85 | 27.59 | no |
| rank <= 2 | 20.72 | 29.80 | no | 14.61 | 21.13 | no |

The trace test clears its bar at `rank <= 0` (75.18 > 69.82) and then fails at `rank <= 1` (45.56 < 47.85), so it picks **rank r = 1**: one stationary basket exists. The max-eigenvalue test never clears even its first bar (29.61 < 33.88), so it picks **rank r = 0**: no stationary combination at all. The two textbook statistics, run on identical data, return opposite verdicts.

{{example: ch12/02_cell13.py | Trace says r=1, max-eigenvalue says r=0: the tests disagree | STAT}}

That disagreement is not a nuisance to be smoothed over - it *is* the finding. When trace and max-eigenvalue diverge, the cointegration is **weak**. The max-eigenvalue test is the more conservative of the two, and the one to trust more for trading, because it guards against reading too many relations into noise. The honest move is to report both, take the cautious view, and trade only the first eigenvector if you trade at all. A weak, contested relationship is exactly the kind that fails to survive costs and regime change - which the rest of the chapter then demonstrates with numbers.

## The cointegrating vector is your basket

The first column of the eigenvector matrix is the strongest-reverting combination, and its entries are the portfolio weights. Sign and scale are arbitrary, so we normalise the first leg (HDFCBANK) to `+1`; the others then read as "per one unit long HDFCBANK, hold this much of each name."

| Leg | Weight (HDFCBANK pinned to +1) | Side |
|---|---:|---|
| HDFCBANK | +1.000 | long |
| ICICIBANK | -0.166 | short |
| KOTAKBANK | -0.518 | short |
| AXISBANK | -0.115 | short |
| SBIN | -0.017 | short |

So the basket is: long HDFCBANK against a weighted short of the other four, with KOTAKBANK carrying most of the short side. The basket spread is just `vector . log-prices` - the weighted sum Johansen claims is stationary - and we z-score it against its in-window mean and standard deviation. It crosses its mean **71 times** across the window, the busy signature of reversion, and the entry/exit bands at plus and minus one and two standard deviations are visible in the lower panel.

{{example: ch12/04_cell20.py | The basket spread and its z-score: 71 mean crossings in-window | STAT}}

Formally, the ADF test on the basket spread gives a statistic of **-4.285 and a p-value of 0.0005** (1%/5%/10% criticals -3.44 / -2.86 / -2.57), a clean rejection of the unit root, and the AR(1) half-life is **23.7 trading days** - about 1.1 months, a sensible holding period. The lag-1 autocorrelation of 0.971 is exactly what a 24-day half-life implies. On paper, this is a textbook mean-reverting basket.

:::warn
That ADF p-value of 0.0005 is **optimistic by construction**. The Johansen vector was fitted to make this exact basket as stationary as possible, so testing the fitted spread for stationarity is grading your own homework. The un-cheated evidence that any relation exists is the trace test from the previous section - and that one was already weak and contested. Hold the half-life and the p-value loosely.
:::

## Now the honesty: the vector will not hold still

Everything above is in-sample. The single most dangerous assumption in basket trading is that the weight vector is a constant of nature. It is an *estimate* with `k-1` free parameters, and it moves. Re-run Johansen on the first half and the second half of the same window and the weights do not line up - they lurch.

| Leg | full window | 1st half | 2nd half |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| ICICIBANK | -0.166 | +0.621 | -6.912 |
| KOTAKBANK | -0.518 | -1.250 | -4.981 |
| AXISBANK | -0.115 | +0.581 | -0.713 |
| SBIN | -0.017 | -0.797 | +8.564 |

Three of the four free legs **flip sign** between the two halves. ICICIBANK goes from a long (+0.621) to a heavy short (-6.912); SBIN swings from -0.797 to +8.564. A sign flip is not a rounding wobble - it turns a long leg into a short one, which is catastrophic for any book holding the position. A block-bootstrap of the full-window vector confirms it is not bad luck: the bootstrap standard deviation over its own weight is 1.43 for ICICIBANK, 1.07 for AXISBANK, and 1.84 for SBIN. When that ratio exceeds one, the weight's *sign* is not even reliably estimated.

{{example: ch12/06_cell27.py | The "constant" vector is not constant: same basket, two halves, signs flip | STAT}}

The cleanest demonstration of the damage is to take the vector fitted on 2019-2023 and apply it, unchanged, to 2024-2026 - data it never saw. A genuinely cointegrated basket keeps reverting around the same mean. This one does not: the out-of-sample spread drifts out of its bands and the **ADF p-value collapses from 0.0005 in-sample to 0.5583 out-of-sample** - from "firmly stationary" to "cannot reject a random walk."

{{example: ch12/07_cell29.py | Same fixed vector, in-sample (p=0.0005) vs out-of-sample (p=0.558) | STAT}}

This is structural, not unlucky. A `k`-leg vector has `k-1` weights estimated from the same fixed sample, so more legs means more parameters means more places for noise to enter. And the bill is real too: a one-way fill costs roughly 6.9 bps in our intraday cost model, so a two-leg pair round-trips at about **28 bps** while a five-leg basket round-trips at about **69 bps** - cost scales linearly with legs, and a basket trips its threshold more often because any single name can drag the spread across a band.

## The selection trap: 94% in-sample, 0% out-of-sample

The deepest trap is the one nobody warns about: we are tempted not just to *test* a basket but to *choose* the best one. Enumerate every sub-basket of 3, 4, and 5 of these banks - 16 combinations - rank them by in-sample stationarity, and the winners look spectacular. **94% of them test stationary in-sample** at the 5% level. Then apply each winning vector, unchanged, to the out-of-sample window, and of those in-sample winners, **0% are still stationary out-of-sample**. The in-sample and out-of-sample p-values carry a correlation of just +0.27 - close enough to nothing.

{{example: ch12/10_cell38.py | Selection overfits: every basket wins in-sample, none survives out-of-sample | STAT}}

The strongest in-sample basket - the full five-name set, ADF p = 0.00047 - posts an out-of-sample p of 0.558. The points hug the left edge of the chart (great in-sample) and smear across the top (dead out-of-sample); the bottom-left "survives both" corner is essentially empty. Picking the basket that tests best is look-ahead bias wearing a lab coat. The in-sample p-value tells you almost nothing about the future.

:::tip
The honest workflow inverts the leaderboard. Start with an **economic prior** - these are all banks for a reason, not because a scan blessed them. Pre-register the rank and the vector before looking at out-of-sample data. Prefer the **fewest legs** that capture the relationship, because every added leg adds estimation variance and cost while rarely adding signal. Confirm out-of-sample, always. Never trade the basket that merely won the in-sample scan.
:::

## Where this breaks

Baskets buy you diversification of the mean-reversion bet and surrender almost everything else to estimation error. In rough order of how often each one kills a real basket:

- **The cointegrating vector is unstable.** Re-estimated on two halves of the *same* window, three of four free weights flipped sign. A fixed vector is a snapshot of one regime, not a law. Any real implementation must re-estimate continuously and swallow the turnover and overfitting that brings.
- **Estimation error grows with the legs.** With `k-1` weights from one sample, the bootstrap standard deviation of several weights rivalled their magnitude, so even the sign was in doubt. Past a point, more legs is more noise, not more signal.
- **Out-of-sample, the basket can simply stop reverting.** The exact vector that gave an in-sample ADF p of 0.0005 drifted out of its bands and failed the test (p = 0.558) in the next window. In-sample stationarity is a weak hypothesis about the future.
- **Selection is look-ahead by another name.** 94% of sub-baskets looked cointegrated in-sample and 0% survived out-of-sample, with the two p-values nearly uncorrelated. Picking "the basket that tests best" manufactures a result that does not exist.
- **Trace and max-eigenvalue disagreed on the rank.** When the two Johansen statistics diverge - r = 1 versus r = 0 here - the cointegration is weak, and weak cointegration is exactly what fails to survive costs and regime change.
- **Every extra leg costs.** Round-trip cost scales linearly with legs (about 28 bps for a pair, 69 bps for a five-name basket), and a basket trips its threshold more often. The basket must out-earn the pair by a wide margin just to break even, and after costs it usually does not.

A basket is the right *idea* - pool the reversion, neutralise the common factor - and a dangerous *object* - a high-dimensional estimate that overfits. Treat the Johansen vector as a fragile prior to be re-checked out-of-sample, use the fewest legs that capture the relationship, and never trade the basket that simply won the in-sample scan. The next logical step is to stop hand-picking one basket at all and instead neutralise market and sector returns across the whole universe, ranking the residuals - a cross-sectional, factor-neutral book.

> **Real-life implementation note.** Everything here uses real equity price series to *learn the statistics*. Treat the short legs as a research abstraction unless you have a valid implementation path. In Indian markets a short may require stock futures, borrowed stock, intraday square-off, or another instrument, and each choice changes costs, margin, borrow availability, taxes, slippage, and risk. A statistical relationship existing is not the same as a tradable edge existing.
