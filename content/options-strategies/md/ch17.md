The ratio back spreads from chapters six and eleven bought more options than they sold, so they opened for a debit, kept a defined loss, and paid off only on a big, fast move. This chapter flips that ratio on its head. Now you sell more than you buy. You collect a fat credit upfront, you profit most if NIFTY drifts gently toward the strike you sold, and you carry a naked tail that can run away if NIFTY moves too far in the wrong direction. These are the **call ratio spread** and the **put ratio spread**. They look clever and they often win, but the uncapped side is real, so read both charts with your eyes open.

## The call ratio spread

A **call ratio spread** buys one at-the-money call and sells two calls a little higher. Because you sold one more call than you bought, the structure leans net short of calls above the higher strike, which is where the danger lives. You build it on the 28 July 2026 expiry like this.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 paid |
| 2 | Sell | 24,150 | Call (x2) | about Rs 48,106 received |

You pay about Rs 27,677 for the one bought call and take in about Rs 48,106 for the two sold calls, so the position opens for a healthy **net credit of about Rs 20,430**. That credit is yours to keep if NIFTY simply stays below 24,050, where every call expires worthless.

The clever part is the peak. As NIFTY rises from 24,050 toward 24,150, your bought call gains value while the two sold calls are still worthless, so your profit climbs. At exactly 24,150 the bought call is worth a full 100 points, about Rs 6,500, on top of the credit you already hold. That is the **peak profit of Rs 26,930**, reached when NIFTY pins the strike you sold.

Push past 24,150 and the two sold calls wake up against you. You are now short one net call, losing 65 rupees a point, so the profit melts away and then turns to loss. The **breakeven is 24,564**, the point where the at-expiry line crosses back through zero. Above it, the loss grows without any ceiling.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 26,930 (at 24,150) |
| Max loss | Unlimited |
| Breakeven | 24,564 |
| Probability of profit | 72 percent |
| Risk to reward | NA |
| Net credit | Rs 20,430 |
| Margin required | Rs 176,491 |

:::warn
A call ratio spread has a maximum loss that is UNLIMITED. The extra sold 24,150 call is naked, so once NIFTY climbs past the breakeven of 24,564 you lose 65 rupees for every point it keeps rising, with no ceiling at all. The exchange blocks about Rs 1.76 lakh of margin precisely because of that uncapped tail. The comfortable 72 percent probability of profit comes from the wide cushion before the loss starts, but a strong rally past the peak can turn a string of wins into one large loss.
:::

:::key
A ratio spread is a credit trade that wants NIFTY to land near the strike you sold. It profits across a wide zone, peaks exactly at the sold strike, and then exposes a naked tail beyond it. The credit you collect is the reward for carrying that uncapped risk.
:::

## Reading the call ratio spread chart

The chart is the real payoff, drawn from live NIFTY data captured on 26 June 2026, with spot 24,056, the 24,050 and 24,150 strikes, lot 65, and about 32 days to expiry. Walk the orange at-expiry line from left to right. Below 24,050 it sits flat on the credit of Rs 20,430, the green floor. From 24,050 it ramps up to the peak of Rs 26,930 at 24,150, then slopes down, crosses zero at the **breakeven of 24,564**, and plunges into the red beyond it with no floor.

The blue dashed T+0 (today) line is smoother and sits below the peak, because the sold time value has not yet decayed. Every calm day pulls the position up toward that orange peak. The faint sigma bands show the move the option market is pricing in by expiry, and the pink line marks spot at 24,056, just left of the rising ramp.

:::info
The **estimated premium** here is about Rs 75,783, the gross value of all three legs added together. That is a turnover figure, not your risk. Your **total P&L** sits at about zero the moment you enter, because you have just exchanged fair value. Both numbers only move as NIFTY does.
:::

## The put ratio spread

The **put ratio spread** is the same idea pointed downward. You buy one at-the-money put and sell two puts a little lower, leaving you net short one put below the lower strike.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 paid |
| 2 | Sell | 23,950 | Put (x2) | about Rs 33,352 received |

You pay about Rs 19,182 for the one bought put and collect about Rs 33,352 for the two sold puts, opening for a **net credit of about Rs 14,170**. You keep that credit in full if NIFTY stays above 24,050.

The peak sits at the lower strike. As NIFTY falls from 24,050 toward 23,950, the bought put gains while the sold puts stay worthless, lifting profit to its **maximum of Rs 20,670** when NIFTY pins 23,950, where the bought put is worth a full 100 points on top of the credit. Below 23,950 the two sold puts turn against you. You are now short one net put, losing 65 rupees a point as NIFTY keeps falling. The **breakeven is 23,632**, and below it the loss grows toward a very large figure on a genuine collapse.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 20,670 (at 23,950) |
| Max loss | Rs 1,536,080 |
| Breakeven | 23,632 |
| Probability of profit | 68 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1:0.01 |
| Net credit | Rs 14,170 |
| Margin required | Rs 170,795 |

:::warn
A put ratio spread carries a very large downside loss, listed as Rs 1,536,080 if NIFTY fell toward zero. The extra sold 23,950 put is naked to the downside, so once NIFTY breaks below the breakeven of 23,632 you lose 65 rupees a point with only the floor of zero stopping it. That is why the exchange blocks about Rs 1.71 lakh of margin. The 68 percent probability of profit reflects the wide cushion above, not the depth of the tail. A sharp fall is exactly what hurts this trade most.
:::

## How a ratio spread differs from a back spread

It is easy to confuse these credit ratio spreads with the debit ratio back spreads from earlier. They are near opposites, and the difference is which side you over-weight.

- A **back spread** buys two and sells one, so it is net long. It pays a small debit, keeps a defined loss, and needs a big, fast move to pay off.
- A **ratio spread** sells two and buys one, so it is net short. It collects a credit, peaks if NIFTY pins the sold strike, and exposes a naked tail beyond it.

The back spread wants violence and accepts a low win rate for a huge payoff. The ratio spread wants a mild, steady drift toward the strike you sold and accepts a naked tail for a high win rate. Same legs, opposite ratio, opposite character.

:::fact
Look at the margins. Both ratio spreads block well over Rs 1.7 lakh, while the back spreads from chapters six and eleven blocked only about Rs 46,000 to Rs 52,000. The exchange is telling you in cash that the naked tail of a ratio spread is the more dangerous structure, even though it opens for a credit that feels like free money.
:::

:::tip
A ratio spread is a high-probability credit trade with a low-probability disaster attached. If you are still learning, prefer the defined-risk relatives: a bull call spread, a bear put spread, or the jade lizard you meet next, which keeps the credit while erasing one of the tails. Never hold a ratio spread through an event without a plan to cap the naked side, and size it so the worst case is survivable.
:::
