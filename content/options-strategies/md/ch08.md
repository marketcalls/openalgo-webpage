You have spent seven chapters leaning bullish. Now turn the telescope around. Picture NIFTY sitting at 24,056 and imagine you think it looks tired, that the next month leans lower, perhaps back toward 23,500. There are two pure single-leg ways to act on that view, and they sit on opposite sides of the very same trade. You can **buy a put**, paying a premium for the right to profit as NIFTY falls. Or you can **sell a call**, collecting a premium and betting that NIFTY does not climb. One of these is the cleanest, safest bearish trade a beginner can place. The other is among the most dangerous positions in this whole course. This chapter teaches both, side by side, and is blunt about which is which.

## The long put: paying for the right to a fall

A **long put** is the mirror image of the long call you met in chapter three. You buy the at-the-money 24,050 put expiring 28 July 2026, and you pay a premium for it. That premium gives you the right to sell NIFTY at 24,050 no matter how far it drops, so the contract grows more valuable the lower NIFTY goes.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 paid |

The premium works out to roughly Rs 295 a share, or **Rs 19,182** for one lot of 65. Because you paid that amount up front and can never be asked for a rupee more, the debit is also the **maximum loss**. You lose it in full only if NIFTY closes at or above 24,050 on expiry, where the put expires worthless. This is what makes the long put a **defined-risk** trade, and it is why the margin the exchange blocks is simply the premium itself, Rs 19,182, and nothing more.

:::key
A long put buys the right to profit from a fall. You pay a premium, and that premium is the most you can lose. Your profit grows as NIFTY drops below the strike, so the trade rewards a real decline while capping your downside at the price of entry.
:::

The **breakeven is 23,755**, which is the 24,050 strike minus the roughly 295 rupees a share you paid. NIFTY has to close below 23,755 for the put to put money in your pocket. The **probability of profit is 38 percent**, the market-implied odds that NIFTY finishes below that breakeven by 28 July. That figure is below half because you are paying for time value and asking the index to travel a fair distance down in about a month.

{{image: strat-long-put.png | The long put payoff: a flat loss of Rs 19,182 above the 24,050 strike, a breakeven at 23,755, and a profit line that climbs steeply as NIFTY falls, with the faint sigma bands showing the move the option market is pricing in. | chart}}

Walk the orange line from right to left. Above 24,050 the put is worthless, so you sit on the flat loss of Rs 19,182, the red zone. As NIFTY slips below 24,050 the put starts gaining intrinsic value and your loss shrinks. At the **breakeven of 23,755** the line crosses zero and you move into the green. From there it keeps climbing the further NIFTY falls. The metric panel reports the **maximum profit as Rs 1,544,068**, which is the theoretical gain if NIFTY collapsed all the way to zero. That will never happen, so treat that number as a label for "very large on a genuine decline" rather than a target. The honest takeaway is that a long put pays handsomely on a real fall and costs you only the premium if you are wrong.

:::fact
The reward to risk on the long put reads 1:80. That looks spectacular, but remember it pairs a tiny, certain risk against an enormous, very unlikely reward. The 38 percent probability of profit is the number that keeps you honest: most of the time NIFTY does not crash, and the put quietly decays.
:::

## The short call: collecting rent against a rise

Now the other side. Instead of buying a put to profit from a fall, you can **sell a call** to profit from the absence of a rise. You sell the 24,050 call and collect its premium the moment you open the trade.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 received |

You take in about **Rs 27,677** for the lot, and that credit is the most you can ever make. You keep all of it if NIFTY closes at or below 24,050, where the call you sold expires worthless. The **breakeven is 24,476**, the strike plus the roughly 426 rupees a share you collected, and the **probability of profit is a comfortable 69 percent**, because NIFTY simply has to avoid rising past that breakeven.

That high win rate is the seduction. Here is the catch that the credit hides.

:::warn
A short call has a maximum profit of Rs 27,677 and a maximum loss that is UNLIMITED. You have sold an uncovered call, so a sharp rally in NIFTY costs you rupee for rupee above the breakeven with no ceiling at all. The exchange knows this, which is why it blocks a margin of about Rs 1.78 lakh, nearly ten times the credit you collected. This is a naked short, and a single gap up can erase many winning trades in one morning. A beginner should understand it mainly to respect it.
:::

## Reading the short call chart

{{image: strat-short-call.png | The short call payoff: a flat profit of Rs 27,677 below the 24,050 strike, a breakeven at 24,476, and a loss line that falls without limit as NIFTY rises, the exact upside-down image of a long call. | chart}}

This is a long call flipped upside down. Below 24,050 you sit on your flat maximum profit of Rs 27,677, the green plateau. As NIFTY rises past the strike the sold call comes alive against you and your profit shrinks. At the **breakeven of 24,476** the orange line crosses zero, and above it the line plunges into the red and keeps falling forever. There is no floor. The blue dashed today line sits below the orange expiry line on the profit side, because the time value you sold has yet to decay in your favour. Every calm day pulls the position toward that peak, which is the appeal, but the uncapped tail is always there.

## The nine numbers side by side

| Number | Long Put | Short Call |
|---|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 1,544,068 | Rs 27,677 |
| Max loss | Rs 19,182 | Unlimited |
| Breakeven | 23,755 | 24,476 |
| Probability of profit | 38 percent | 69 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1:80 | NA |
| Net credit or debit | debit Rs 19,182 | credit Rs 27,677 |
| Margin required | Rs 19,182 | Rs 177,541 |
| Total P&L at entry | about zero | about zero |

Read the two columns against each other and the trade-off jumps out. The long put has a low probability of profit but a known, modest cost and a vast upside if NIFTY truly falls. The short call has a high probability of profit but a capped reward and a loss that can run away without limit, demanding a large margin to hold. One risks a little to chase a lot. The other collects a little while exposing a lot. That is the buyer versus seller bargain, drawn in its starkest form.

:::info
The **estimated premium** for each of these single-leg trades equals its one premium, since there is only one leg: Rs 19,182 of turnover for the put and Rs 27,677 for the call. The **total P&L** sits at about zero the instant you enter either trade, because you have just paid or received fair value. Both numbers only start to move as NIFTY does.
:::

## Which one a beginner should reach for

If your honest view is that NIFTY falls, reach for the **long put**. Your worst case is printed on the ticket before you place the order, Rs 19,182 and not a rupee more, and the margin equals that same small figure. You can be wrong, lose the premium, and walk away with your account intact. That is exactly the kind of risk a learner should be taking.

The **short call** is a professional's tool, not a beginner's. The 69 percent win rate flatters it, but it pairs a small, capped reward against an unlimited, uncapped loss and a heavy margin block. The way experienced traders tame it is to buy a cheaper, higher call as protection, which converts the naked short into the defined-risk **bear call spread**, the subject of the very next chapter. That single bought wing turns "unlimited" into a known number.

:::tip
When you want to be bearish and you are still learning, buy the put rather than sell the call. You give up the high probability of profit, but you gain a fixed, survivable worst case. The trader who is still in the game next year is almost always the one who refused to hold uncapped risk while learning.
:::
