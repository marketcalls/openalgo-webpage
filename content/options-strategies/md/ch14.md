In the last chapter you were the buyer, paying a rich premium and praying for a big move. Now stand on the other side of that very trade. Someone has to sell the straddle buyer their call and their put, collect that fat premium, and bet the opposite way: that NIFTY goes nowhere. That seller is you in this chapter. The **short straddle** and the **short strangle** are the income trades of the calm market. You take in the premium up front, and every quiet day pulls the position toward profit. The shape is seductive and the win rate is high. The danger is that you have inherited the buyer's unlimited reward as your own unlimited loss, and that changes everything.

## The short straddle: selling both sides at the money

A **short straddle** is the exact inverse of the long straddle. You sell the at-the-money call and the at-the-money put on the same 24,050 strike expiring 28 July 2026, and you collect both premiums at once.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 received |
| 2 | Sell | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 received |

Add the two premiums and you take in a **net credit of Rs 46,858** for one lot, money that lands in your account the moment you open the trade. That credit is your **maximum profit**, kept in full only if NIFTY finishes exactly on 24,050 at expiry, where both options you sold expire worthless and you owe nothing. The **breakevens are 23,329 and 24,771**, the same two marks as the long straddle, because you have simply taken the other side. NIFTY can wander anywhere inside that 1,442-point band and you still finish in the black. The **probability of profit is 58 percent**, comfortably above half, because most months NIFTY does not travel 721 points either way.

That high win rate is the seduction. Here is the truth the credit hides.

:::warn
A short straddle has a maximum profit of Rs 46,858 and a maximum loss that is UNLIMITED. You have sold a naked call, so a strong rally costs you rupee for rupee above 24,771 with no ceiling at all, and you have sold a naked put, so a crash costs you all the way down to 23,329 and far below. This is two uncovered short legs stacked on the same strike. The exchange blocks a margin of about Rs 1.62 lakh precisely because the risk is open ended. A single gap, up or down, can erase many months of quiet credits in one morning.
:::

## Reading the short straddle chart

{{image: strat-short-straddle.png | The short straddle payoff on NIFTY: an inverted V peaking at the full Rs 46,858 credit right at 24,050, breakevens at 23,329 and 24,771, and loss lines that fall without limit on either side as the index moves away from the strike. | chart}}

The orange at-expiry line is the long straddle's V turned upside down. Its peak sits at the 24,050 strike, on the full credit of Rs 46,858, which is the most you can ever make. Walk outward in either direction and the profit shrinks, crossing zero at the breakevens of **23,329 and 24,771**, the amber dots. Beyond those marks the line plunges into the red and keeps falling, with no floor on either side. The pink spot line at 24,056 sits almost on the peak, which is the appeal: you have entered at the very top of the at-expiry curve, and you only need NIFTY to stay near home.

The blue dashed T+0 line tells you how you get paid. Near the centre it sits below the orange peak, because the options you sold still hold the time value that has not yet decayed in your favour. Every calm day, that blue line lifts toward the orange peak as the time value you collected melts. **Time decay, the enemy of the straddle buyer, is now your ally.** The whole trade is a wager that decay arrives faster than any move that hurts you.

## The short strangle: a wider safe zone

The short straddle pays the most but its breakevens are uncomfortably close to spot. The **short strangle** buys you a wider margin for error by selling out-of-the-money options instead. You sell the 23,950 put and the 24,150 call, leaving a cushion on each side before either leg comes alive.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 23,950 | Put | out-of-the-money |
| 2 | Sell | 24,150 | Call | out-of-the-money |

Because both legs are out of the money, each fetches less premium, so the **net credit falls to Rs 40,729** for the lot, about Rs 6,000 less than the straddle. In exchange you get a flat profit plateau in the middle rather than a single peak. The **breakevens are 23,323 and 24,777**, wider than the straddle's, and between 23,950 and 24,150 you keep the entire credit because both options expire worthless across that band. The **probability of profit is 59 percent**, a fraction higher than the straddle, because you have given NIFTY more room to roam before it touches you.

But the wider safe zone does not change the nature of the trade. The loss is still uncapped.

:::warn
A short strangle has a maximum profit of Rs 40,729 and a maximum loss that is still UNLIMITED. The wider breakevens make it feel safer, and the higher 59 percent probability of profit flatters it further, but both sold legs are naked. Above 24,777 the short call bleeds without limit, and below 23,323 the short put behaves like an obligation to buy NIFTY all the way down. The margin is about Rs 1.62 lakh for the same reason as the straddle. A higher win rate on a trade that can lose without bound is not safety, it is a slower fuse.
:::

{{image: strat-short-strangle.png | The short strangle payoff on NIFTY: a flat profit plateau of Rs 40,729 anywhere between 23,950 and 24,150, breakevens at 23,323 and 24,777, and unlimited loss once the index travels far beyond either wing. | chart}}

On the chart the inverted V has flattened into a tabletop. The orange line runs flat along the top across the gap between the two strikes, where the full Rs 40,729 is yours, then turns down outside the wings and falls away on both sides. The plateau is the comfort, the falling tails are the danger, and the tails never stop.

## The two trades side by side, and the risk that defines them

| Number | Short Straddle | Short Strangle |
|---|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 46,858 | Rs 40,729 |
| Max loss | Unlimited | Unlimited |
| Breakevens | 23,329 and 24,771 | 23,323 and 24,777 |
| Probability of profit | 58 percent | 59 percent |
| Net credit | Rs 46,858 | Rs 40,729 |
| Margin required | Rs 162,339 | Rs 162,195 |

Read the columns and the bargain is clear. The straddle collects the larger credit but with a tighter safe zone. The strangle collects less but gives you more room to be wrong. Both win most of the time, and both share the one feature that should make a beginner pause: the loss has no limit and the margin is large. You are collecting a few thousand rupees of premium while putting more than a lakh and a half of capital on the line, exposed to a tail that can take far more than you ever took in.

:::fact
The high probability of profit is exactly why these trades are dangerous to the unwary. A 58 or 59 percent win rate means you will collect the credit again and again, building quiet confidence, and the rare loss that finally arrives can be many times larger than every gain before it. The numbers reward you on most days and punish you savagely on the few that matter. That is the opposite of the defined-risk discipline this course keeps pressing.
:::

:::warn
If you ever do sell premium this way, never hold a naked short straddle or strangle without a plan to cap the loss. The professional fix is to buy a cheaper, further out-of-the-money call and put as insurance, which turns the unlimited tails into known numbers and converts the trade into an iron fly or an iron condor. Those are the defined-risk versions of this exact idea, and they are where the next two chapters go. A beginner should learn the short straddle and strangle mainly to respect them, not to place them naked.
:::

:::tip
The market-implied odds are computed for a single hold to expiry. Many sellers never reach expiry. They take the trade off once a large part of the credit has decayed, locking a partial profit and stepping out of the path of a late move, or they exit the moment NIFTY threatens a breakeven, accepting a small loss rather than waiting for an unlimited one. The exit plan matters more here than on any defined-risk trade in this course.
:::

You can rehearse both structures in **sandbox trading (analyzer mode in OpenAlgo)**, selling the call and the put and watching the inverted V of the straddle spread into the tabletop of the strangle as you push the strikes apart. Slide the modelled price toward a breakeven and watch the falling tail keep going, with no floor to catch it. Feel both halves of the trade at once: the quiet daily pull of decay in your favour, and the open cliff on either side that one violent session can send you over. That tension, steady income against an uncapped tail, is what every premium seller lives with.
