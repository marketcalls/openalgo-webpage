The long call from the last chapter had a problem. To buy the 24,050 call you had to pay about Rs 27,677, and NIFTY then had to climb all the way to 24,476 before you made a single rupee, with only about a one in three chance of getting there. That is a lot to pay, and a long way to travel, for a view that might only be mildly bullish. If you expect NIFTY to drift up toward 24,150 rather than rocket away, there is a smarter, cheaper way to back exactly that opinion. You sell a higher call against the one you bought, and let the market fund most of your bet. This is the **bull call spread**, the first true two-legged strategy every options trader should learn, and a clean example of defined risk. The numbers here use real NIFTY option data captured on 26 June 2026, spot 24,056, lot 65, expiry 28 July 2026.

## What a bull call spread is

A bull call spread has exactly two legs, both calls, on the same expiry.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 paid |
| 2 | Sell | 24,150 | Call | about Rs 24,053 received |

You **buy the 24,050 call**, the leg that profits as NIFTY rises. Then you **sell the 24,150 call** one strike higher, which brings premium straight back in. The cash you collect from the sold call pays down most of the cost of the call you bought. You started owing about Rs 27,677 for the lone call, but after the sold call refunds about Rs 24,053, your real outlay shrinks to a **net debit of about Rs 3,624** for one lot.

Because you paid that money upfront and can never be asked for more, the net debit is also the most you can ever lose. That is what makes the bull call spread a **defined-risk** trade. You have traded away the dream of unlimited upside in return for paying a fraction of the cost.

:::key
A bull call spread buys a lower-strike call and sells a higher-strike call on the same expiry. The sold call funds most of the bought call, so the position costs far less. That net debit, here Rs 3,624, is both your total cost and your maximum loss. You can never lose more than you put in.
:::

## The nine numbers

Here is the full panel the builder reports for this NIFTY bull call spread.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 2,876 |
| Max loss | Rs 3,624 |
| Breakeven | 24,106 |
| Probability of profit | about 47 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1 : 0.79 |
| Net debit | Rs 3,624 |
| Est. premium | Rs 51,730 |
| Total P&L now | about Rs 0 |
| Margin required | Rs 35,378 |

Read the key three one at a time.

- The **maximum loss is Rs 3,624**, the net debit you paid. You suffer it if NIFTY closes at or below 24,050, where both calls expire worthless and your whole outlay is gone. On the chart this is the flat floor on the left.
- The **breakeven is 24,106**. NIFTY must close above this for the trade to make money. It sits a little above the bought strike of 24,050, because the index first has to rise enough to earn back the net debit before you cross into profit.
- The **maximum profit is Rs 2,876**, reached once NIFTY closes at or above 24,150. Above that strike the sold call cancels any further gains from the bought call, so your profit stops climbing. On the chart this is the flat ceiling on the right.

Notice that the breakeven of 24,106 is far closer to spot than the lone call's 24,476, and the cost has dropped from Rs 27,677 to Rs 3,624. You profit on a much smaller move, which is the whole point.

:::fact
The gap between the strikes is 100 points, worth Rs 6,500 for a lot of 65. You paid Rs 3,624 of that as the net debit, so the most you can keep is the rest, Rs 2,876. The two numbers, max profit Rs 2,876 and max loss Rs 3,624, always add up to the full strike width of Rs 6,500. The spread simply splits that width between you and the market.
:::

## Reading the chart

{{image: strat-bull-call-spread.png | The NIFTY bull call spread, buying the 24,050 call and selling the 24,150 call. The orange line rests on a flat loss floor of Rs 3,624 below 24,050, crosses zero at the breakeven of 24,106, and flattens onto a capped profit of Rs 2,876 above 24,150. | chart}}

Walk the orange at-expiry line across the chart. Below 24,050 both calls are worthless and you sit on your maximum loss of Rs 3,624, the flat floor. As NIFTY climbs past 24,050 the bought call gains intrinsic value, so the loss shrinks. At the **breakeven of 24,106**, marked by the amber dot, the line crosses zero and you move into profit. From 24,106 up to 24,150 you are in the green, your gains growing with every point. At 24,150 the sold call comes alive and starts offsetting any further gains, so the line flattens onto its ceiling of Rs 2,876 and stays there no matter how high NIFTY runs.

The blue dashed line is the value today, the T+0 curve, rounder because time value is still in both options. As the days pass toward 28 July, that dashed line settles down onto the orange one. The pink line marks spot at 24,056, and the faint vertical bands are the sigma ranges the option market is pricing. The breakeven of 24,106 sits just inside the plus one sigma band, which is why the probability of profit is a fairly even 47 percent, far healthier than the lone call's 31 percent.

## Three ways expiry can play out

There are only three places NIFTY can finish relative to your two strikes on 28 July, and walking each one makes the chart shape obvious.

- **NIFTY closes at or below 24,050,** say at 23,900. Both the 24,050 call and the 24,150 call expire worthless. The net debit is gone and you lose the full Rs 3,624, your maximum loss. This is the bet not working.
- **NIFTY closes between 24,050 and 24,150,** say at 24,110. Your bought 24,050 call is worth 60 points of intrinsic value while the sold 24,150 call is still worthless. You collect that value, subtract the debit you paid, and the remainder is your profit. At 24,110 you are just past the 24,106 breakeven and barely in the green. This is the partial reward zone.
- **NIFTY closes at or above 24,150,** say at 24,300. Now both calls have intrinsic value. The 24,050 call is worth 250 points and the sold 24,150 call costs you back 150, leaving a net 100 points, the full strike width, worth Rs 6,500. Subtract the Rs 3,624 debit and you bank the maximum profit of Rs 2,876. Anything above 24,150 gives the same result, because from there the sold call grows exactly as fast as the bought one.

:::example
If NIFTY closes at 24,130 at expiry, your 24,050 call is worth 80 points, about Rs 5,200 for the lot, and the sold 24,150 call expires worthless. Subtract the Rs 3,624 you paid and you keep roughly Rs 1,576. You are past breakeven but well short of the maximum, exactly as the rising middle of the chart shows.
:::

## When to prefer it over a long call

The bull call spread is not always better than a plain bought call. It is better for one common view and worse for another.

- Choose the spread when you expect a **moderate move**, roughly to 24,150 and not far beyond. The sold call cuts your cost from about Rs 27,677 down to Rs 3,624, so you risk far less, your breakeven of 24,106 is much closer than the lone call's 24,476, and your odds rise from about 31 percent to about 47 percent. You profit sooner and on a smaller move.
- Stay with the lone call when you expect a **genuine surge**, a violent run well past 24,150. The sold call caps your upside at the maximum profit of Rs 2,876, so a large rally that the lone call would ride all the way up is wasted on the spread. You traded that open-ended upside away in exchange for a much lower cost.

In short, the bull call spread says, I am bullish but realistic. You give up the dream of unlimited gains, and in return you pay less, risk less, and reach profit on a smaller move. For a beginner backing a mild view, that is usually the wiser trade.

:::tip
Pick your sold strike at the price you genuinely expect NIFTY to reach, not higher in hope. The maximum profit sits at that strike, here 24,150. Selling a strike you do not expect NIFTY to touch just throws away premium and raises your cost for no benefit, while selling one too close caps your gain before the move is done.
:::

## What it costs and how to size it

Every benefit has a price, and for the bull call spread the price is your capped upside. You will never catch the full run of a runaway rally, because the sold 24,150 call gives back everything the bought call earns above that strike. You accepted that cap deliberately, in exchange for paying less and breaking even sooner.

On sizing, the comfort of defined risk is that the worst case is printed in advance. One lot risks Rs 3,624. Trade two lots and you risk Rs 7,248, and so on. Decide first how much you are willing to lose if NIFTY simply sits still or falls, then count back to how many lots that allows. Always size to the maximum loss, never to the maximum profit, because the loss is the number that actually tests your account.

One quirk worth noting. The margin the builder blocks here is about **Rs 35,378**, larger than the Rs 3,624 you actually risk. That is because the exchange margin system charges an exposure amount on the sold call leg even though the bought call fully caps it. The cash you can truly lose is still only the net debit, but be ready for the larger sum to be set aside while the position is open.

:::warn
Defined risk does not mean small risk. Rs 3,624 per lot is real money, and you lose all of it if NIFTY closes at or below 24,050 at expiry, which is entirely possible with the index sitting near 24,056 today. Size the position so that losing the full net debit, and having the larger margin tied up meanwhile, is something your account can absorb without strain.
:::
