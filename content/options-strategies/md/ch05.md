Here is a different way to be bullish. Instead of paying money upfront and hoping NIFTY climbs enough to pay you back, what if you got paid first, and simply kept the cash as long as the index did not fall? That is the appeal of a **credit** strategy. You collect premium on day one, and time works for you rather than against you. The **bull put spread** is the first credit strategy in this course, and it is the mirror image of the bull call spread you just built. Same bullish view, opposite construction. You receive money rather than spend it, and you win by NIFTY simply not falling. The numbers use real NIFTY option data captured on 26 June 2026, spot 24,056, lot 65, expiry 28 July 2026.

## What a bull put spread is

A bull put spread has two legs, both puts, on the same expiry.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 received |
| 2 | Buy | 23,950 | Put | about Rs 16,676 paid |

You **sell the 24,050 put**, which brings in the larger premium, and you **buy the 23,950 put** one strike lower as protection. The sold put is the engine of the trade, collecting rent while NIFTY holds up. The bought put is your safety net, capping how much the position can lose if NIFTY does fall. The sold leg brings in more than the bought leg costs, so you keep the difference as a **net credit of about Rs 2,506** the moment you open the trade.

That credit is the most you can ever make. You keep all of it if NIFTY closes at or above 24,050 on expiry, where both puts expire worthless. Because the bought 23,950 put caps the damage on the downside, your loss can never run away. This is a **defined-risk** credit trade, and that bought wing is exactly what separates it from the naked short put of chapter three.

:::key
A bull put spread sells a higher-strike put and buys a lower-strike put on the same expiry. You collect a net credit, here Rs 2,506, which is your maximum profit. The bought put caps your loss, so unlike a naked short put your worst case is a known, finite number printed on the chart.
:::

## The nine numbers

Here is the full panel for this NIFTY bull put spread.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 2,506 |
| Max loss | Rs 3,994 |
| Breakeven | 24,011 |
| Probability of profit | about 51 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1 : 0.63 |
| Net credit | Rs 2,506 |
| Est. premium | Rs 35,857 |
| Total P&L now | about Rs 0 |
| Margin required | Rs 35,358 |

Read the key three.

- The **maximum profit is Rs 2,506**, the net credit you collected. You keep it in full if NIFTY closes at or above 24,050, where both puts expire worthless. On the chart this is the flat ceiling on the right.
- The **breakeven is 24,011**. This is the sold strike of 24,050 less the credit you banked per unit. As long as NIFTY closes above 24,011 you finish in profit. Notice it sits below today's spot of 24,056, so NIFTY can even drift down a little and you still win.
- The **maximum loss is Rs 3,994**, suffered if NIFTY closes at or below 23,950, where both puts are in the money and the spread is fully against you. On the chart this is the flat floor on the left.

The maximum profit of Rs 2,506 and maximum loss of Rs 3,994 once again add up to the full strike width, Rs 6,500 for the 100-point gap. The credit you took in is your slice, and the rest is what the market kept.

:::fact
The strike width is 100 points, worth Rs 6,500 for a lot of 65. You collected Rs 2,506 of that as credit, so the most the spread can take from you is the remaining Rs 3,994. Credit plus maximum loss always equals the full width. Knowing this, you can read a credit spread's worst case straight off its width and its credit, before you ever place it.
:::

## Reading the chart

{{image: strat-bull-put-spread.png | The NIFTY bull put spread, selling the 24,050 put and buying the 23,950 put. The orange line rests on a flat loss floor of Rs 3,994 below 23,950, crosses zero at the breakeven of 24,011, and flattens onto its maximum profit of Rs 2,506 above 24,050. | chart}}

Walk the orange at-expiry line from left to right. Below 23,950 both puts are deep in the money, the spread is fully against you, and you sit on your maximum loss of Rs 3,994, the flat floor. As NIFTY rises through 23,950 the bought put stops paying and your loss shrinks, until at the **breakeven of 24,011**, marked by the amber dot, the line crosses zero. From there up to 24,050 you are in the green, and at 24,050 and above both puts expire worthless, so the line flattens onto its ceiling of Rs 2,506. Anywhere at or above 24,050 you keep the entire credit.

The blue dashed line is the value today, the T+0 curve. For a credit trade it sits below the orange expiry line over the safe zone, which is the visual signal that time decay is on your side. Every quiet day, the options you sold lose a little value and that loss is your gain, so the dashed line rises toward the orange one as expiry nears. The pink line marks spot at 24,056, comfortably above the breakeven, which is why the probability of profit is a slight majority at about 51 percent.

:::info
Time decay, the long call buyer's enemy in chapter three, is now your friend. As a net seller you are paid by the clock. The blue T+0 line sits below the orange expiry line across the safe zone, so the position quietly improves toward the full credit as the days pass, provided NIFTY holds above your breakeven. Sellers are paid to wait.
:::

## Three ways expiry can play out

There are three places NIFTY can finish relative to your two strikes on 28 July.

- **NIFTY closes at or above 24,050,** say at 24,200. Both puts expire worthless. Nothing is owed, you keep the full credit, and you bank the maximum profit of Rs 2,506. This is the bet working, and it does not even need NIFTY to rise, only to hold.
- **NIFTY closes between 23,950 and 24,050,** say at 24,000. The sold 24,050 put is worth 50 points against you while the bought 23,950 put is still worthless. That cost eats into your credit. At 24,000 you are just below the 24,011 breakeven, so you are in a small loss. This is the partial zone where the credit is only partly kept.
- **NIFTY closes at or below 23,950,** say at 23,800. Both puts are in the money. The sold put costs you 250 points and the bought put refunds 150, leaving a net 100 points against you, the full width worth Rs 6,500. Subtract the Rs 2,506 credit you keep and you lose the maximum of Rs 3,994. Anything below 23,950 gives the same capped result, because the bought put matches the sold one rupee for rupee from there down.

:::example
If NIFTY closes at 24,030 at expiry, your sold 24,050 put is worth 20 points against you, about Rs 1,300 for the lot, and the bought 23,950 put expires worthless. You keep the Rs 2,506 credit and pay back Rs 1,300, netting roughly Rs 1,206. You are above the 24,011 breakeven and in modest profit, exactly as the chart shows just left of the ceiling.
:::

## Credit or debit, two roads to the same view

You now hold two bullish defined-risk spreads, and it is worth setting them side by side, because they answer the same opinion from opposite directions.

| | Bull call spread | Bull put spread |
|---|---|---|
| Construction | pay a debit | collect a credit |
| Net cash | debit Rs 3,624 | credit Rs 2,506 |
| Max profit | Rs 2,876 | Rs 2,506 |
| Max loss | Rs 3,624 | Rs 3,994 |
| Breakeven | 24,106 | 24,011 |
| Prob. of profit | about 47 percent | about 51 percent |

- The **bull call spread** needs NIFTY to actually rise above 24,106 to pay. It profits from a move up.
- The **bull put spread** only needs NIFTY to hold above 24,011, which is below today's spot. It profits from a move up, a flat market, and even a small drift down. That wider safe zone is why its probability of profit is a touch higher, about 51 percent against 47 percent.

The trade-off is that the bull put spread's worst case, Rs 3,994, is a little larger than its best case, Rs 2,506, so you are risking slightly more than you can make. That is normal for high-odds credit trades. You accept a less flattering risk to reward in exchange for winning more often. Neither spread is better in the abstract. Pick the credit version when you mainly expect NIFTY not to fall, and the debit version when you expect it to genuinely climb.

:::tip
A clean rule of thumb. If your view is NIFTY will hold up or rise, the bull put spread pays you to wait and wins on a flat tape. If your view is NIFTY will rise by a clear amount, the bull call spread rewards that move more directly. Let the shade of your opinion, hold versus climb, choose between them.
:::

On sizing, treat the maximum loss of Rs 3,994 as the number that counts, not the credit. The margin the builder blocks is about Rs 35,358, larger than the Rs 3,994 you can lose, because the exchange charges exposure on the sold put even though the bought put caps it. Plan for that cash to be set aside while the trade is open, and never size to the credit you hope to keep.
