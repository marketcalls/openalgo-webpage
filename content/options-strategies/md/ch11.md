The two bearish spreads you have just built were polite, defined-risk trades with capped rewards. This chapter steps up to three bolder bearish shapes that each chase a much larger payoff on a fall, and two of them carry uncapped risk to the upside that you must respect. You will meet the **put ratio back spread**, which costs little and pays enormously on a crash. Then the **short synthetic future**, where a call and a put combine into something that behaves exactly like selling a NIFTY future. And finally the **risk reversal**, a bearish collar that opens for a credit. Each is built from legs you already know, and each teaches a different way to lean hard on the downside.

## The put ratio back spread

A ratio back spread deliberately breaks the one-for-one balance of a normal spread. Here you sell one at-the-money put and buy two puts a little lower, so you end up net long puts, which means you profit powerfully if NIFTY falls hard.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 received |
| 2 | Buy | 23,950 | Put (x2) | about Rs 33,352 paid |

You sell one 24,050 put for about Rs 19,182 and use that money to help buy two 23,950 puts for about Rs 33,352. The net result is a small **net debit of about Rs 14,170** for the lot. Because you own one more put than you sold, a deep fall makes that extra put pay without limit on the downside, all the way to the theoretical **maximum profit of Rs 1,536,080** if NIFTY collapsed toward zero. Treat that figure as a label for "very large on a crash."

The risk hides in the middle. Your worst case is not on the upside but at the lower strike. If NIFTY closes exactly at 23,950, the put you sold is 100 points in the money against you while both puts you bought expire worthless, and you also lose the debit. That **maximum loss is Rs 20,670**, the deepest point of a small valley. Above 24,050 all three puts expire worthless and you simply lose the Rs 14,170 debit. The **breakeven is 23,632**, well below the valley, where the second long put has finally earned back everything.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 1,536,080 |
| Max loss | Rs 20,670 |
| Breakeven | 23,632 |
| Probability of profit | 32 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1:74 |
| Net debit | Rs 14,170 |
| Margin required | Rs 46,304 |

{{image: strat-put-ratio-back-spread.png | The NIFTY put ratio back spread: a shallow loss valley bottoming at Rs 20,670 around 23,950, a breakeven at 23,632, and a profit line that accelerates steeply as NIFTY falls because you hold one extra long put. | chart}}

:::key
A put ratio back spread sells one put and buys two lower puts, leaving you net long a put. It costs a small debit, takes its worst loss if NIFTY drifts only mildly down to the short strike, and pays without limit on a genuine collapse. It is a cheap, high-conviction bet on a hard fall, not a mild one.
:::

The 32 percent probability of profit is the honest catch. This trade rewards a big, fast decline. A small slide to 23,950 is actually the worst place NIFTY can land, which feels backwards until you see the valley on the chart. Reach for it only when you expect a real break lower, not a gentle drift.

## The short synthetic future

The next shape copies a futures position out of two options. Sell the at-the-money call and buy the at-the-money put at the same 24,050 strike, and the pair behaves exactly like a short NIFTY future: a single straight diagonal line of profit and loss.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 received |
| 2 | Buy | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 paid |

The call you sell brings in more than the put you buy costs, so the position opens for a small **net credit of about Rs 8,496**. From there it is a straight line. Every point NIFTY falls, you gain 65 rupees. Every point it rises, you lose 65 rupees. The **breakeven is 24,181**, the strike plus the credit per share, and that level is the **synthetic future** price, sitting just above spot because of the cost of carry baked into the options.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 1,571,746 |
| Max loss | Unlimited |
| Breakeven | 24,181 |
| Probability of profit | 56 percent |
| Risk to reward | NA |
| Net credit | Rs 8,496 |
| Margin required | Rs 169,046 |

{{image: strat-short-synthetic.png | The NIFTY short synthetic future: a single straight diagonal line crossing zero at 24,181, gaining as NIFTY falls toward a very large profit and losing without limit as NIFTY rises, identical to selling a future. | chart}}

:::warn
A short synthetic future has a maximum loss that is UNLIMITED. Because you sold a call with no protection above it, the position loses 65 rupees for every point NIFTY rises, with no ceiling. It is a leveraged short on the index, which is why the exchange blocks a margin of about Rs 1.69 lakh. The 56 percent probability of profit does not soften the uncapped tail. A sharp rally can run the loss far past anything you collected.
:::

The synthetic future is not a beginner's first trade. Its value is conceptual: it shows that a call and a put are two halves of the same instrument, and that selling a call while buying a put equals being short the future. Understanding that identity makes every later structure easier to read.

## The risk reversal

The last shape softens the synthetic into a bearish collar. You buy a put below the money for protection and sell a call above the money to pay for it, leaving a gentle slope in between.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 23,950 | Put | about Rs 16,676 paid |
| 2 | Sell | 24,150 | Call | about Rs 24,053 received |

The call you sell brings in more than the put you buy costs, so the **risk reversal** opens for a **net credit of about Rs 7,378**. Below 23,950 the long put profits and the gain grows the further NIFTY falls, toward a very large maximum if it collapses. Between the strikes, from 23,950 to 24,150, you simply hold the credit, give or take. Above 24,150 the sold call turns against you and the loss runs without limit. The **breakeven is 24,264**, the call strike plus the credit per share.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 1,564,128 |
| Max loss | Unlimited |
| Breakeven | 24,264 |
| Probability of profit | 60 percent |
| Risk to reward | NA |
| Net credit | Rs 7,378 |
| Margin required | Rs 163,941 |

{{image: strat-risk-reversal.png | The NIFTY bearish risk reversal: a long put profit zone below 23,950, a roughly flat credit between the strikes, and an unlimited loss above the breakeven at 24,264 from the sold call. | chart}}

:::warn
A bearish risk reversal has a maximum loss that is UNLIMITED. The sold 24,150 call has no protection above it, so a rally past 24,264 costs you without limit and the exchange blocks about Rs 1.64 lakh of margin. The 60 percent probability of profit comes entirely from the wide gap before the loss begins, but that gap does not cap the tail. Treat it as a leveraged bearish position, not a safe income trade.
:::

## How the three compare

| Strategy | Net | Max loss | Probability of profit | Breakeven |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Put ratio back spread | debit Rs 14,170 | Rs 20,670 | 32 percent | 23,632 |
| Short synthetic future | credit Rs 8,496 | Unlimited | 56 percent | 24,181 |
| Risk reversal | credit Rs 7,378 | Unlimited | 60 percent | 24,264 |

The three share a bearish direction but split sharply on risk. The **put ratio back spread** is the only one with a defined, modest loss, Rs 20,670 at worst, which makes it the most beginner-friendly of the three despite its exotic shape. Its price is a low 32 percent chance of profit, because it needs a real fall to pay. The **short synthetic future** and the **risk reversal** both open for a credit and show a higher probability of profit, but both carry the uncapped upside loss of a sold call, and both demand a large margin to hold.

:::fact
Look at the margins. The defined-risk put ratio back spread blocks Rs 46,304, while the two undefined-risk trades each block over Rs 1.6 lakh. The exchange is telling you, in cash, exactly how much more dangerous an uncapped position is. When a structure ties up three to four times the margin, that is your signal to check where the unlimited tail lives.
:::

:::tip
If you are bearish and still learning, prefer the put ratio back spread from this trio, or better still the bear put spread from the last chapter. Both keep your worst case printed on the ticket. Leave the short synthetic and the risk reversal until you fully understand and can manage an uncapped short, and never hold one through an event without a plan to cap the upside.
:::
