The ratio spread you just met collected a generous credit but left one tail naked and uncapped. What if you could keep almost all of that credit and yet erase one of the tails completely, so that an entire direction simply cannot hurt you? That is the trick behind the **jade lizard** and its mirror, the **reverse jade lizard**. Each adds one cheap bought wing to a pair of sold options, arranged so that the credit you collect is larger than the only spread that can lose. When the credit is bigger than the spread, one whole side of the chart can never dip below zero. It is one of the most elegant ideas in this course, and once you see it you will never unsee it.

## The jade lizard

A **jade lizard** sells a put below the money for income, sells a call above the money for more income, and then buys a further call to cap the upside. You build it on the 28 July 2026 expiry with three legs.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 23,950 | Put | about Rs 16,676 received |
| 2 | Sell | 24,150 | Call | about Rs 24,053 received |
| 3 | Buy | 24,250 | Call | about Rs 20,550 paid |

The two sold options bring in about Rs 40,729, and the bought 24,250 call costs about Rs 20,550, leaving a **net credit of about Rs 20,179**. That credit is also your **maximum profit**, kept in full whenever NIFTY finishes between the sold strikes, from 23,950 to 24,150, where all three options expire worthless.

Now look at the upside. Legs 2 and 3 form a bear call spread that is just 100 points wide, from 24,150 to 24,250. The most that spread can ever lose is 100 points, about Rs 6,500. But you collected Rs 20,179 in credit, far more than Rs 6,500. So even if NIFTY rockets to the moon, the call spread caps out at a small loss while your credit more than covers it, and you still walk away with about Rs 13,679. That is why the jade lizard has **no upside risk** at all. The entire right side of the chart stays green.

The risk lives only on the downside, where the sold 23,950 put is naked. The single **breakeven is 23,640**, which is the put strike of 23,950 minus the roughly 310 rupees a share of credit. Below it, the loss deepens toward a very large figure if NIFTY truly collapses.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 20,179 |
| Max loss | Rs 1,536,571 |
| Breakeven | 23,640 (no upside risk) |
| Probability of profit | 68 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1:0.01 |
| Net credit | Rs 20,179 |
| Margin required | Rs 170,750 |

Once NIFTY breaks below the breakeven of 23,640 you lose 65 rupees for every point it keeps falling, which is why the exchange blocks about Rs 1.71 lakh of margin. The signature of a jade lizard is a credit that is larger than the width of its call spread. When that is true, the upside cannot lose: the call spread caps your loss above, and the credit you banked is bigger than that cap. You are left with risk on one side only, the side you actually have a view on.
:::

## The reverse jade lizard

The **reverse jade lizard** simply flips the structure to erase the downside instead. You sell a call above the money, sell a put below the money, and buy a further put to cap the downside.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 24,150 | Call | about Rs 24,053 received |
| 2 | Sell | 23,950 | Put | about Rs 16,676 received |
| 3 | Buy | 23,850 | Put | about Rs 14,563 paid |

The two sold options bring in about Rs 40,729, the bought 23,850 put costs about Rs 14,563, and the position opens for a **net credit of about Rs 26,166**, which is also the maximum profit kept between 23,950 and 24,150.

This time legs 2 and 3 form a bull put spread just 100 points wide, from 23,950 to 23,850, whose worst loss is about Rs 6,500. Your credit of Rs 26,166 dwarfs that, so a crash to the downside caps out at a small loss the credit easily covers, leaving you about Rs 19,666 in pocket. That is why the reverse jade lizard has **no downside risk**. The naked side is now the upside, the sold 24,150 call. The single **breakeven is 24,553**, the call strike plus the roughly 403 rupees a share of credit, and above it the loss runs without limit.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 26,166 |
| Max loss | Unlimited |
| Breakeven | 24,553 (no downside risk) |
| Probability of profit | 72 percent |
| Risk to reward | NA |
| Net credit | Rs 26,166 |
| Margin required | Rs 176,758 |

A reverse jade lizard has a maximum loss that is UNLIMITED. The sold 24,150 call has no protection above it, so once NIFTY climbs past the breakeven of 24,553 you lose 65 rupees a point with no ceiling, which is why the exchange blocks about Rs 1.77 lakh of margin. The downside is fully covered, but the bearish-to-neutral lean here comes with a genuine uncapped tail on a rally. Have a plan to defend the call before you place it.
:::

## Why the credit erases a tail

Both structures rest on a single piece of arithmetic that is worth saying plainly. On the protected side you hold a spread that is 100 points wide, and the most a 100 point spread can lose is about Rs 6,500. If the credit you collected is bigger than Rs 6,500, then even the worst case on that side leaves you in profit. The credit absorbs the entire spread loss and still has money left over.

That is the whole secret. You are not buying expensive far protection. You are using the premium from one rich sold option to more than pay for the small spread on the other side. The result is a payoff with risk on one direction only.

:::fact
The jade lizard is bullish to neutral: it makes its money when NIFTY holds up or drifts higher, with the right side bulletproof and only a fall to defend. The reverse jade lizard is bearish to neutral: it profits when NIFTY softens or holds, with the left side bulletproof and only a rally to defend. Choose the one whose naked tail points away from your real view, so the side you fear is the side that cannot hurt you.
:::

## Which one a beginner should reach for

Both jade lizards are smarter than a plain naked short option, because each removes one tail entirely and turns a two-sided worry into a one-sided one. That alone makes them worth understanding. But neither is fully defined-risk: the surviving tail is still naked, still large, and still demands about Rs 1.7 lakh of margin.

- Reach for the **jade lizard** when you lean mildly bullish and want the upside taken off the table, keeping only a downside to manage.
- Reach for the **reverse jade lizard** when you lean mildly bearish and want the downside taken off the table, keeping only an upside to manage.

:::tip
If you are still learning, treat a jade lizard as the next step up from defined-risk spreads, not your first credit trade. Place it only when you have a clear plan to defend or close the naked side, and size it so that one bad gap on that side cannot wreck the account. The elegance of erasing a tail is real, but the tail that remains is the one that bites.
:::
