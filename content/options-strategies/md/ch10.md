There are two ways to build a defined-risk bearish spread, just as there were two ways to build a bullish one. The last chapter sold calls for a credit and won if NIFTY stayed put or fell. This chapter does the opposite construction. The **bear put spread** is built from puts, paid for as a debit, and it is the natural choice when you actively expect NIFTY to drop rather than merely fail to rise. It is the bearish twin of the bull call spread you met early in the course, flipped to the downside. You buy a put to profit from a fall, then sell a lower put to cut the cost, accepting a capped reward in exchange for a cheaper, defined ticket.

## What a bear put spread is

A bear put spread has two legs, both puts, on the same expiry. You buy the higher strike and sell the lower one.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 paid |
| 2 | Sell | 23,950 | Put | about Rs 16,676 received |

You **buy the 24,050 put**, the leg that profits as NIFTY falls. Then you **sell the 23,950 put** a hundred points lower, which brings in premium and pays down most of the cost of the put you bought. A lone 24,050 put would have cost Rs 19,182. The sold put cuts that to a **net debit** of about **Rs 2,506** for one lot. Because you paid that debit up front and can never owe more, it is also your **maximum loss**.

The most you can make is the gap between the strikes, 100 points worth Rs 6,500 for the lot, minus the Rs 2,506 you paid, which leaves a **maximum profit of Rs 3,994**. You reach it if NIFTY closes at or below 23,950, where both puts are fully in the money and the spread is worth its entire width. As always with a vertical spread, the maximum profit and the maximum loss add up to the full strike width.

:::key
A bear put spread buys a higher-strike put and sells a lower-strike put on the same expiry. The sold put funds most of the bought put, so the trade costs less than a lone put. That net debit is both your cost and your maximum loss, and your reward is capped at the strike width minus the debit.
:::

## The nine numbers

Here is the full metric panel for the NIFTY bear put spread, built from live data captured on 26 June 2026, with spot 24,056 and expiry 28 July 2026.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 3,994 |
| Max loss | Rs 2,506 |
| Breakeven | 24,011 |
| Probability of profit | 49 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1:1.59 |
| Net debit | Rs 2,506 |
| Margin required | Rs 34,113 |
| Total P&L at entry | about zero |

Read them one at a time.

- The **maximum loss is Rs 2,506**, the net debit. You lose it in full if NIFTY closes at or above 24,050, where both puts expire worthless. On the chart this is the flat floor on the right.
- The **maximum profit is Rs 3,994**, reached if NIFTY closes at or below 23,950. Below that strike the sold put cancels any further gains from the bought put, so your profit stops climbing. This is the flat ceiling on the left.
- The **breakeven is 24,011**, the bought strike of 24,050 minus the roughly 38 rupees a share you paid. NIFTY has to fall below 24,011 for the trade to make money, only a little under the current spot.
- The **probability of profit is 49 percent**, almost a coin flip, and the **reward to risk reads 1:1.59**, so your capped reward of Rs 3,994 is comfortably larger than your capped risk of Rs 2,506. You are risking less than you stand to make.

:::fact
The breakeven sits at 24,011, just 45 points below the current spot of 24,056. That means NIFTY only has to slip a little for this trade to start working, unlike the lone long put whose breakeven of 23,755 demanded a 300-point fall. Selling the lower put pulled the breakeven much closer to the money, the quiet advantage of a spread over a single option.
:::

## Three ways expiry can play out

There are only three places NIFTY can finish relative to your two strikes on 28 July 2026.

- **NIFTY closes at or above 24,050,** say at 24,200. Both puts expire worthless. Your whole Rs 2,506 debit is gone. This is the bet failing, and it fails if NIFTY simply holds or rises.
- **NIFTY closes between 23,950 and 24,050,** say at 24,000. Your bought 24,050 put is worth 50 points of intrinsic value while the sold 23,950 put is still worthless. You collect that value, subtract the debit, and the result is a small profit just past the 24,011 breakeven. This is the middle ground.
- **NIFTY closes at or below 23,950,** say at 23,800. Both puts have intrinsic value. The 24,050 put is worth 250 points, the sold 23,950 put costs you back 150, leaving a net 100 points, the full width, worth Rs 6,500. Subtract the Rs 2,506 debit and you bank the maximum profit of Rs 3,994. Anything below 23,950 gives the same result.

:::example
If NIFTY closes at 24,000 at expiry, your 24,050 put is worth about Rs 3,250 for the lot, and the sold 23,950 put expires worthless. Subtract the Rs 2,506 you paid and you keep about Rs 744. You are just past breakeven and well short of the maximum, exactly as the middle of the chart shows.
:::

## Debit or credit, which bearish spread to choose

You now know two defined-risk bearish trades that profit in the same direction. The bear call spread from the last chapter takes in a Rs 3,624 credit and wins if NIFTY stays at or below 24,050, time decay working for it. The bear put spread here pays a Rs 2,506 debit and wins if NIFTY actively falls below 24,011, time decay working gently against it. They reach for different shades of the same view.

- Choose the **bear put spread** when you expect a genuine, fairly prompt decline. Its reward to risk of 1:1.59 is more generous than the bear call spread, and it pays you the most precisely when NIFTY drops to your lower strike. You want movement, and movement down is your friend.
- Choose the **bear call spread** when you think the upside is capped but you are not confident NIFTY will actually fall. It pays you simply for the index failing to rise, and the clock is on your side. You want stillness, not a crash.

Against a lone long put, the bear put spread is the better trade for a moderate fall. It cuts your cost from Rs 19,182 to Rs 2,506, pulls your breakeven from 23,755 up to 24,011, and starts paying on a much smaller move. You surrender the put's vast upside on a collapse in exchange for a far cheaper, closer-to-the-money bet. For a beginner backing a measured bearish view, that is usually the wiser trade.

:::tip
Set your sold strike at the level you genuinely expect NIFTY to reach on the downside, here 23,950. The maximum profit sits exactly at that strike. Selling a strike far below the money in hope of a crash collects little premium and leaves your debit high for almost no benefit.
:::

:::warn
Defined risk still means real risk. Rs 2,506 per lot is gone in full if NIFTY closes at or above 24,050 at expiry, which it can do by simply holding steady from today's 24,056. Size to that maximum loss, not to the Rs 3,994 reward, and place only as many lots as your account can absorb if the fall never comes.
:::
