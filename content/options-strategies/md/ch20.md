Every strategy you have met so far makes a bet on direction or on the size of a move. Calendars are different. They make a bet on time itself. A **calendar spread** sells an option in the near expiry and buys the same kind of option in a later expiry, and it earns its money from one simple fact: the near-month option loses its time value faster than the far-month one. If NIFTY sits quietly near the strike while the front leg melts away, the back leg you still own keeps most of its worth, and the gap between the two is your profit. This chapter builds three of these time trades on real NIFTY option data captured on 26 June 2026, spot 24,056, lot 65, near expiry 28 July 2026 and far expiry 25 August 2026.

## The idea: sell the near, own the far

Time value does not drain out of an option evenly. It drains slowly when expiry is far away, then faster and faster as the final days approach. A 28 July option and a 25 August option both hold time value today, but over the next month the July one loses almost all of it while the August one, with a fresh month still ahead, barely notices. A calendar harvests exactly that difference.

You **sell the near-month leg**, the 28 July option, and you **buy the far-month leg**, the 25 August option, at the same strike. Because the back-month option always costs more than the front-month one, you pay a small **net debit** to put the trade on. That debit is your stake.

Now look at what the chart draws. The orange "At Expiry" line is plotted at the **near expiry**, the moment your sold July leg dies. If NIFTY is sitting near the strike on 28 July, the front leg expires worthless and you keep its premium in full, while the back leg you own still has a whole month of life and therefore still holds a healthy slice of time value. The spread between a dead front leg and a living back leg is worth more than the debit you paid, and that surplus is your gain. Plot that gain across every possible closing price and you get a **tent**: a peak right at the strike, sloping down on both sides.

:::key
A calendar spread is long time. You collect the fast decay of the near-month option you sold and pay for the slow decay of the far-month option you own. It makes its most money when NIFTY finishes right at the strike on the near expiry, where the front leg dies worthless and the back leg keeps the most time value. Drift too far either way and both legs lose their time value together, the tent collapses, and you lose close to the debit you paid.
:::

## The call calendar

The plainest version uses two calls at the at-the-money strike.

| Leg | Action | Expiry | Strike | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell 1 | 28 Jul 2026 | 24,050 | Call |
| 2 | Buy 1 | 25 Aug 2026 | 24,050 | Call |

The far call costs more than the near call, so you pay a **net debit of about Rs 13,130** to open the trade. Here are the builder's nine numbers.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 14,480 |
| Max loss | Rs 13,090 |
| Breakevens | 23,495 / 24,634 |
| Probability of profit | about 47 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1 : 1.11 |
| Net debit | Rs 13,130 |
| Margin required | Rs 58,373 |
| Total P&L now | about Rs 0 |

The orange at-expiry line forms a tent peaking near the 24,050 strike, where the maximum profit of Rs 14,480 sits, and sloping down to the breakevens at 23,495 and 24,634. | chart}}

Read the tent. The peak, a profit of **Rs 14,480**, sits right at the 24,050 strike, because that is where your sold July call expires worthless while your August call still holds the most time value. As NIFTY moves away in either direction the gain shrinks, until the line crosses zero at the two **breakevens, 23,495 and 24,634**. Outside that band the tent keeps falling to its **maximum loss of about Rs 13,090**, which is essentially the debit you paid, because far from the strike both calls lose their time value and the spread between them collapses toward zero.

The maximum loss, Rs 13,090, is a touch less than the debit because the back-month call never quite reaches zero value at the near expiry. The risk to reward of 1 : 1.11 and the probability of profit of about 47 percent describe a balanced trade: you want NIFTY to stay roughly where it is for the next month.

{{image: strat-put-calendar.png | The NIFTY put calendar, selling the 28 July 24,050 put and buying the 25 August 24,050 put. The same tent shape peaks near the strike, but it costs only about Rs 5,818 and shows a stronger reward to risk of 1 : 1.93. | chart}}

The shape is the same tent, but the numbers are kinder. On the captured data the put calendar costs only a **net debit of about Rs 5,818**, far less than the call version, yet its **maximum profit of Rs 11,236** is nearly as large. That is why its **risk to reward is 1 : 1.93**, the best of the three: you risk under six thousand to make over eleven. The **breakevens, 23,570 and 24,548**, again form a tent around the strike, and the **maximum loss of Rs 5,817** is essentially the small debit you paid.

:::tip
When the call and put calendars target the same strike and expiry, their tents look identical, so let the numbers decide. On this NIFTY snapshot the put calendar is the cheaper, higher-reward route to the same shape, a debit of about Rs 5,818 for a peak of Rs 11,236, against the call calendar's Rs 13,130 for Rs 14,480. Building both in the strategy builder and comparing the debit against the reward to risk is the quickest way to pick the better one on the day.
:::

## The diagonal calendar

A **diagonal calendar** keeps the time element of a calendar but adds a second twist: the two legs sit at different strikes as well as different expiries. You still sell the near month and buy the far month, but the strike gap tilts and stretches the tent, giving it a directional lean instead of a symmetric peak.

| Leg | Action | Expiry | Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell 1 | 28 Jul 2026 | Call |
| 2 | Buy 1 | 25 Aug 2026 | Call |

Because the strikes differ, the debit is smaller than a plain call calendar, a **net debit of about Rs 8,791**, and the payoff is no longer balanced.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 14,881 |
| Max loss | Rs 15,161 |
| Breakevens | 23,382 / 24,593 |
| Probability of profit | about 50 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1 : 0.98 |
| Net debit | Rs 8,791 |
| Margin required | Rs 55,782 |
| Total P&L now | about Rs 0 |

{{image: strat-diagonal-calendar.png | The NIFTY diagonal calendar, selling the near-month call and buying the far-month call at a different strike. The tent is wider and leans to one side, with breakevens at 23,382 and 24,593 and a maximum profit of Rs 14,881. | chart}}

Read the diagonal as a calendar pulled to one side. Its **breakevens, 23,382 and 24,593**, sit wider apart than the plain call calendar's, so it tolerates a larger move before it stops making money, which is why its **probability of profit, about 50 percent**, is the highest of the three. The price for that wider tent is a slightly larger **maximum loss of Rs 15,161**, a touch more than the debit, because the strike gap means a sharp move can cost a little beyond what you paid. The **maximum profit of Rs 14,881** still arrives when NIFTY drifts gently toward the back-month strike at the near expiry.

:::info
The diagonal is the calendar's more flexible cousin. By choosing how far apart the two strikes sit, you decide how wide the tent is and which way it leans, so you can express a mild directional view as well as a view on time. It is still a defined-risk trade, with a worst case of about Rs 15,161 here, but the strike gap makes that worst case slightly larger than the simple debit, unlike the plain calendars.
:::

## Choosing and managing a calendar

Put the three side by side and the trade-offs are clear.

| Strategy | Debit | Max profit | Max loss | Reward to risk | POP |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Call calendar | Rs 13,130 | Rs 14,480 | Rs 13,090 | 1 : 1.11 | 47 percent |
| Put calendar | Rs 5,818 | Rs 11,236 | Rs 5,817 | 1 : 1.93 | 42 percent |
| Diagonal calendar | Rs 8,791 | Rs 14,881 | Rs 15,161 | 1 : 0.98 | 50 percent |

All three want the same thing: a calm NIFTY that lingers near the strike while the near-month leg decays away. They suit a market you expect to drift sideways for a few weeks, not one you expect to break out. Because the most you can lose is roughly the debit, they are a defined-risk way to sell time, with margins between about Rs 49,000 and Rs 58,000 rather than the lakhs a naked short would demand.

:::fact
Unlike a short straddle or strangle, a calendar has no unlimited tail. You own the back-month leg, and that long option caps your loss at roughly the debit you paid. This makes calendars one of the few ways a beginner can sell time decay without taking on open-ended risk. The cost of that safety is that you must also pay for the back-month option, so a calendar is a debit trade, not a credit one.
:::

Two cautions before you reach for one. First, a calendar is sensitive to implied volatility, because the back-month leg you own carries more of it than the front leg you sold, so a drop in volatility after you enter can shrink the tent even if NIFTY behaves. Second, the tent is real only near the strike, so a calendar is a poor choice just before a known event that could send NIFTY flying. Rehearse the shape in sandbox trading (analyzer mode in OpenAlgo) and watch the tent rise and fall as you nudge the strikes and expiries.

With calendars you have learned to trade time as well as direction. Next we return to the straight line, the synthetic future built from two options, and meet the income trade that sits on top of a holding: the covered call.
