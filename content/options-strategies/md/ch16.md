The iron condor pays you to keep NIFTY inside a range. This chapter tightens that range until it is a single point. If your view is not just that NIFTY will stay calm but that it will settle almost exactly where it sits now, near 24,050, you can build a structure that peaks sharply at that one strike and falls away on both sides. These are the **iron fly** and the **butterfly**, the pin-the-strike trades. They cost almost nothing or collect a rich credit, they are fully defined-risk, and they pay many times their cost if NIFTY lands on the nail. The honest catch, the same one that shadowed the directional butterflies, is that pinning a single strike is a low-odds event. This chapter shows three different ways to build the very same peak, and is clear about the slim chance of hitting it.

## The iron fly: a condor with its plateau collapsed

An **iron fly** is an iron condor whose two inner short strikes have been pulled together onto the same strike. Instead of selling a put and a call a little apart, you sell both at the money, then buy a wing on each side for protection. The OpenAlgo builder calls the credit version the **Long Iron Fly**.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 23,950 | Put | lower wing |
| 2 | Sell | 24,050 | Put | body |
| 3 | Sell | 24,050 | Call | body |
| 4 | Buy | 24,150 | Call | upper wing |

The two sold at-the-money options are a short straddle, and they collect a large premium, while the two bought wings cap each tail one hundred points out. The net is a **credit of Rs 6,130**, which is your **maximum profit**, kept in full only if NIFTY finishes exactly on 24,050 at expiry, where both sold options die together. Each wing is one hundred points wide, worth Rs 6,500 on a lot, and you collected Rs 6,130 of it, so the **maximum loss is only Rs 370**. The margin is about Rs 63,940. The **breakevens are 23,956 and 24,148**, a band barely 200 points wide, and the **probability of profit is just 9 percent**, because NIFTY has to settle almost dead centre. The reward to risk reads a dazzling **1 to 16.5**.

{{image: strat-long-iron-fly.png | The long iron fly on NIFTY: a sharp profit peak of Rs 6,130 at the 24,050 strike, narrow breakevens at 23,956 and 24,148, and a capped loss of just Rs 370 once the index drifts past either wing. | chart}}

The orange line is the condor's tabletop sharpened into a tent. It climbs to a single peak right at 24,050 and falls away steeply on both sides, flattening at the tiny Rs 370 loss past each wing. The pink spot line at 24,056 sits almost on the peak, which is the appeal and the trap at once. You are at the best point on the curve today, but the slightest drift in either direction walks you down the slope.

:::key
An iron fly is a short straddle fenced by two bought wings. It is the iron condor with no plateau, peaking at a single strike. It collects a rich credit because the sold legs are at the money, caps the loss at a small known amount with the wings, and pays the most only if NIFTY pins the body strike at expiry.
:::

## The short iron fly: the high-probability inverse

As with the condor, the builder offers the mirror image, the **Short Iron Fly**. You take the other side of all four legs, paying a **debit of Rs 6,130** instead of collecting it. Every number flips. The **maximum profit is Rs 370**, the **maximum loss is Rs 6,130**, and the **probability of profit jumps to 91 percent**, because NIFTY almost always drifts off the exact strike. The breakevens are the same 23,956 and 24,148, the margin about Rs 69,490, and the reward to risk a meagre 1 to 0.06.

{{image: strat-short-iron-fly.png | The short iron fly on NIFTY: the inverse of the long fly, a small Rs 370 profit almost anywhere except pinned at 24,050, but a Rs 6,130 loss if the index settles on the strike, with a 91 percent probability of profit. | chart}}

So the iron fly, like the condor, has two faces. The credit Long Iron Fly is a long-shot bet on a precise pin, with a tiny risk and a rich reward at 9 percent odds. The debit Short Iron Fly wins a small amount almost every time NIFTY moves at all, and loses the large Rs 6,130 only on the rare day it freezes on the strike. Same structure, opposite wager.

:::warn
The same caution from the iron condor applies here, and harder. The 91 percent probability of profit on the Short Iron Fly is seductive, but it wins only Rs 370 against a Rs 6,130 loss. One bad pin undoes sixteen wins. And do not be misled by the Long and Short labels: the Long Iron Fly is the credit version, the Short Iron Fly the debit version, which runs against intuition. Judge each by its nine numbers, never by its name.
:::

## The butterfly: the same peak from one option type

Here is the elegant part. You can build that identical sharp tent without selling any straddle at all, using a single option type. A **butterfly** uses three strikes and four legs of one kind, calls or puts, to peak at the middle strike. The **Call Butterfly** does it with calls.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 23,950 | Call | lower wing |
| 2 | Sell | 24,050 | Call (x2) | body |
| 3 | Buy | 24,150 | Call | upper wing |

You buy one call below, sell two at the body, and buy one above. The two sold calls bring in most of the premium and the two bought wings cost a little more, leaving a tiny **net debit of Rs 348** for the whole lot. That debit is your **maximum loss**, suffered if NIFTY finishes outside the wings where the whole structure is worth nothing. The **maximum profit is Rs 6,152**, reached only if NIFTY lands precisely on the 24,050 body, where the lower wing is worth its full hundred points against worthless sold calls and an idle upper wing. The **breakevens are 23,954 and 24,148**, the **probability of profit is 9 percent**, and the reward to risk is a brilliant **1 to 17.7**, on a margin of about Rs 64,000.

{{image: strat-call-butterfly.png | The call butterfly on NIFTY: built from calls alone, a sharp profit tent peaking at Rs 6,152 on the 24,050 body, breakevens at 23,954 and 24,148, and a tiny Rs 348 loss outside the wings. | chart}}

Lay this chart beside the long iron fly and they are almost the same picture: a sharp peak at 24,050, breakevens around 23,955 and 24,148, a huge reward against a tiny risk, and the same 9 percent odds. One was built from a credit straddle and two wings, the other from four calls for a small debit. Different legs, different cash flow, the same payoff.

## Two routes to one shape

The **Put Butterfly** reaches the identical peak using only puts, the mirror construction of the call version.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,150 | Put | upper wing |
| 2 | Sell | 24,050 | Put (x2) | body |
| 3 | Buy | 23,950 | Put | lower wing |

It costs a **net debit of Rs 361**, its **maximum loss**, and pays a **maximum profit of Rs 6,139** if NIFTY pins 24,050. The breakevens are 23,955 and 24,148, the probability of profit again 9 percent, the reward to risk 1 to 17, and the margin about Rs 63,877. Place the call butterfly and the put butterfly side by side and you cannot tell their payoffs apart.

{{image: strat-put-butterfly.png | The put butterfly on NIFTY: built from puts alone, the same peak of Rs 6,139 at 24,050, breakevens at 23,955 and 24,148, and a near-identical Rs 361 loss outside the wings, proving calls and puts are two routes to one shape. | chart}}

:::fact
The call butterfly costs Rs 348 and the put butterfly Rs 361, a few rupees apart, and both pin the same strike for the same reward and the same 9 percent odds. The tiny difference is just where each option sits relative to the money on the day you build it. The lesson is deeper than the numbers: a payoff shape is not tied to one option type. Calls and puts are two languages that describe the very same picture, and a skilled trader picks whichever legs are cheaper or more liquid to reach the shape they want.
:::

## Choosing among the pin-the-strike family

You now have three ways to build the same tent over 24,050: the credit iron fly, the call butterfly, and the put butterfly. They share a profit peak of roughly Rs 6,100 to Rs 6,150, a maximum loss of only a few hundred rupees, breakevens about 200 points wide, and a 9 percent probability of profit. The choice between them comes down to cash flow and liquidity, not outcome.

- Choose a **butterfly** when you want to pay a tiny known debit, Rs 348 or Rs 361, and you would rather work in a single option type.
- Choose the **credit iron fly** when you prefer to collect the premium up front and accept the same defined risk through the wings.
- Use the **short** versions only with clear eyes: a high probability of a small win, paired with a large loss on the rare pin, is the opposite trade and demands tight management.

:::tip
Place pin-the-strike trades close to expiry, not far from it. A fly or butterfly built weeks early gives NIFTY too much room to wander off the body strike. The same structure put on in the final days, when the sigma bands have shrunk and you have a strong read on where the index will settle, has a far better chance of landing on the nail. The peak is dazzling, but only if you collect it.
:::

:::info
As ever, the estimated premium across all four legs looks large, but it is not your risk. Your risk is the small net debit on a butterfly, Rs 348 or Rs 361, or the capped Rs 370 on the credit iron fly, because the bought wings fence every outcome. Read the maximum loss the builder reports, never the gross turnover.
:::

Build all three in **sandbox trading (analyzer mode in OpenAlgo)** and watch the same tent appear from a straddle plus wings, from four calls, and from four puts. Drag the modelled price a hundred points off the body and see how fast the peak collapses, a vivid lesson in how rarely a single strike is pinned and how richly it pays when it is. With the fly and butterfly the income and pin-the-strike families are complete, and from here the course turns to the ratio trades, where the legs are no longer balanced and a naked tail reappears.
