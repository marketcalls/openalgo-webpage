The last chapter left you holding a problem. The short call is a tempting bearish trade, with a 69 percent chance of profit, but it carries an unlimited loss and demands a Rs 1.78 lakh margin block. You wanted to be bearish without betting the house. The **bear call spread** is the answer. It keeps the sold call that pays you when NIFTY stays calm or falls, and it adds a second, cheaper call bought a little higher up that caps the disaster at a known number. You give back a slice of the credit in exchange for a hard ceiling on your loss. For a beginner who wants to collect premium on a bearish view, this is the trade that the naked short should have been all along.

## What a bear call spread is

A bear call spread has exactly two legs, both calls, on the same expiry. You sell the lower strike and buy the higher one.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 received |
| 2 | Buy | 24,150 | Call | about Rs 24,053 paid |

You **sell the 24,050 call**, the leg that pays you if NIFTY stays at or below the strike. Then you **buy the 24,150 call** a hundred points higher, which costs you premium but caps your risk. The call you sold brings in more than the call you bought costs, so the position opens with money in your pocket. That net is the **net credit**, and here it works out to about **Rs 3,624** for one lot.

Because you collected that credit and the bought call limits how far the sold call can hurt you, this is a **defined-risk** trade. The most you can make is the credit you took in. The most you can lose is the gap between the strikes, 100 points worth Rs 6,500 for the lot, minus the Rs 3,624 you collected, which leaves **Rs 2,876**. Those two numbers always add up to the full strike width.

:::key
A bear call spread sells a lower-strike call and buys a higher-strike call on the same expiry. You open it for a net credit, you keep that credit if NIFTY stays at or below the lower strike, and your loss is capped by the bought call. It is the defined-risk version of selling a call.
:::

## The nine numbers

Here is the full metric panel for the NIFTY bear call spread, built from live data captured on 26 June 2026, with spot 24,056 and expiry 28 July 2026.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 3,624 |
| Max loss | Rs 2,876 |
| Breakeven | 24,106 |
| Probability of profit | 53 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1:1.26 |
| Net credit | Rs 3,624 |
| Margin required | Rs 34,789 |
| Total P&L at entry | about zero |

Read them one at a time.

- The **maximum profit is Rs 3,624**, the net credit. You keep all of it if NIFTY closes at or below 24,050 at expiry, where both calls expire worthless and the credit is yours. On the chart this is the flat ceiling on the left.
- The **maximum loss is Rs 2,876**, suffered if NIFTY closes at or above 24,150, where the spread is worth its full 100-point width against you. This is the flat floor on the right, and it is the number that makes the trade safe to hold.
- The **breakeven is 24,106**, the sold strike of 24,050 plus the roughly 56 rupees a share of credit you collected. NIFTY can drift up to 24,106 and you still do not lose money.
- The **probability of profit is 53 percent**, a touch better than a coin flip, because NIFTY only has to avoid rising past 24,106. The **reward to risk reads 1:1.26**, meaning your capped reward is actually slightly larger than your capped risk, which is unusual and pleasant.

:::fact
Notice the margin. A naked short call blocked about Rs 1.78 lakh of your capital. This bear call spread, with the same bearish stance, blocks just Rs 34,789, because the bought 24,150 call defines the worst case for the exchange. The single protective wing cuts the margin to roughly a fifth and turns an unlimited loss into Rs 2,876. That is the whole point of the structure.
:::

Walk the orange at-expiry line from left to right. Below 24,050 both calls are worthless, and you sit on your maximum profit of Rs 3,624, the green ceiling. As NIFTY climbs past 24,050 the sold call starts costing you, so your profit shrinks. At the **breakeven of 24,106**, marked by the amber dot, the line crosses zero. From 24,106 up to 24,150 you are in the red loss zone, the loss deepening with every point. At 24,150 the bought call comes alive and stops the bleeding, so the line flattens onto its floor of Rs 2,876 and stays there no matter how high NIFTY runs.

The blue dashed line is the position today, the T+0 curve. It sits below the orange line on the profit side because the options still hold time value that you, as the net seller, have yet to collect. The pink vertical marks spot at 24,056, almost exactly at your sold strike, and the faint sigma bands show the one and two standard deviation move the option market is pricing into the next 32 days. As expiry nears, the blue line settles down onto the orange one and your credit ripens.

## Three ways expiry can play out

There are only three places NIFTY can finish relative to your two strikes on 28 July 2026.

- **NIFTY closes at or below 24,050,** say at 23,800. Both calls expire worthless. You keep the full Rs 3,624 credit. This is the bet working, and notice it works even if NIFTY merely sits still, not only if it falls.
- **NIFTY closes between 24,050 and 24,150,** say at 24,100. The sold call is now worth 50 points against you while the bought call is still worthless. You give back part of the credit. At 24,100 you are just below the 24,106 breakeven, so you are roughly flat. This is the middle ground.
- **NIFTY closes at or above 24,150,** say at 24,300. Both calls have intrinsic value. The sold 24,050 call costs you 250 points, the bought 24,150 call returns 150, leaving a net 100 points, the full width, worth Rs 6,500 against you. Subtract that from the Rs 3,624 credit and you are left with the maximum loss of Rs 2,876. Anything above 24,150 gives the same result.

:::example
If NIFTY closes at 24,100 at expiry, the sold 24,050 call is worth about Rs 3,250 against you for the lot, and the bought 24,150 call expires worthless. Subtract that from the Rs 3,624 you collected and you keep about Rs 374. You are barely on the right side of breakeven, exactly as the middle of the chart shows.
:::

## When to choose it

The bear call spread suits a specific, common view. Reach for it when you think NIFTY is more likely to drift sideways or fall than to rally, but you are not expecting a crash. You do not need NIFTY to drop at all. You only need it to stay below 24,106, which means time itself is on your side. As the days pass and the index goes nowhere, the sold time value decays into your profit.

It is the wrong trade when you expect a hard, fast fall. In that case the long put from the last chapter pays far more, because the spread caps your reward at the modest Rs 3,624 credit no matter how far NIFTY drops. The bear call spread says, "I think the upside is done, but I do not expect fireworks down." For that mild-to-neutral bearish view, collecting Rs 3,624 against a known Rs 2,876 risk, with time working for you, is a clean and beginner-friendly trade.

:::tip
Pick the strikes around the level you genuinely think NIFTY will struggle to cross. Selling the 24,050 call places your breakeven at 24,106, just above the current spot, so you are paid for the index simply failing to rise. Selling a strike far above the money brings in less credit for the same width and wastes the edge.
:::

:::warn
Defined risk is not the same as small risk. Rs 2,876 per lot is real money, and you lose all of it if NIFTY closes at or above 24,150, which a single strong up day can deliver. Always size to the maximum loss, never to the credit. Decide how much you can afford to lose if the index rallies against you, then count back to how many lots that allows.
:::
