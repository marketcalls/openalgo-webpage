So far every bullish trade has had a neat, finite shape. Now we step up in ambition with three structures that trade those tidy caps for something larger, more open-ended, and in two cases far more dangerous. They are all still bullish, but each expresses the view differently. The **call ratio back spread** keeps your downside small while leaving the upside wide open. The **long synthetic future** turns two options into a straight line that behaves exactly like buying a NIFTY future. The **range forward** is a bullish collar that mostly sleeps until NIFTY makes a real move. These are the trades where the strategy builder's nine numbers earn their keep, because the shapes are no longer obvious from the name. All figures use real NIFTY option data captured on 26 June 2026, spot 24,056, lot 65, expiry 28 July 2026.

## The call ratio back spread

A back spread is built around an unequal number of legs. Here you sell one at-the-money call and buy two calls one strike higher.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell 1 | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 received |
| 2 | Buy 2 | 24,150 | Call | about Rs 48,107 paid (2 lots) |

The sold call funds part of the two you buy, but the two cost more in total, so you pay a **net debit of about Rs 20,430**. In return you own one extra long call above the strike, and that spare call is what gives the structure its unlimited upside. The more NIFTY rallies, the more that net long call runs, with no ceiling, which is why the builder prints the maximum profit as **Unlimited**.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Unlimited |
| Max loss | Rs 26,930 |
| Breakeven | 24,564 |
| Probability of profit | about 28 percent |
| Risk to reward | NA |
| Net debit | Rs 20,430 |
| Est. premium | Rs 75,784 |
| Total P&L now | about Rs 0 |
| Margin required | Rs 52,256 |

The key surprise is where the loss lives. It is not on the downside. Below 24,050 every call expires worthless, so you simply lose the net debit. The real pain sits in the middle, at the 24,150 strike, where the sold call is fully in the money but your two bought calls have barely woken up. That is the **maximum loss of Rs 26,930**, the deepest point of the valley. Above it, the two long calls take over, climb through the **breakeven of 24,564**, and run without limit.

{{image: strat-call-ratio-back-spread.png | The NIFTY call ratio back spread, selling one 24,050 call and buying two 24,150 calls. The orange line dips to its worst loss of Rs 26,930 at the 24,150 strike, climbs through the breakeven at 24,564, and then rises without any ceiling into the green. | chart}}

Read the chart. On the left, below 24,050, the line is a flat shelf at the net debit loss. As NIFTY rises into the 24,050 to 24,150 zone the sold call starts costing you faster than the bought calls pay, so the line sinks to its low point of Rs 26,930 right at 24,150. Past there the two long calls accelerate, the line turns sharply up, crosses zero at 24,564, and keeps climbing forever. The probability of profit is only about 28 percent, because NIFTY must clear 24,564, beyond the plus one sigma band, for the open-ended upside to pay.

:::key
A call ratio back spread has a defined, finite maximum loss, here Rs 26,930 at the 24,150 strike, and an unlimited maximum profit on a strong rally. The danger sits in the middle, not the tails, so the worst outcome is NIFTY drifting up only mildly and pinning the sold strike. This is the rare structure where unlimited means unlimited profit, not unlimited risk.
:::

## The long synthetic future

The next structure abandons curves entirely. By combining one bought call and one sold put at the same strike, you create a straight diagonal line that rises and falls exactly like owning a NIFTY future.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 paid |
| 2 | Sell | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 received |

The bought call gives you all the upside of a future, and the sold put hands you all of its downside. Together they cost a small **net debit of about Rs 8,496**, and the position then tracks NIFTY point for point in both directions. There is no flat floor anywhere, because there is no protective leg. This is the whole future, rebuilt from two options.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Unlimited |
| Max loss | Rs 1,571,746 |
| Breakeven | 24,181 |
| Probability of profit | about 44 percent |
| Risk to reward | NA |
| Net debit | Rs 8,496 |
| Est. premium | Rs 46,858 |
| Total P&L now | about Rs 0 |
| Margin required | Rs 173,537 |

{{image: strat-long-synthetic.png | The NIFTY long synthetic future, buying the 24,050 call and selling the 24,050 put. The orange line is a single straight diagonal, rising without limit above the breakeven of 24,181 and falling rupee for rupee below it, exactly like a long future. | chart}}

:::warn
The long synthetic future behaves exactly like a long NIFTY future, which means its risk is two-sided and enormous. The maximum loss is about Rs 1,571,746 if NIFTY collapses, the sold put is a naked short leg, and the exchange blocks a heavy margin of about Rs 173,537. There is no defined floor here. This is a futures-grade position dressed as two options, and a beginner should treat it with futures-grade respect.
:::

Read the line and there is nothing to interpret, which is the point. Above the **breakeven of 24,181** you gain point for point with no ceiling. Below it you lose point for point with no floor, all the way down. The blue T+0 line lies almost exactly on the orange one, because a synthetic future holds almost no time value to decay. You hold this when you want pure directional exposure but prefer the options route, never because it feels safer than a future, because it is not.

## The range forward

The third structure is gentler. A **range forward**, sometimes called a bullish collar, sells an out-of-the-money put below the market to fund an out-of-the-money call above it.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 23,950 | Put | about Rs 16,676 received |
| 2 | Buy | 24,150 | Call | about Rs 24,053 paid |

The sold put pays for most of the bought call, leaving a small **net debit of about Rs 7,378**. Between the two strikes, from 23,950 to 24,150, neither option has any intrinsic value, so the position simply rests on that small debit. It is a dead zone where nothing much happens. Outside the strikes it springs to life. Above 24,150 the bought call gives you unlimited upside. Below 23,950 the sold put hands you a future-like downside, deep and open.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Unlimited |
| Max loss | Rs 1,564,128 |
| Breakeven | 24,264 |
| Probability of profit | about 40 percent |
| Risk to reward | NA |
| Net debit | Rs 7,378 |
| Est. premium | Rs 40,729 |
| Total P&L now | about Rs 0 |
| Margin required | Rs 166,553 |

{{image: strat-range-forward.png | The NIFTY range forward, selling the 23,950 put and buying the 24,150 call. The orange line is nearly a long future with a small flat dead zone between the strikes, unlimited above the breakeven of 24,264 and a deep future-like loss below 23,950. | chart}}

:::warn
The range forward erases your cost by selling a naked put, so the downside is future-like and very large, a maximum loss of about Rs 1,564,128, with margin near Rs 166,553. The flat dead zone between the strikes makes it feel calm, but that calm ends the instant NIFTY drops through 23,950, where the sold put begins losing rupee for rupee with no floor. Treat the downside exactly as you would a short put.
:::

Read the chart and the range forward looks almost like the synthetic future, but with a short flat shelf in the middle where the two strikes straddle the dead zone. Above the **breakeven of 24,264** you run with the long call. Below 23,950 you fall with the sold put. The structure suits a trader who is bullish and willing to be assigned NIFTY on a fall, in exchange for cheap, open-ended upside if the rally comes.

## Three shapes, one bullish family

| Strategy | Max loss | Max profit | Breakeven | Margin |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Call ratio back spread | Rs 26,930 | Unlimited | 24,564 | Rs 52,256 |
| Long synthetic future | Rs 1,571,746 | Unlimited | 24,181 | Rs 173,537 |
| Range forward | Rs 1,564,128 | Unlimited | 24,264 | Rs 166,553 |

Read that maximum loss column carefully, because it sorts these three by danger. The **call ratio back spread** is the only one a beginner should reach for, since its worst case is a defined Rs 26,930 and it asks for modest margin. The **long synthetic future** and the **range forward** both carry a naked short leg, a near six-figure or larger downside, and heavy margin, because each is really a future in disguise.

:::fact
All three structures share an unlimited upside, but they differ entirely in what they risk to get it. The call ratio back spread risks a capped Rs 26,930. The synthetic future and the range forward each risk well over fifteen lakh on a collapse, because a sold put or sold combination leaves the downside open. Unlimited profit is attractive, but always check which side of the chart hides the unlimited, or near unlimited, loss.
:::

You have now met the open-ended bullish shapes and learned to separate the one with defined risk from the two that behave like futures. In the next chapter we swing to the opposite extreme, the tightest and cheapest bullish bets of all, the bullish butterfly and the bullish condor, where the risk is tiny but the odds are slim.
