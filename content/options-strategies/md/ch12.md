The bearish trades so far have all rewarded a fall, the bigger the better. This chapter teaches the opposite instinct. What if you do not just think NIFTY will drop, but you think it will drop to a particular place and stop, settling into a narrow band a little below where it sits now? When your view is that precise, you can buy a structure that costs almost nothing and pays many times its cost if the index lands exactly where you guessed. These are the **bearish butterfly** and the **bearish condor**, built entirely from puts, sitting just below the money. They are cheap, they are defined-risk, and they offer a dazzling reward against a tiny outlay. The honest catch, which this chapter will not hide, is that the odds of winning are low, because the price has to thread a needle.

## The bearish butterfly

A butterfly has three strikes and four legs. You buy one put above, sell two in the middle, and buy one below, so the two bought wings fence in the two sold puts at the centre. Placed below the current spot, the whole structure peaks if NIFTY drifts down to the middle strike.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Put | upper wing |
| 2 | Sell | 23,950 | Put (x2) | body |
| 3 | Buy | 23,850 | Put | lower wing |

The two sold puts at the body bring in most of the premium, and the two bought wings cost a little more, leaving a tiny **net debit of about Rs 393** for the whole lot. That debit is your **maximum loss**, suffered if NIFTY finishes outside the wings, above 24,050 or below 23,850, where the structure is worth nothing. The **maximum profit is Rs 6,107**, reached only if NIFTY lands precisely on the 23,950 body at expiry, where the two sold puts are worth the full wing width against worthless wings working for you.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 6,107 |
| Max loss | Rs 393 |
| Breakevens | 23,856 and 24,047 |
| Probability of profit | 9 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1:16 |
| Net debit | Rs 393 |
| Margin required | Rs 63,606 |

It costs a tiny debit, that debit is the most you can lose, and it pays many times over if NIFTY drifts down and pins the body strike at expiry. It is a targeted bet on a precise landing, not on a big move.
:::

## The bearish condor

The condor is a butterfly with its peak stretched into a plateau. Instead of selling two puts at one body strike, you sell them at two adjacent strikes, which spreads the maximum profit across a small range rather than a single point. That makes it a little more forgiving about exactly where NIFTY lands, at the cost of a lower peak.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Put | upper wing |
| 2 | Sell | 24,000 | Put | inner |
| 3 | Sell | 23,900 | Put | inner |
| 4 | Buy | 23,850 | Put | lower wing |

The two sold inner puts again bring in most of the premium, leaving a **net debit of just Rs 36** for the lot, the cheapest entry in this entire chapter. That Rs 36 is the **maximum loss**. The **maximum profit is Rs 3,214**, earned across the plateau if NIFTY finishes anywhere between the two inner sold strikes of 23,900 and 24,000 at expiry.

| Number | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 3,214 |
| Max loss | Rs 36 |
| Breakevens | 23,837 and 24,055 |
| Probability of profit | 10 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1:90 |
| Net debit | Rs 36 |
| Margin required | Rs 63,256 |

{{image: strat-bearish-condor.png | The NIFTY bearish condor in puts: a flat profit plateau of Rs 3,214 between 23,900 and 24,000, breakevens at 23,837 and 24,055, and a near-zero Rs 36 loss outside the wings. | chart}}

The orange line crosses zero at **23,837 and 24,055**, marking the edges of the profit zone, and runs flat across the plateau between the inner strikes. Compared with the butterfly, the tent has become a tabletop: a lower ceiling of Rs 3,214 instead of Rs 6,107, but earned over a wider landing zone instead of a single point.

:::fact
Look at the reward to risk. The butterfly reads 1:16 and the condor an astonishing 1:90, because the Rs 36 debit is so small against the Rs 3,214 it can earn. These look like the best trades in the course. The number that keeps you honest is the probability of profit, just 9 to 10 percent. A 1:90 payoff that wins one time in ten is a lottery ticket, not an edge.
:::

## Why the odds are low, told honestly

The dazzling reward to risk and the tiny cost make these structures look almost too good. The catch is geometry. To win, NIFTY must finish inside a band only a couple of hundred points wide, and to win the most it must finish in an even narrower slice. With about 32 days to expiry and the sigma bands on the chart showing how far NIFTY could roam, the chance of it settling into that exact pocket below the money is genuinely small. That is what the 9 to 10 percent probability of profit is measuring, and it is not a flaw in the trade. It is the price of the huge payoff. The market does not hand out 1:90 odds on likely events.

So the cheapness is a feature and a trap at once. Because one lot risks only Rs 36 to Rs 393, it is tempting to scale up to many lots and treat the structure as nearly free. Do not. Stacking lots multiplies a low-probability bet, and the small per-lot loss becomes a real loss across size, suffered nine or ten times out of ten.

:::warn
A near-zero cost is not a near-zero risk once you size up. Ten lots of the bearish condor risk Rs 360 and ten lots of the butterfly risk Rs 3,930, and you will lose that full amount roughly nine times out of ten, because the probability of profit is only about 10 percent. Treat these as small, deliberate, low-odds bets, not as free money to load up on.
:::

## When these earn their place

Used with clear eyes, the bearish butterfly and condor have a real role. They are the right tool when you hold a specific, time-bound view that NIFTY will ease down to a particular level and stall there, perhaps into a known support or ahead of an event you expect to pin the index. You are paying a few hundred rupees for a defined-risk ticket on that precise outcome, and if you are right the payoff dwarfs the cost.

- Choose the **butterfly** when your view of the landing spot is sharp and you want the highest payoff for pinning the 23,950 body exactly.
- Choose the **condor** when you want a little more room, accepting a lower ceiling in exchange for a plateau that pays anywhere from 23,900 to 24,000.

Both keep your worst case printed on the ticket, Rs 393 or Rs 36, which is exactly the discipline this course keeps pressing. The margin, around Rs 63,000, reflects the four legs the exchange must track, but your actual cash at risk is only the tiny debit.

:::tip
Place these closer to expiry, not far from it. A butterfly or condor pinned weeks early gives NIFTY too much time to wander out of the narrow band. The same structure put on in the final week, when the sigma bands have shrunk and you have a strong read on where the index will settle, has a far better chance of landing in the pocket.
:::

:::info
The estimated premium on these trades is a turnover figure across all four legs and looks large, but it is not your risk. Your risk is the small net debit, Rs 393 or Rs 36, because the bought wings cap every outcome. Never read the gross premium as the amount you can lose; read the net debit, which the builder reports as the maximum loss.
:::
