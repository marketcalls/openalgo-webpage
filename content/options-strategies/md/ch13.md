Every strategy so far has needed a direction. You leaned bullish or you leaned bearish, and you chose a structure to match. Now meet the first trades that do not care which way NIFTY goes, only how far. Picture the index sitting at 24,056 with a big event on the calendar, a policy decision or a result that everyone knows will land but nobody can call in advance. You are convinced NIFTY is about to move hard. You simply do not know whether up or down. The **long straddle** and the **long strangle** are built for exactly that moment. You buy both a call and a put, so a large move in either direction pays, and your only real enemy is a market that goes nowhere.

## The long straddle: buying both sides at the money

A **long straddle** is the purest big-move bet there is. You buy the at-the-money call and the at-the-money put on the same strike and the same expiry, here the 24,050 strike expiring 28 July 2026. One leg profits if NIFTY climbs, the other if it falls, and you own both.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 paid |
| 2 | Buy | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 paid |

Add the two premiums. The call costs about Rs 27,677 and the put about Rs 19,182, for a total **net debit of Rs 46,858** for one lot of 65. That debit is large because you are buying two at-the-money options at once, each fat with time value. Because you paid it up front and can never be asked for more, that Rs 46,858 is also your **maximum loss**, suffered only if NIFTY finishes exactly on 24,050 at expiry, where both options expire worthless together. The margin the exchange blocks is simply the debit itself, Rs 46,858, because this is a fully **defined-risk** trade.

The **maximum profit is unlimited**. Above the upper breakeven the call runs without a ceiling, so the position keeps gaining the further NIFTY climbs. On a fall the put gains too, all the way down toward zero, a very large but technically finite amount. The builder reports the upside as unlimited, which is the honest label for a payoff that has no cap.

:::key
A long straddle buys the at-the-money call and the at-the-money put together. It profits from a big move in either direction and loses only if NIFTY sits still. The premium you pay, here Rs 46,858, is the whole risk, and the reward is open ended on a genuine move.
:::

The two **breakevens are 23,329 and 24,771**. Those come straight from the debit. You paid about 721 points a share in total premium, so NIFTY has to travel more than 721 points down to 23,329 or up to 24,771 just to cover what you spent. Only beyond those marks does the straddle turn a profit. The **probability of profit is 42 percent**, the market-implied chance that NIFTY finishes outside that band by 28 July. It sits below half because a 721-point move, roughly three percent of the index in about a month, is a real distance to ask for.

## Reading the long straddle chart

{{image: strat-long-straddle.png | The long straddle payoff on NIFTY: a sharp V with its trough at the 24,050 strike where the full Rs 46,858 is lost, breakevens at 23,329 and 24,771, and profit lines that rise without limit as the index moves far in either direction. | chart}}

The orange at-expiry line draws a clean V. Its lowest point sits right at the 24,050 strike, on the full loss of Rs 46,858, because that is the one place both options die worthless. Walk outward from there in either direction and the loss shrinks as one leg comes alive. At **23,329 on the left and 24,771 on the right** the line crosses zero, the amber breakeven dots, and beyond them you climb into profit, steeper and steeper. The pink spot line at 24,056 stands almost on top of the trough, which says it plainly: you have entered at the worst point on the at-expiry curve and you need NIFTY to walk a long way out of the valley.

Now look at the blue dashed T+0 line, the position today. Near the centre it floats well above the orange V, because the two options are still stuffed with time value. That gap is the catch. As the days pass and nothing happens, the blue line sags down toward the orange, and your position loses money even when NIFTY barely moves. That sag is **time decay**, and for a straddle buyer it is a daily tax.

:::warn
A long straddle bleeds time value every calm day. You are long two at-the-money options at once, so the decay runs against you twice as fast. The Rs 46,858 you paid is at risk not only if NIFTY stays put but also if it drifts slowly, because the move has to be large and reasonably quick to outrun the decay. Never treat a straddle as a position to hold and forget. It is a bet that something happens soon.
:::

## The long strangle: the same idea, made cheaper

The straddle is powerful but expensive, because both legs sit at the money where time value is richest. The **long strangle** keeps the same shape for less money by buying out-of-the-money options instead. You buy the put two strikes below and the call two strikes above, here the 23,950 put and the 24,150 call.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 23,950 | Put | out-of-the-money |
| 2 | Buy | 24,150 | Call | out-of-the-money |

Because both legs are out of the money, each costs less, and the **net debit falls to Rs 40,729** for the lot, about Rs 6,000 cheaper than the straddle. That smaller debit is the **maximum loss**, and the margin equals it. The trade-off is that the cheaper entry buys you a worse breakeven, not a better one.

The **breakevens are 23,323 and 24,777**, wider than the straddle on both sides. The strangle gives back nothing in the middle. Between 23,950 and 24,150 both options are worthless at expiry, so across that whole central band you simply lose the full Rs 40,729. The **probability of profit is 41 percent**, a touch lower than the straddle, because although you paid less, NIFTY now has to clear an even wider gap before either leg pays.

{{image: strat-long-strangle.png | The long strangle payoff on NIFTY: a flat-bottomed valley losing the full Rs 40,729 anywhere between 23,950 and 24,150, breakevens at 23,323 and 24,777, and unlimited profit once the index travels far enough beyond either wing. | chart}}

On the chart the V has become a bucket. Instead of a single sharp trough, the orange line runs flat along the bottom across the gap between the two strikes, then turns up only outside the wings. The breakevens sit a little farther out than the straddle's, at **23,323 and 24,777**. You have paid less to put the trade on, and in return NIFTY must move a little more before you are paid.

## Straddle against strangle, and the move you are buying

Put the two side by side and the choice becomes a simple bargain about cost versus distance.

| Number | Long Straddle | Long Strangle |
|---|---|---|
| Max profit | Unlimited | Unlimited |
| Max loss | Rs 46,858 | Rs 40,729 |
| Breakevens | 23,329 and 24,771 | 23,323 and 24,777 |
| Probability of profit | 42 percent | 41 percent |
| Net debit | Rs 46,858 | Rs 40,729 |
| Margin required | Rs 46,858 | Rs 40,729 |

The straddle costs more but starts working a few points sooner, because its legs are already at the money. The strangle costs less but asks for a slightly bigger move before it pays. Neither is free, and both share the same Achilles heel: they need NIFTY to actually go somewhere.

:::fact
Look at what the market is charging you to bet on a move. The straddle's Rs 46,858 debit means NIFTY has to travel about 721 points just to reach breakeven, the strangle about 727 points. Those distances are the option market's own estimate of how far NIFTY might roam by 28 July, drawn on the chart as the sigma bands. You are not getting a cheap bet on volatility, you are paying the fair price for it.
:::

:::tip
The best time to buy a straddle or strangle is when implied volatility is low and you expect it to rise, not after it has already spiked ahead of a known event. If you buy into already expensive options, even a real move can disappoint, because the volatility you paid for drains out of both legs once the event passes. Buy the move before the crowd prices it in, not after.
:::

:::info
The one comfort in both trades is that the risk is fully defined and the margin equals the debit. You can lose Rs 46,858 on the straddle or Rs 40,729 on the strangle and not a rupee more, no matter how violently NIFTY swings. That is the gift of being a buyer. Your worst case is printed on the ticket before you ever place the order, which is exactly the discipline a beginner should keep.
:::

That instinct, that you are buying motion and fighting the clock, is the heart of every long-volatility trade.
