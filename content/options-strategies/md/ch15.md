The short strangle from the last chapter had a lovely income shape and one fatal flaw: tails that fell forever. The obvious question is whether you can keep the steady premium while putting a hard floor under the loss. You can, and the structure that does it is the **iron condor**, the most popular defined-risk income trade in the options world. The idea is simple. Take the sold strangle and buy a cheaper, further out-of-the-money call and put as insurance. Those two bought wings turn the unlimited tails into known numbers. What you are left with is a four-leg structure that profits inside a range and can never lose more than a fixed, modest amount. NIFTY, the deepest index option market in the country, is the natural home for it.

## From a naked strangle to a fenced range

An iron condor is two vertical spreads sharing one expiry: a **bull put spread** on the downside and a **bear call spread** on the upside, placed around the current price. Here is the version the OpenAlgo builder calls the **Long Iron Condor**, built tight to the money on the 28 July 2026 expiry.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 23,850 | Put | lower wing |
| 2 | Sell | 23,950 | Put | inner short |
| 3 | Sell | 24,150 | Call | inner short |
| 4 | Buy | 24,250 | Call | upper wing |

The two inner sold options, the 23,950 put and the 24,150 call, are the same legs as a short strangle and bring in most of the premium. The two outer bought options, the 23,850 put and the 24,250 call, cost a little and act as insurance, capping each tail one hundred points out. The net is a **credit of Rs 5,616** that lands in your account when you open the trade. That credit is your **maximum profit**, kept in full if NIFTY finishes between the inner strikes of 23,950 and 24,150 at expiry, where all four legs settle in your favour.

Now the loss is fenced. Each wing is one hundred points wide, worth Rs 6,500 on a lot of 65, and you already collected Rs 5,616 of that, so the most you can lose is the gap, **Rs 884**. That is the whole difference between this and the naked strangle: the worst case has gone from unlimited to Rs 884, and the margin has fallen from over a lakh and a half to about Rs 64,319.

:::key
An iron condor is a bull put spread plus a bear call spread sharing one expiry. The inner sold strikes collect the premium, and the outer bought wings cap both tails. It is the defined-risk version of the short strangle: the same range-bound income shape, but with a known, small maximum loss instead of an open cliff.
:::

## Reading the long iron condor chart

{{image: strat-long-iron-condor.png | The long iron condor on NIFTY: a flat profit plateau of Rs 5,616 between the 23,950 and 24,150 short strikes, breakevens at 23,864 and 24,236, and a capped loss of just Rs 884 once the index pushes past either wing. | chart}}

The orange at-expiry line draws a flat-topped table. Across the gap between the inner strikes it runs flat at the full credit of Rs 5,616, the green plateau, then steps down on each side as one spread comes alive, flattening again at the small Rs 884 loss once NIFTY passes a wing. The amber **breakevens sit at 23,864 and 24,236**, the points where the credit you banked is exactly eaten by a leg moving against you. The pink spot line at 24,056 sits inside the plateau, near its centre, so today you are sitting in the profitable zone.

But notice how narrow that profit band is. The breakevens are only about 190 points on each side of spot, and the sigma bands on the chart show NIFTY can easily travel three or four times that far by expiry. That is why the **probability of profit is just 17 percent**. The structure pays beautifully against its risk, a **reward to risk of 1 to 6.4**, because the inner strikes hug the money and collect a rich credit. The price of that rich credit is a tight, low-odds profit zone. You are most likely to walk away with the small Rs 884 loss, and only occasionally with the full Rs 5,616.

:::fact
This is the same honest tension you met with the directional butterflies. A reward to risk of 1 to 6.4 looks wonderful until you read the 17 percent probability of profit beside it. The market does not give away a six-to-one payoff on a likely event. The big credit and the tiny risk are paid for with a narrow band that NIFTY usually escapes.
:::

## The short iron condor: the high-probability inverse

The builder also offers the exact mirror of this trade, which it calls the **Short Iron Condor**. You take the other side of all four legs: buy the inner strikes, sell the outer wings, and pay a **debit of Rs 5,616** instead of collecting it.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 23,850 | Put | lower wing |
| 2 | Buy | 23,950 | Put | inner long |
| 3 | Buy | 24,150 | Call | inner long |
| 4 | Sell | 24,250 | Call | upper wing |

Because it is the inverse, every number flips. The **maximum profit is Rs 884**, earned whenever NIFTY finishes outside the breakevens, that is, anywhere except pinned inside the narrow central band. The **maximum loss is Rs 5,616**, suffered only if NIFTY settles between the inner strikes at expiry. The breakevens are the same 23,864 and 24,236, and the margin is about Rs 68,864. The headline number is the **probability of profit of 83 percent**, because NIFTY almost always drifts at least a little away from dead centre over 32 days, and any such drift hands this version its small win.

{{image: strat-short-iron-condor.png | The short iron condor on NIFTY: the inverse shape, a small Rs 884 profit anywhere outside the 23,864 to 24,236 band, but a Rs 5,616 loss if the index pins the central zone at expiry, with an 83 percent probability of profit. | chart}}

So you have one structure and two faces. One collects a big credit and rarely keeps it. The other pays a debit, wins a little most of the time, and loses a lot on the rare occasion NIFTY sits perfectly still.

## Which one for income, and the lesson of the labels

A trader chasing income wants to be right often, to collect a small, repeatable edge rather than swing for a jackpot. By that single test, the **high-probability structure is the one to study**, here the version the builder labels the Short Iron Condor at 83 percent. Being right four times in five feels like income. But the nine numbers tell you the rest of the story, and it is not all comfort.

- The high-probability side wins only **Rs 884** when it is right and loses **Rs 5,616** when it is wrong. A handful of wins is undone by a single loss, so winning often is not the same as winning overall.
- The big-credit side collects **Rs 5,616** but is right only 17 percent of the time, so its rare wins must carry a long string of small Rs 884 losses.
- Either way the position is **defined-risk**: your worst case is printed on the ticket, Rs 884 on one side or Rs 5,616 on the other, with no open tail. That alone makes the iron condor a far safer income tool than the naked strangle it is built from.

:::warn
Do not trust the words Long and Short on a builder, and do not assume a credit means a good trade or a debit a bad one. Here the Long Iron Condor is the credit version and the Short Iron Condor is the debit version, which runs against most people's intuition. The only honest way to judge any structure is to read its nine numbers: max profit, max loss, the breakevens, the probability of profit, and whether the credit or debit fits the outcome you actually expect. The label can mislead you. The numbers cannot.
:::

:::tip
Real income condors are usually placed wider than this tight example. Push the inner short strikes further out of the money and the profit band widens, the probability of profit climbs, and the credit shrinks. You trade a smaller reward for better odds and a calmer night. The builder lets you drag the strikes and watch the plateau stretch and the probability rise in real time, so you can find the balance of credit and odds that suits your view before risking a rupee.
:::

:::info
The margin on either version, about Rs 64,000 to Rs 69,000, reflects the four legs the exchange must track, but your true cash at risk is only the defined maximum loss, Rs 884 or Rs 5,616. That is the whole reason traders accept the lower reward of a condor over a naked strangle: a heavy, open-ended margin and an unlimited tail are replaced by a modest, knowable risk you can size with confidence.
:::
