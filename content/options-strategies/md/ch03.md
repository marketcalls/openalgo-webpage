You have finished Options Basics, so the four single legs are already familiar. Now we put them to work with a clear opinion. You think NIFTY is heading higher over the next month. There are two plain ways to back that view with a single option, and they sit on opposite sides of the very same trade. You can **buy a call**, paying a premium now for the right to ride a rise. Or you can **sell a put**, collecting a premium now and agreeing to buy NIFTY if it falls to your strike. One of you is the buyer, hopeful and patient with a small, fixed cost. The other is the seller, paid upfront but carrying a real obligation. This chapter teaches both on real NIFTY option data captured on 26 June 2026, with the index at 24,056, the at-the-money strike at 24,050, one lot of 65 units, and expiry on 28 July 2026.

## The long call: pay a little, aim for a lot

The long call is the most natural bullish trade there is. You buy one at-the-money call and that single leg is the whole position.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Buy | 24,050 | Call | about Rs 27,677 paid |

The 24,050 call costs about **Rs 27,677** for one lot of 65. That money leaves your account the moment you buy, and you can never be asked for a rupee more. That one fact is the whole comfort of buying options. Your **maximum loss is the premium you paid**, Rs 27,677, and not a paisa beyond it. In exchange, your upside is left wide open. Every point NIFTY climbs above the strike adds to the call's value with no ceiling, which is why the builder prints the maximum profit as **Unlimited**.

Here is the full nine-number panel for the long call.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Unlimited |
| Max loss | Rs 27,677 |
| Breakeven | 24,476 |
| Probability of profit | about 31 percent |
| Risk to reward | NA |
| Net debit | Rs 27,677 |
| Est. premium | Rs 27,677 |
| Total P&L now | about Rs 0 |
| Margin required | Rs 27,677 |

Notice the margin equals the debit. Because you cannot lose more than you paid, the exchange blocks only the premium itself and nothing extra. That is the cleanest margin in the entire course.

:::key
A long call has a fixed, known cost that is also its maximum loss, here Rs 27,677, paired with an unlimited upside. You risk a defined amount to chase an open-ended gain. That shape, a small capped loss against a large open profit, is exactly why beginners are pointed at buying options before they ever sell them.
:::

## Reading the long call chart

{{image: strat-long-call.png | The long call on NIFTY, buying the 24,050 call for Rs 27,677. The orange at-expiry line sits on a flat loss floor below the strike, crosses zero at the breakeven of 24,476, then climbs without limit into the green profit zone. | chart}}

Walk the orange at-expiry line from left to right. Far to the left, with NIFTY below 24,050, the right to buy at 24,050 is worthless, so the line rests on a flat floor at a loss of Rs 27,677, your maximum loss. As NIFTY pushes above 24,050 the call starts gaining intrinsic value and the loss shrinks. At the **breakeven of 24,476**, marked by the amber dot, the line crosses zero. NIFTY has to climb about 420 points, past the strike and far enough to earn back the whole premium, before you make a single rupee. Above 24,476 you are in the green and every further point is pure gain, the line rising without any ceiling.

The blue dashed line is the T+0 curve, the value today. It is smooth and rounded because the call still holds plenty of time value. As the days tick toward 28 July, that dashed line sinks toward the orange one, which is time decay working steadily against you as a buyer. The faint vertical bands are the sigma ranges, the spread of moves the option market itself is pricing in. The pink line marks spot at 24,056.

This is where the 31 percent probability of profit comes from. The breakeven of 24,476 sits above the plus one sigma band, so the market is implying only about a one in three chance NIFTY finishes there or higher by expiry. A long call is a low-odds, high-payoff bet, and the chart shows you exactly why.

:::info
Time decay is the call buyer's quiet enemy. Even if NIFTY drifts sideways, the blue T+0 line falls toward the orange expiry line day after day, so the call bleeds value while you wait. A long call needs the move to actually happen, and reasonably soon, not merely eventually.
:::

## The short put: get paid to be patient

Now cross to the other side of the trade. Instead of paying for a call, you **sell an at-the-money put** and collect the premium upfront.

| Leg | Action | Strike | Type | Premium per lot |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sell | 24,050 | Put | about Rs 19,182 received |

You take in about **Rs 19,182** the instant you sell, and that credit is the most you can ever make. You keep all of it if NIFTY closes at or above 24,050 on expiry, because the put you sold then expires worthless and the buyer walks away. You are, in effect, being paid to promise you will buy NIFTY at 24,050 if it sinks there. As long as it does not, you simply keep the rent.

The catch is the obligation. If NIFTY falls below 24,050, the put you sold moves against you rupee for rupee, exactly as though you owned NIFTY from 24,050 downward. The builder puts the maximum loss at a startling **Rs 1,544,068**, the cost of being forced to buy at 24,050 in a near total collapse toward zero. That is not a realistic single session outcome, but it is the honest theoretical floor, and it dwarfs the Rs 19,182 you collected.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Max profit | Rs 19,182 |
| Max loss | Rs 1,544,068 |
| Breakeven | 23,755 |
| Probability of profit | about 62 percent |
| Risk to reward | 1 : 0.01 |
| Net credit | Rs 19,182 |
| Est. premium | Rs 19,182 |
| Total P&L now | about Rs 0 |
| Margin required | Rs 165,042 |

:::warn
The short put is a naked short leg. Its maximum profit is the Rs 19,182 you collect, but its maximum loss runs to about Rs 1,544,068 if NIFTY collapses, and the exchange blocks a heavy margin of about Rs 165,042 just to hold it. The risk to reward of 1 : 0.01 says it plainly. You are risking a large, lumpy amount to earn a small, fixed one. A beginner should study this trade carefully, not lead with it.
:::

{{image: strat-short-put.png | The short put on NIFTY, selling the 24,050 put for a credit of Rs 19,182. The orange line is flat at maximum profit above the strike, crosses zero at the breakeven of 23,755, then falls deep into the loss zone toward a very large downside. | chart}}

Read the chart. To the right, with NIFTY at or above 24,050, the orange line sits flat on its maximum profit of Rs 19,182. As NIFTY falls below the strike the line slopes down, crossing zero at the **breakeven of 23,755**, which is the strike less the credit you banked per unit. Below 23,755 you are in genuine loss, and the line keeps sliding toward the bottom left with no flat floor until NIFTY is near zero. The safe zone, everything at or above 23,755, is wide, which is why the probability of profit is a comfortable 62 percent.

## Buyer against seller, both bullish

Both trades are bullish, yet they are mirror images, and laying them side by side teaches the central bargain of this whole course.

| | Long call | Short put |
|---|---|---|
| You pay or receive | pay Rs 27,677 | receive Rs 19,182 |
| Max loss | Rs 27,677 | Rs 1,544,068 |
| Max profit | Unlimited | Rs 19,182 |
| Breakeven | 24,476 | 23,755 |
| Prob. of profit | about 31 percent | about 62 percent |
| Margin | Rs 27,677 | Rs 165,042 |

- The **long call** carries a small, fully defined risk and an unlimited reward, but it needs a real move higher to pay, so its odds are low at about 31 percent and it loses ground to time decay every quiet day.
- The **short put** carries high odds, about 62 percent, and is paid by the passage of time rather than punished by it, but it caps your gain at the credit while leaving a large, lumpy downside and demanding heavy margin.

This is the buyer versus seller bargain in miniature. Buyers pay for the chance of a big, open-ended win and accept low odds. Sellers take high odds and friendly time decay in exchange for a capped gain and a tail of real danger. Neither side is right or wrong, but for a beginner the defined-risk long call, or better still the defined-risk spreads in the chapters just ahead, is the safer place to begin.

:::tip
A simple way to choose. Buy the call when you expect a real, soon move and can accept losing the whole premium if it does not arrive. Sell the put only when you genuinely would be happy to own NIFTY near 23,755, can fund the heavy margin, and fully respect the downside. If you cannot say yes to all three, do not sell the put.
:::

:::fact
On the same bullish view and the same 24,050 strike, the long call risks Rs 27,677 for an unlimited upside at about 31 percent odds, while the short put risks up to Rs 1,544,068 to earn Rs 19,182 at about 62 percent odds. High odds with a small reward, or low odds with a large one. Almost every strategy ahead is built by combining these two instincts.
:::
