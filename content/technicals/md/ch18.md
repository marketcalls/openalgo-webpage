Moving averages tell you the trend. RSI tells you the momentum. The MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence — is the tool that tries to fold both into a single panel, which is exactly why it became one of the most-watched indicators on every trading screen. It does this with no new mathematics at all: just two moving averages and the gap between them.

That gap is the whole idea. When a fast average pulls away from a slow one, the trend is accelerating; when they converge, momentum is fading; when they cross, something has shifted. The MACD turns that distance into a wiggling line, a signal line, and a histogram — and traders treat the crossover of those lines as a buy or sell trigger. As with the golden cross, the data has an opinion about that, and it is a sobering one: across the Nifty 50, the famous MACD bullish crossover beat a modest +5% only **37%** of the time. Let us build it first, then look honestly at what the cross is worth.

## How MACD is built

Three pieces, stacked from two EMAs:

1. **MACD line** = the 12-day EMA minus the 26-day EMA. When the fast (12) average is above the slow (26), this line is positive; when below, negative. It measures how far the short-term trend has separated from the medium-term one.
2. **Signal line** = a 9-day EMA *of the MACD line*. A smoothed, slower version of the MACD line itself.
3. **Histogram** = the MACD line minus the signal line, drawn as bars. It visualises the gap between the two lines — the rate at which momentum is building or fading.

```python
from openalgo import ta
macd_line, signal_line, histogram = ta.macd(df["close"], 12, 26, 9)
```

The 12/26/9 settings are the defaults everyone shares; like the RSI's 14, their power is partly that the crowd watches the same numbers. You *can* tune them — faster settings (say 5/13/5) react sooner but whipsaw more, slower settings smooth out noise at the cost of even more lag — but the same trade-off you met with moving averages applies, and most traders leave the defaults precisely so they are reading the same picture as everyone else. Because MACD is built from EMAs, it inherits their lag — it is a follower, not a forecaster.

One quirk worth knowing: MACD is plotted in the *price units* of the stock, so a ₹4,000 stock will show much bigger MACD values than a ₹200 stock. That means MACD levels are not comparable across stocks the way RSI's 0–100 scale is — only the *shape* (crossings, the histogram's rise and fall, divergence) carries meaning, not the absolute number.

:::key
MACD is just the distance between two moving averages, smoothed and plotted. Everything it shows — crossover, histogram, zero line — is a restatement of "how far has the fast average pulled away from the slow one, and is that gap growing or shrinking?"
:::

## The signal-line crossover

The headline signal: when the **MACD line crosses above the signal line**, momentum is turning up (bullish cross); when it crosses **below**, momentum is turning down (bearish cross). It is the MACD equivalent of a moving-average crossover, but faster, because it operates on the *difference* of averages rather than the averages themselves.

Here is one that worked beautifully. SBIN's MACD line crossed above its signal line on 11 September 2025 at a price of 823 — and crucially, the cross happened with MACD already *above* the zero line, in established bullish territory. Over the next 30 days the stock gained **+12%**.

{{image: ch18-sbin-macd.png | SBIN's MACD line crossed above its signal line on 11 Sep 2025 at 823, with MACD already above zero — and the stock rose +12% over the next 30 days. A clean winner, but across the whole Nifty 50 only 37% of bullish crosses beat +5% in 30 days. | chart}}

One clean win. Now the universe. We measured **7,640** bullish MACD crossovers across the Nifty 50 over 16 years. The results:

- **58% were positive** after 30 days — barely better than a coin flip, and only a little above the +1.48%/20-day baseline drift the market gives you for free.
- Only **37% beat +5%** over those 30 days. Nearly two-thirds of bullish crosses failed to deliver even a modest gain.

:::fact
Across 7,640 bullish MACD crossovers on the Nifty 50, only **37%** went on to gain more than +5% within 30 days, and just 58% were positive at all. The crossover *by itself* is close to a coin flip — it is a starting point for analysis, not a signal to act on alone.
:::

The crossover is so common because the lines cross constantly — including dozens of times during any sideways grind, each one a potential whipsaw. That frequency is precisely why the standalone hit rate is so ordinary.

## The zero line is the context

Here is the filter most beginners skip. The MACD line crossing **zero** is more meaningful than it crossing the signal line, because zero is where the 12-EMA and 26-EMA themselves cross — a genuine change in the medium-term trend.

- MACD **above zero** = the fast average is above the slow one = the underlying trend leans up. A bullish signal-line cross here (as in the SBIN example) is *with* the trend.
- MACD **below zero** = the trend leans down. A bullish cross down here is fighting the prevailing direction — a counter-trend bet.

You might expect "only take bullish crosses while MACD is above zero" to sharply improve results. It is a sensible instinct — and on this data it **did not help**. A MACD-above-zero filter on the bullish crosses did not meaningfully lift the win rate. The lesson is not that context is useless; it is that *this one mechanical filter, alone,* does not rescue a near-coin-flip signal. Real edge comes from combining the cross with structure — a level, a trend, volume — not from stacking another version of the same momentum reading.

:::warn
Do not assume a "smart" filter fixes a weak signal just because it sounds rigorous. Requiring MACD above zero before a bullish cross feels safer, but on the Nifty 50 it did not improve the outcome. Test the filter before you trust it; an untested rule is just a story.
:::

## The histogram: momentum's early tell

The histogram — MACD minus signal — is often the most useful part, because it changes *before* the lines actually cross. When bars are tall and growing, the two lines are diverging and momentum is accelerating. When the bars start *shrinking* while still positive, the lines are converging — momentum is fading even though no cross has happened yet. A shrinking histogram is an early warning that the current leg is losing steam, giving you a heads-up well ahead of the lagging crossover.

:::tip
Watch the histogram for the *change*, not the absolute height. Bars that stop growing and begin to contract tell you momentum is decelerating — often the first crack in a move, visible before price rolls over and long before the signal-line cross confirms it.
:::

## MACD divergence

Like RSI, MACD can diverge from price, and this is arguably its most respected use. A **bearish divergence**: price makes a higher high while the MACD makes a *lower* high — the new peak was reached with less underlying momentum, hinting the trend is tiring. A **bullish divergence**: price makes a lower low while MACD makes a higher low — selling pressure is weakening beneath a still-falling price. Because MACD measures the *acceleration* of a trend, divergence on it is a clean visual of a move quietly running out of fuel. As always, divergence is a warning, not a dated trigger — it tells you to tighten risk and wait for price to confirm, not to reverse on the spot.

## Strengths and blind spots

MACD's strength is genuine: in a **trending** market it keeps you reading momentum and trend at once, the histogram flags fatigue early, and divergence catches exhaustion. Its blind spot is equally real: in a **sideways, choppy** market the lines cross back and forth endlessly, each cross a small loss, and the indicator becomes a whipsaw machine. Built from lagging EMAs, it is always a step behind price — by design.

This is the recurring theme of every indicator in this module. RSI, MACD and the moving averages are all derived from the same price you can already see; they repackage it usefully, but none of them know the future, and each has a regime where it shines and a regime where it misleads. The 37% figure is not a knock on MACD specifically — it is a reminder that no single indicator, used mechanically, carries an edge on its own. The edge lives in *combining* a momentum read like MACD with trend, structure and disciplined risk control.

:::example
A grounded way to use MACD: let price and a moving average decide the trend first. In a confirmed uptrend, treat a bullish MACD cross as *timing* for a continuation entry near support — not as a reason to buy in isolation. Use the histogram to gauge whether momentum is still building, and divergence to warn you when to step aside. The cross is one input among several, never the whole decision — remember, alone it is barely better than a coin flip.
:::

## Quick recap

- MACD is two EMAs distilled: **MACD line** (12-EMA − 26-EMA), **signal line** (9-EMA of MACD), and the **histogram** (the gap between them).
- The **signal-line crossover** is the headline trigger but, across 7,640 Nifty 50 events, only **37% beat +5% in 30 days** and 58% were positive — close to a coin flip alone.
- The **zero line** gives trend context (above = uptrend, below = downtrend), but a mechanical MACD>0 filter **did not** improve the cross on this data.
- The **histogram** changes before the cross — a shrinking histogram is an early warning that momentum is fading.
- **Divergence** between price and MACD flags a trend losing fuel — a warning to manage risk, not a dated signal.
- MACD shines in trends and whipsaws in ranges; it always lags, because it is built from lagging averages.

Moving averages, RSI and MACD all describe direction and momentum — but none of them measure *volatility*, the width of price's daily swings. Next, Bollinger Bands wrap price in a volatility envelope, and we will see why "touching the band" is one of the most misread signals in technical analysis.
