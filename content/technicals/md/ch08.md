Markets trend in straight lines about a third of the time and spend the rest of their lives going sideways, coiling, and faking people out. That is inconvenient, because the breakout — price finally bursting out of a range — is the single most exciting event on a chart, and excitement is exactly when traders make their worst decisions. The breakout *looks* like the start of a big move every time. Most of the time it is a trap.

This chapter is about the two-thirds of market life that isn't a clean trend: channels and ranges, how to trade inside them, and the moment they end. We will give the breakout the honest treatment the data demands — including the uncomfortable number that more than half of them fail within a week — and we will cover the throwback, the pullback after a breakout that shakes out the impatient and rewards the patient.

## Channels and ranges

A **range** is a sideways market: price oscillating between a horizontal support floor and a horizontal resistance ceiling. A **channel** is the same idea tilted — price moving between two *parallel* sloping lines, an up-channel or a down-channel. Both are just support and resistance working together to box price in.

{{image: ch08-channel-types.png | The three corridors price travels in: a flat horizontal range between support and resistance, a rising up-channel of parallel trendlines, and a falling down-channel - each a box defined by two boundaries price oscillates between. | diagram}}

To draw a channel: first draw the trendline along the swing lows (in an uptrend) or swing highs (in a downtrend), then draw a **parallel** line touching the opposite extremes. Price tends to travel between the two. A horizontal range is the special case where both lines are flat. The discipline is the same as drawing any trendline — you need real touches at both boundaries, and you should refuse to force a channel that the price action does not actually respect.

## Trading inside a range

While price is boxed in, the playbook inverts everything trend-followers do:

- **Buy near support, sell near resistance.** Fade the edges, not the middle. The high-probability trades happen *at* the boundaries, where you are wrong quickly and cheaply if the box breaks.
- **The middle of the range is no-man's-land.** Entering mid-range gives you a poor entry and an unclear stop. Patience at the edges beats action in the middle.
- **Your stop lives just outside the box.** Buy support with a stop a little below it; sell resistance with a stop a little above. The break of the boundary *is* your "I was wrong" signal.

:::tip
Inside a range, the boundary that just rejected price is your reference for both entry and risk. Buy the support touch with a stop just under the floor — small risk, the whole range as reward. The trader who waits for the edge gets a better price and a tighter stop than the one who acts in the middle out of boredom.
:::

## Breakout versus fakeout — the number that matters

Eventually price leaves the box. A **breakout** is a close beyond the boundary — above range resistance or below range support — and it promises a new trend. The promise is broken more often than it is kept.

{{image: ch14-breakout-fakeout.png | The base rate behind the hype: of 20-day breakouts across 16 years of Nifty 50 data, 56 percent were fakeouts that fell back inside the range within five sessions. Requiring above-average volume cut the failure rate to 47 percent - better, but still close to a coin flip. | data}}

Here is the measured truth across 16 years of Nifty 50 data: of all **20-day breakouts, 56% were fakeouts** — price closed beyond the level and then fell back inside the range within **5 sessions**. More than half. The breakout that looks like the obvious start of a trend is, on the base rate alone, slightly *more* likely to be a trap than a launch.

:::warn
The breakout is the most over-traded event in technical analysis precisely because it feels so convincing. A clean break of an obvious level triggers a rush of buyers — and that rush is exactly what lets larger players sell into the move and fade it back inside. Treat every fresh breakout as guilty until proven innocent.
:::

## Volume on a breakout

If most breakouts fail, what separates the real ones? Conviction — and conviction shows up as volume. A genuine breakout should come on a surge of volume: it means real demand is overwhelming the supply that was capping price, not just a few orders nudging it over the line on a quiet day.

The data backs this, with appropriate humility. Filtering for breakouts on **more than 1.5x the average volume cut the fakeout rate from 56% to 47%** — and those breakouts also delivered better forward returns. That is a real, worthwhile improvement. It is also still a coin flip: nearly half of even the high-volume breakouts failed.

:::fact
Volume helps; it does not save you. A breakout on greater than 1.5x average volume failed **47%** of the time versus **56%** for breakouts overall. Demanding volume tilts the odds in your favour — from worse-than-even to roughly even — but there is no filter that turns a breakout into a sure thing. Position size and a stop, not faith in the volume bar, keep you safe.
:::

```python
from openalgo import ta

# A simple, objective breakout test with a volume filter:
# a new 20-day high on above-average volume is a *higher-quality*
# breakout - not a guarantee, just better odds than a quiet one.
df["hh20"] = ta.highest(df["high"], 20).shift(1)
df["volavg"] = ta.sma(df["volume"], 20)
df["breakout"] = (df["close"] > df["hh20"]) & (df["volume"] > 1.5 * df["volavg"])
```

A related caution carries over from how trends behave at their edges: a close that pokes *outside* a band is not automatically a signal. In trending markets price can ride along an upper boundary for a long time — a close above the upper Bollinger band, for instance, still returned **+0.58% over the next 10 days**, i.e. it kept drifting up rather than snapping back. "Touched the edge" is not the same as "must reverse," and it is not the same as "must continue" either.

## The throwback and the retest

Even a *real* breakout rarely runs cleanly. Price frequently surges out of the range and then comes **back** to the breakout level before resuming — a **throwback** after an upside break, a **pullback/retest** after a downside break. This is where the trapped-trader psychology from the last chapter pays off: the old resistance, now broken, becomes support, and price returns to test that flipped level.

The throwback is brutal for the impatient. Traders who chased the breakout at its high get a sudden loss as price falls back, panic out near the retest — and then watch the move resume without them. The patient trader does the opposite: rather than chase the initial break, they **wait for the retest** of the broken level, entering as the old ceiling proves itself the new floor. The entry is at a better price, with a stop just on the other side of the level, and with the fakeout largely filtered out — because a fakeout usually fails to hold the level on the retest in the first place.

:::key
Don't chase the break — wait for the box to be re-tested. A genuine breakout pulls back to the broken level and holds; a fakeout falls back through it and stays inside. Letting price come to you on the throwback both improves your entry and screens out a chunk of the 56% that were never going anywhere.
:::

## Range-to-trend transitions

The whole arc of this module now connects. Ranges are where trends are born and die — accumulation and distribution, in Dow's language from Chapter 5. A long, dull range is the market storing energy; the breakout is the release. The transition from range to trend is the most profitable single move on a chart *when it is real*, which is exactly why it is so heavily faked.

So hold the two ideas together. Respect the range while it lasts — fade the edges, keep stops outside the box. Respect the breakout when it comes — but demand volume, wait for the retest, and assume guilt until the level proves it has flipped. The market spends most of its time in boxes; your job is to trade the box honestly and to be sceptical, not euphoric, on the day it finally breaks.

## Quick recap

- A **range** is a sideways box between flat support and resistance; a **channel** is the same between parallel sloping lines (up or down).
- **Inside a range:** buy near support, sell near resistance, avoid the middle, and keep stops just outside the box.
- **Most breakouts fail:** **56%** of 20-day breakouts fell back inside within 5 sessions. Treat a fresh breakout as guilty until proven innocent.
- **Volume helps, doesn't guarantee:** breakouts on >1.5x average volume failed **47%** of the time (vs 56%) with better returns — better odds, still near a coin flip.
- Poking outside a boundary isn't automatically a signal: a close above the upper Bollinger band still drifted **+0.58%/10d** — price can walk the band.
- Don't chase — wait for the **throwback/retest**. A real breakout returns to the broken level and holds (role reversal); a fakeout falls back through it.
