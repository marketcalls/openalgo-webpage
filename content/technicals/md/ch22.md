The same stock can look like a textbook buy and a textbook sell at the very same minute. Open BEL on the weekly chart and you see a powerful, multi-year climb; drop to the 15-minute chart and you might catch it bleeding red, three down-candles in a row, RSI sinking. Both pictures are true. They just answer different questions.

That contradiction is not a flaw in the charts — it is the whole point of looking at more than one. The professional's habit is to never trade off a single timeframe. They zoom out to decide *which way* they want to lean, then zoom in to decide *when* to act. This chapter teaches that top-down routine: the higher timeframe for direction, the lower one for timing, and how to refuse the trades where the two disagree.

## Why one chart is never enough

A timeframe is just the amount of price packed into each candle. On a daily chart, one candle is one trading day. On a weekly chart, one candle is a whole week — roughly five daily candles squeezed into one shape. On a monthly chart, one candle swallows about four weekly candles.

Here is the consequence that trips up beginners: **a move that looks enormous on a small timeframe can be a single, meaningless wick on a large one.** A 4% two-day drop feels like a disaster when you are staring at the hourly chart. Zoom out to the weekly and that same drop is one small lower shadow on a candle still making higher highs. Same price, same week, opposite emotion.

If you only ever look at one timeframe, you are blind to the context that decides whether your signal matters. The day-trader who shorts every red hourly candle inside a roaring weekly uptrend is fighting the tide. The investor who buys a "cheap dip" without noticing the weekly chart broke down months ago is catching a falling knife. Neither has done anything wrong on their own chart — they have just looked at only one.

:::key
The higher timeframe is the ocean; the lower timeframe is the wave. You can surf the wave, but if you ignore which way the tide is running you will get dragged out. Direction comes from the big picture; timing comes from the small one.
:::

## The higher timeframe sets the direction

Start at the top. For a swing trader working off daily charts, the "higher timeframe" is usually the weekly. Your one and only job here is to answer a simple question: **is this market in an uptrend, a downtrend, or going sideways?** You are not looking for an entry. You are deciding which side of the trade you are even allowed to take.

You already have every tool you need from earlier chapters. On the weekly chart, ask:

- Is price making higher highs and higher lows (up), lower highs and lower lows (down), or neither (range)?
- Is it above or below a long moving average, and is that average sloping up or down?
- Where are the major weekly support and resistance levels — the big walls price has to deal with?

Look at BEL on the weekly view and the answer is not subtle. From a low of 174.7 on 12 February 2024, the stock climbed to 468.4 by 6 March 2026 — a gain of 168.2%, built from a clean series of higher highs and higher lows. On the weekly timeframe, this is an uptrend, full stop. The top-down rule that follows is blunt: in a market that looks like this, you hunt for *buys*, not shorts. The tide is going up.

## The lower timeframe times the entry

Once the weekly chart has told you the direction, drop to the daily to find the *moment*. The higher timeframe says "lean long"; the lower timeframe answers "but not at any price — wait for a pullback, a level, a trigger."

This matters because even the strongest uptrends do not go straight up. Inside BEL's 168% weekly advance there were dozens of daily pullbacks — stretches where the stock fell for days and every short-term indicator screamed weakness. Those daily dips are not the trend ending. In a higher-timeframe uptrend, they are the discount window: the place where you get to join the move at a better price than chasing a fresh high.

The data gives this a measured, honest edge. Across the Nifty 50, when a stock pulled back to a *rising* 50-day average, it returned +0.67% over the next 10 days, versus +0.58% for any day already in an uptrend. That is a real edge — but a thin one. A dip to a rising average is a slightly-better-than-average place to buy, not a magic floor that guarantees a bounce. The higher timeframe earns you the right to buy the dip; it does not promise the dip will hold.

:::fact
On the daily chart, BEL's 50-day average crossed above its 200-day on 12 May 2025 at 322.8, and the stock then gained 19.8% over the next 60 sessions. That entry worked partly because it agreed with the weekly trend - a long signal pointing the same way the bigger tide was already flowing.
:::

This is also why you should resist trading the *very* lowest timeframes for direction. On the daily, BEL's Supertrend flipped 25 times in two years — twenty-five "change your mind" signals inside one clean weekly uptrend. The smaller the timeframe, the more noise you get and the more often you are whipsawed. Use the small chart to time, never to decide direction.

## When the timeframes disagree

The most useful thing multiple-timeframe analysis does is tell you when to *stand aside*. The cleanest setups happen when the timeframes agree: weekly up **and** the daily giving you a pullback-and-turn into the trend. The most dangerous trades happen when they fight.

There are three states to recognise:

- **Aligned (act).** Weekly uptrend, daily pulling back to support or a rising average and starting to turn up. This is your A-grade long. Both tides push the same way.
- **Conflicted (wait).** Weekly up but daily breaking down hard, or weekly down but daily ripping up. One of them is lying to you, or a turn is genuinely underway and unconfirmed. The honest move is usually no trade — you have no edge when your own charts argue.
- **Aligned down (avoid or short).** Weekly downtrend and daily weak. If you are long-only, this is simply "do not buy here," no matter how oversold and tempting the daily looks.

The classic beginner mistake is letting the exciting small timeframe override the boring big one. A thrilling daily breakout inside a weekly *downtrend* is exactly the kind of move that fails — you are buying a bounce in a market that wants to go lower. When in doubt, the larger timeframe wins, because it represents more money, more participants and more conviction.

:::warn
A signal on a lower timeframe that contradicts the higher timeframe is not a brave contrarian trade - it is usually just early and wrong. If the weekly says down and your 15-minute chart says buy, you are not smarter than the market; you are looking at noise. Demote the small chart to timing only.
:::

## Aligning weekly and daily in practice

You do not need three monitors and ten timeframes. For most swing traders, two are enough — one to set direction, one finer to time. A common, sensible pairing is **weekly for the map, daily for the move**. A pure intraday trader might shift the whole stack down a notch (daily for direction, 15-minute for timing), but the logic is identical: pick two timeframes about four-to-five times apart, and give each one job.

Computing this is straightforward. You can resample your daily data up to weekly and read the trend from there, then time the entry on the daily series:

```python
from openalgo import ta

# Daily data in df, indexed by date
weekly_close = df["close"].resample("W").last()
weekly_trend = ta.ema(weekly_close, 20)      # weekly direction filter

df["ema20"]  = ta.ema(df["close"], 20)       # daily timing
df["sma50"]  = ta.sma(df["close"], 50)       # daily pullback reference
```

Read the weekly EMA's slope for direction; use the daily averages to find a pullback and a turn. The tools are the same ones you learned for a single chart — you are just applying them at two zoom levels and only acting when both agree.

## A simple top-down checklist

Run this in order, top to bottom, before every trade:

1. **Weekly — direction.** Up, down or sideways? Higher highs/lows? Above or below the long average, and which way is it sloping? This decides whether you may go long, short or neither.
2. **Weekly — the walls.** Mark the major weekly support and resistance. Is price arriving at a wall (be cautious) or breaking free of one (be interested)?
3. **Daily — location.** Is price at a sensible *entry* spot — a pullback to support or a rising average — or extended far from it? Never buy into thin air just because the weekly is up.
4. **Daily — trigger.** Is there an actual signal to act on (a turn off the level, a confirming candle, a momentum cross) *in the direction the weekly allows*?
5. **Conflict check.** Do the two timeframes agree? If they fight, the trade is "no." Stand aside.

Notice what this checklist quietly does: it stops you taking most trades. That is a feature. By the time a setup survives all five questions, you are entering with the bigger tide behind you and a defined spot to act — not chasing a single exciting candle on a single chart.

## Quick recap

- A timeframe is just price-per-candle; the same move can look huge on a small chart and trivial on a big one. Never decide off one chart alone.
- **Higher timeframe = direction.** On the weekly, BEL ran +168.2% in a clean higher-high/higher-low uptrend — that decides which side you may trade.
- **Lower timeframe = timing.** Use the daily to buy pullbacks into the trend; a dip to a rising 50-day average gave a thin +0.67%/10d edge, not a guaranteed bounce.
- Smaller timeframes are noisier — BEL's daily Supertrend flipped 25 times in two years inside one weekly uptrend. Use small charts to time, never to set direction.
- When timeframes disagree, the larger one wins and the honest move is usually no trade.
- Run the five-step top-down checklist; its job is to *reject* most setups and only pass aligned ones.
