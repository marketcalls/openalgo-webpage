A single candlestick is the smallest unit of story a chart can tell. Open, high, low, close - four numbers squeezed into one shape, drawn from a single day's tug-of-war between buyers and sellers. Sometimes that shape is loud: a long body says one side ran the day from open to close. Sometimes it is a whisper: a tiny body with long wicks, where price travelled far in both directions and ended almost where it began.

This chapter is about those whispers - the doji, the hammer, the shooting star, the marubozu and the spinning top. Each is a named shape that *hints* a shift in control may be coming. The word to hold onto is *hint*. A single candle is the start of a sentence, not the whole paragraph. Read in isolation it is nearly worthless; read in the right place - which is the next chapter's job - it can be the first real evidence of a turn.

## First, re-read the anatomy

Every pattern below is just a particular arrangement of three measurements, so keep them straight:

- The **body** is the thick part - the distance between **open** and **close**. A green/hollow body means the close was above the open (buyers won the session); a red/filled body means the close was below the open (sellers won).
- The **wicks** (or shadows) are the thin lines above and below the body, reaching to the session's **high** and **low**. A wick marks ground that price *visited but could not hold*.
- The **range** is the full distance from high to low.

In code, every pattern is a comparison of these pieces. You rarely need a library for this - plain arithmetic on the OHLC columns does it:

```python
body  = (df["close"] - df["open"]).abs()
rng   = df["high"] - df["low"]
upper = df["high"] - df[["open", "close"]].max(axis=1)   # upper wick
lower = df[["open", "close"]].min(axis=1) - df["low"]     # lower wick
```

## The doji: a market that can't decide

A **doji** is a candle whose open and close are virtually the same, so the body shrinks to a thin horizontal line. Price may have swung widely during the session, but by the end neither buyers nor sellers had gained ground. It is the chart's way of drawing a shrug.

A doji after a long, one-directional run is the more interesting kind. If a stock has climbed for days on fat green bodies and then prints a doji, the relentless buying has, for one session at least, stalled. That is not a sell signal - it is a *loss of momentum* worth noting. There are flavours: a **gravestone doji** (long upper wick, open and close near the low) shows buyers pushed up and got rejected; a **dragonfly doji** (long lower wick, open and close near the high) shows sellers pushed down and got rejected. Both are rejection stories with the close back at the open.

:::key
A doji means *indecision*, full stop. It does not say "reverse" - it says "the prevailing pressure just paused." Whether that pause becomes a reversal or a brief rest depends entirely on what surrounds it. On its own, a doji is a question, not an answer.
:::

## The hammer and the hanging man: same shape, opposite meaning

A **hammer** has a small body near the *top* of the range and a long lower wick - ideally twice the body or more - with little or no upper wick. The story: sellers drove the price sharply down during the session, then buyers stormed back and closed it near the high. That long lower wick is rejected selling.

Here is the catch that separates a learner from a chartist: **the exact same shape means different things depending on where it appears.**

- After a **downtrend**, near a support level, that shape is a **hammer** - a hint that sellers are exhausting and buyers are stepping in.
- After an **uptrend**, near resistance, the identical candle is called a **hanging man** - a warning that sellers appeared intraday for the first time in a while, even though buyers managed to claw the close back up.

The candle is identical. Only the *context* - the trend it sits in - flips its meaning from bullish to bearish. This is the single most important idea in candlestick reading, and it is why the next chapter exists.

:::warn
You cannot identify a hammer or a hanging man from the candle alone - you must know the trend it appears in. Anyone who labels a long-lower-wick candle "bullish" without checking what came before it is guessing. The wick shows rejection; the trend tells you rejection *of what*.
:::

## The shooting star and inverted hammer: the mirror image

Flip a hammer upside down and you get a small body near the *bottom* of the range with a long *upper* wick. Same context rule applies:

- After an **uptrend**, near resistance, it is a **shooting star** - buyers pushed to a new high, then sellers slammed it back down to close near the low. A bearish hint.
- After a **downtrend**, the same candle is an **inverted hammer** - a tentative bullish hint that buyers tried to lift price, and even though they faded, the attempt itself shows demand waking up.

The long upper wick is the key: it is buying that was *rejected*. In an extended uptrend, that rejection is a small crack in the floor of demand.

## The marubozu: total conviction

The opposite of a doji is a **marubozu** - a long body with **no wicks at all** (or barely any). Price opened at one extreme and closed at the other, never looking back.

- A **bullish marubozu** opens at the low and closes at the high: buyers controlled every minute.
- A **bearish marubozu** opens at the high and closes at the low: sellers ran the whole session.

A marubozu is not an indecision candle - it is a *conviction* candle. It often appears as a breakout bar, when price clears a level and runs. The message is momentum, not reversal: whatever direction it points, one side dominated completely and that pressure frequently carries into the next session.

:::example
Picture a stock that has drifted sideways in a range for two weeks, then one day prints a big green **marubozu** that closes above the top of the range on heavy volume. That single candle is doing real work: it shows buyers overwhelmed the supply that had capped the stock. Compare that to a doji on the same day - same range broken, but a doji would say the breakout had no conviction behind it and might fail. The shape *is* the information.
:::

## Spinning tops: small body, two wicks

A **spinning top** has a small body sitting between *two* wicks of meaningful length - upper and lower. Price ranged widely on both sides but closed near where it opened. Like the doji, it signals indecision and a balance of power, but with a touch more drama: both sides showed up and fought, and neither won.

A cluster of spinning tops after a strong trend is often the visual signature of a trend running out of fuel - the smooth one-way bodies giving way to choppy, undecided ones. Again: a heads-up to pay attention, not a trade trigger.

You can flag the indecision candles mechanically - a tiny body relative to the day's range:

```python
doji         = body <= 0.10 * rng          # body under 10% of range
spinning_top = (body <= 0.30 * rng) & (upper > body) & (lower > body)
```

## What a single candle can - and can't - tell you

Be honest about the ceiling on this. A single candle captures **one** session of supply and demand. That is genuine information - conviction, rejection, indecision are all real and readable. But one session is a small sample, and noise is everywhere. The same hammer that marks a beautiful bottom on one chart marks nothing at all on a dozen others.

So what *can* one candle reliably tell you?

- **Who won today** (body direction and size).
- **Where price was rejected** (the wicks).
- **Whether conviction is rising or fading** (marubozu versus doji/spinning top).

What it *cannot* tell you on its own is **what happens next**. For that, the single candle has to be read together with the trend it sits in, the level it forms at, and the volume behind it. A hammer floating in the middle of a featureless drift is just a candle. A hammer at a tested support level, on heavy volume, after a clean downtrend, is a setup.

:::key
A single candle whispers; it never shouts a forecast. Its job is to flag *that something may be changing* and tell you *who is currently in control*. Turning that whisper into a decision needs context - and acting on a lone candle, with no level, no trend and no volume behind it, is the most common beginner mistake in all of charting.
:::

## Quick recap

- A **doji** (open ~ close) and a **spinning top** (small body, two wicks) both signal **indecision** - a pause in the prevailing pressure, not a reversal call.
- A **hammer** and a **hanging man** are the *same shape* (long lower wick); only the trend they appear in decides whether the meaning is bullish or bearish.
- A **shooting star** (uptrend) and an **inverted hammer** (downtrend) are the mirror image - a long *upper* wick showing rejected buying.
- A **marubozu** (full body, no wicks) is a **conviction** candle: momentum in one direction, often a breakout bar - the opposite of indecision.
- One candle reliably tells you **who won today** and **where price was rejected** - but never, by itself, what happens next.
