"The trend is your friend" is the most quoted line in trading, and most people stop reading before the rest of it: *until the bend at the end*. A trend will carry you further than any forecast you could make, right up to the day it quietly stops, usually while you are still congratulating yourself. The skill is not spotting a trend; a child can see a line going up. The skill is knowing, in advance and by rule, the exact point at which you will admit the trend has bent.

This chapter turns Dow's abstract "primary direction" into something you can draw, count and act on. We will define the three trend states precisely, learn to draw a trendline that means something rather than one drawn to flatter a position, and, most importantly, separate an ordinary pullback (buy the dip) from a real change of trend (get out). Get that one distinction right and you are ahead of most chart-watchers.

## Three states: up, down, sideways

Everything price does fits into three structures, and we met the definition last chapter:

- **Uptrend**, higher highs and higher lows. The buyers keep paying up, and dips keep finding support before the previous low.
- **Downtrend**, lower highs and lower lows. Rallies fail earlier each time; declines cut deeper.
- **Sideways (range)**, roughly equal highs and lows, price oscillating in a horizontal corridor. Markets spend a surprising amount of their life here, and we give ranges their own chapter next.

{{image: ch06-trend-types.png | The three trend states drawn as price structure: an up-staircase of higher highs and higher lows, a down-staircase of lower highs and lower lows, and a flat sideways corridor. | diagram}}

The reason this matters is that the *same tactic* works in opposite directions depending on the state. In an uptrend you buy weakness. In a range you sell strength and buy weakness at the edges. In a downtrend, buying weakness is how accounts die. Step one of any analysis is naming the state, out loud, honestly, before you do anything else.

## Drawing a trendline that means something

A trendline is just a straight line connecting the *lows* in an uptrend or the *highs* in a downtrend, extended forward as a moving floor or ceiling. The rules for a valid one are simple and worth being strict about:

- In an **uptrend**, connect the rising **swing lows**. The line sits *below* price and acts as support.
- In a **downtrend**, connect the falling **swing highs**. The line sits *above* price and acts as resistance.
- You need **at least two points to draw** a line, but it takes a **third touch** to confirm it. Two points are a guess; the third is the market agreeing with your guess.

:::warn
The cardinal sin is forcing the line. If you find yourself cutting through candle bodies, ignoring a touch that doesn't fit, or redrawing the line every few days to keep a losing trade "valid," you are not analysing, you are rationalising. A trendline you had to fight the chart to draw is telling you the trend isn't there.
:::

How many touches make it real? More touches mean more traders see the same line, which makes it more meaningful, and also more obvious, so the eventual break gets crowded. Three to four clean touches is the sweet spot: enough to be real, not so many that the line is exhausted. A line touched eight times is not "stronger"; it is tired, and the next test often breaks it.

One practical wrinkle carries over from the chapter on scales: on a long-running chart, draw your trendlines on a **logarithmic** scale. On a linear scale, a line that connected the early lows of a multi-year rally will look hopelessly broken halfway up, because equal *percentage* moves take ever-larger point distances. A 10% move from 100 is 10 points; the same 10% from 400 is 40. A log scale keeps the percentage rise constant, so a trendline that genuinely tracks a percentage-based uptrend stays meaningful for its whole length rather than appearing to "break" simply because the price got bigger.

## A real uptrend, measured

Here is what a textbook uptrend looks like in the wild, with no airbrushing.

{{image: ch06-bel-uptrend.png | BEL in a multi-year primary uptrend: a clean staircase of higher highs and higher lows that ran from 174.7 on 12 February 2024 to 468.4 by 6 March 2026, a gain of 168.2 percent, with a rising trendline you could draw under the swing lows. | chart}}

BEL rose **168.2%**, from **174.7 on 12 February 2024 to 468.4 on 6 March 2026**. That is the whole point of trends: a single, well-structured primary uptrend did more than any clever in-and-out trading would likely have managed over the same window. But notice what the chart does *not* show, a straight line. It is a staircase, full of pullbacks that each looked frightening in the moment. Every one of those dips was a "is the trend over?" decision, and every one of them, until the structure actually broke, was a higher low.

The slope of a moving average is an objective read on trend direction and strength: a steadily rising 50-day line sitting under price points to an intact, healthy uptrend, while a flattening line warns the move is stalling.

## Trend strength and angle

The *angle* of a trend tells you about its health, and steeper is not better.

- A **shallow, sustainable** slope, price grinding up at a gentle angle, repeatedly reclaiming a rising moving average, is the kind of trend that lasts for months.
- A **near-vertical** slope is a warning, not a celebration. Parabolic moves are emotional, run on the final phase of buying, and tend to collapse as fast as they rose. A trendline that has to be redrawn ever-steeper is describing a market running out of buyers, not gaining them.

A useful, objective proxy for strength is the slope of a moving average (above) or simply the spacing of the staircase: are the higher lows still comfortably above the rising average, or is each dip cutting closer to it? Dips that increasingly puncture the average are the trend losing its grip even before the structure formally breaks.

## When a trendline breaks, and the pullback trap

This is the part that separates traders from spectators. Not every dip is a reversal, and not every trendline break is the end of the world.

A **change of trend** is a *structural* event: in an uptrend, it is the day price closes decisively below the **previous higher low**, not merely below the drawn line. A trendline can break while the higher-highs/higher-lows structure stays intact, that is often just the trend changing *angle*, settling into a gentler slope. The swing-low break is the real signal.

A **pullback** is a secondary move *within* an intact trend, a dip that holds above the prior higher low and resumes. The whole BEL staircase above was a series of pullbacks that resolved upward. And here the data gives a sober reality check on the popular "buy the dip to the moving average" tactic: across 16 years of Nifty 50 stocks, **buying a dip into a rising 50-day average returned +0.67% over the next 10 days, versus a +0.58% baseline** for any day in an uptrend. That is a real, positive edge, and a *tiny* one. The rising average is a sensible place to look for support; it is not a magic floor that pays you for showing up.

:::key
A trendline break tells you the *angle* changed. A broken **swing low** (or swing high) tells you the *trend* changed. Anchor your exit to the structure, the prior higher low, not to a line you drew by hand. Lines are for attention; structure is for decisions.
:::

:::tip
Three honest questions at every pullback: (1) Is the prior higher low still intact? (2) Is volume on the dip light, the way a healthy pullback should be? (3) Is the broader, higher-timeframe trend still up? Three yeses and it is probably a pullback. A no on the first, a broken swing low, and treat it as a change of trend until proven otherwise.
:::

## Putting it together

The routine is unglamorous and that is the point. Name the state (up, down, sideways). Draw the line under the lows or over the highs, and refuse to force it. Wait for the third touch before you trust it. Watch the angle for health, not excitement. And when a dip comes, as it always does, judge it against the prior swing point, not against your hopes. A trend really is your friend, but friendship here means following, not predicting. You let the staircase carry you, and you have decided in advance the single stair whose breach ends the relationship.

## Quick recap

- Three states: **uptrend** (HH/HL), **downtrend** (LH/LL), **sideways** (equal highs and lows). Name the state before doing anything.
- Draw a trendline along **rising lows** (up) or **falling highs** (down). Two points to draw, a **third touch** to confirm. Never force it through the candles.
- BEL's real uptrend ran **+168.2%** (174.7 on 12 Feb 2024 → 468.4 on 6 Mar 2026), a staircase of pullbacks, not a straight line.
- **Angle** signals health: shallow and sustained lasts; near-vertical is a warning, not a win.
- A broken **trendline** = the angle changed; a broken **swing low/high** = the trend changed. Anchor decisions to structure.
- Buying a dip to a rising 50-day average earned **+0.67%/10d vs a +0.58% baseline**, a tiny edge, not a guaranteed floor.
