A setup is a sentence you could hand to a stranger and they would take the exact same trades you would. "Buy BEL when it looks strong" is not a setup — it is a feeling. "Go long when the close is above the 200-day average and the 50-day is above the 200-day; exit when that stops being true" is a setup, because there is nothing left to argue about. Every term is measurable, every decision is made in advance.

Turning a chart read into rules like that is what separates a hobby from a process. But writing the rules is the easy half. The hard, humbling half is testing them honestly against real history — and being willing to hear that your clever idea barely beats doing nothing. This chapter builds a simple rule-based setup, runs it across 48 real stocks, and reads the uncomfortable truth in the results.

## From a hunch to a rule

A complete trading setup answers four questions, no exceptions:

- **Context** — *when am I allowed to even consider this trade?* (the trend, the timeframe, the market regime)
- **Trigger** — *what specific event puts me in?* (a cross, a breakout, a turn off a level)
- **Exit** — *what takes me out when I am right, and when I am wrong?* (a target, a trailing rule, a stop)
- **Size** — *how much do I commit?* (next chapter's job)

If any one of these is vague, you do not have a system — you have a story you will rewrite mid-trade to feel better. The discipline is to nail all four down *before* a rupee is at risk, so that in the heat of a live trade there is nothing to decide, only a rule to follow.

Our example setup is deliberately plain — a classic long-only **trend filter**:

```python
from openalgo import ta

df["sma50"]  = ta.sma(df["close"], 50)
df["sma200"] = ta.sma(df["close"], 200)

# In the market only when both conditions hold; otherwise sit in cash.
in_trend = (df["close"] > df["sma200"]) & (df["sma50"] > df["sma200"])
```

The rule: hold the stock whenever the close is above its 200-day average *and* the 50-day is above the 200-day; otherwise, be flat in cash. It is about as simple as a system gets — one trend filter, no targets, no cleverness. That simplicity is the point, because a simple rule is something we can actually trust the backtest on.

:::key
A real setup is a rule a stranger could execute identically. If your "system" needs you there to interpret it, it is not testable, not repeatable, and not a system - it is discretion wearing a costume.
:::

## Decide entry, exit and stop before you click

Notice that our rule defines the exit in the same breath as the entry. That is non-negotiable. The single most common way traders turn a winning idea into a losing account is by entering on a rule and exiting on an emotion — taking profits early out of fear, holding losers forever out of hope.

Three things must be fixed in advance:

- **Entry** — the exact condition that puts you in. ("Both averages line up and price closes above the 200-day.")
- **Exit-when-right** — what banks the win. A target, a trailing stop, or simply "stay until the trend rule breaks."
- **Exit-when-wrong** — the stop. The price or condition that admits the trade has failed and gets you out small. (We size and place this properly in the next chapter.)

Our trend filter folds the stop into the rule: the day the conditions stop being true, you are out. Crude, but defined. The virtue is that you never have to *decide* during the trade — the decision was made when you were calm.

## What a backtest actually shows

A backtest replays your rule over historical data and reports what it would have done. It is the closest thing technical analysis has to a laboratory — and it is where most beautiful ideas go to die. We ran our trend filter across 48 Nifty stocks over 16 years and compared it to the simplest possible alternative: buy the stock and just hold it.

The result is a masterclass in honesty.

{{image: ch23-backtest-universe.png | The 50/200 trend filter vs buy-and-hold across 48 stocks: it beat simply holding in only 4 of 48 cases (8%). Median total return was +107% for the filter against +744% for holding - yet median maximum drawdown was shallower (-47% vs -56%), and the filter was invested only about 55% of the time. A simple rule mostly underperforms holding, but it controls the downside. | data}}

Read those numbers slowly:

- The trend filter beat buy-and-hold in only **4 of 48 stocks — about 8% of the time.**
- Median total return was **+107% for the filter versus +744% for just holding.** Holding won, and not by a little.
- But the filter's median maximum drawdown was **−47% versus −56%** for holding — a shallower worst-case fall.
- And it was invested only **about 55% of the time**, sitting safely in cash for the other 45%.

So the honest verdict on our clean, sensible, widely-taught trend filter is: **it mostly underperformed simply holding the stock, while delivering a somewhat smoother ride and far less time exposed to the market.** It is not a money machine. It traded a large slice of return for a smaller drawdown and more peace of mind. Whether that is a good deal depends entirely on what you value — but anyone who sold you this rule as a path to riches was not looking at the data.

:::fact
This is the recurring lesson of the whole course, now measured end-to-end: a golden cross was positive only 59% of the time at 60 days; MACD crosses beat +5% only 37% of the time; and now a full trend-filter backtest underperforms holding 92% of the time. The edge in trading was never in the signal. It is in risk control and discipline.
:::

## Reading the equity curve

The single most useful output of a backtest is the **equity curve** — the running value of the account over time. It shows not just *how much* you made but *how* you made it: the smooth stretches, the violent drawdowns, the long flat periods of doing nothing.

{{image: ch23-backtest-equity.png | RELIANCE: the trend filter's equity (the rule sat in cash through much of the recovery) badly lagged simply holding the stock. The filter sidesteps some of the deepest declines but also misses the sharp rebounds off the lows - it is out of the market exactly when the biggest up-moves arrive. | chart}}

The RELIANCE curve tells the story behind the averages. The trend filter dutifully stepped aside during big declines — good — but because the rule needs the trend to *re-establish* before re-entering, it was sitting in cash through the early, explosive part of every recovery. Markets often make their largest single-day and single-week gains right off the lows, precisely when a lagging trend filter is still flat. Miss those, and you lag for years.

This is the trade-off no indicator can escape: **a filter that protects you from the worst declines also locks you out of the best recoveries.** Lag cuts both ways.

## Win rate is not edge

Beginners obsess over win rate — the percentage of trades that profit. It is the most misleading number in trading. You can win 90% of the time and still go broke if the 10% of losses are huge, and you can lose 60% of the time and grow rich if the 40% of wins are large enough.

What actually matters is **expectancy** — the average rupee outcome per trade:

```
expectancy = (win% × average win) − (loss% × average loss)
```

A system that wins just 40% of the time but makes 2 rupees on winners for every 1 it loses on losers has a positive expectancy: (0.40 × 2) − (0.60 × 1) = +0.20 per rupee risked. It loses more often than it wins and still grinds upward. This is why the **reward-to-risk ratio** is the lever that pays. A trader who religiously cuts losers small and lets winners run can be wrong most of the time and still win overall.

:::tip
Stop asking "how often does it win?" and start asking "what does it make per trade after costs?" A high win rate with tiny wins and rare giant losses is a slow bankruptcy. A modest win rate with disciplined reward-to-risk is a business.
:::

## The trap that ruins every backtest: curve-fitting

Here is the seductive danger. Our trend filter underperformed — so the temptation is to *tune* it. Try 47/210 instead of 50/200. Add an RSI condition. Skip trades in March. Keep twiddling and eventually you will find a combination that, on this exact history, beat buy-and-hold spectacularly.

You will also have built something worthless. That is **curve-fitting** (or overfitting): bending the rules until they perfectly explain the past, which guarantees nothing about the future. With enough parameters you can fit any historical wiggle — and a model tuned to old noise will be helpless against new noise. The market does not repeat; it rhymes, and a curve-fit system is memorising the verse instead of learning the rhyme.

The defences are simple and unforgiving:

- **Prefer fewer rules and round numbers.** A 50/200 filter that you did not optimise is more trustworthy than a 47/213 filter that you did.
- **Test out-of-sample.** Build the idea on one stretch of history, then check it on a stretch it never saw. A real edge survives the move; a curve-fit one collapses.
- **Test it across many instruments**, as we did with 48 stocks — not on the one chart where it happened to look brilliant.
- **Be suspicious of perfection.** A backtest with no losing years and a silky curve is almost always overfit. Real edges are lumpy and uncomfortable.

:::warn
If adding a rule makes the backtest much prettier, assume you are fitting noise until proven otherwise. The goal of a backtest is not to find the rule that fit the past best - it is to find a robust rule that will probably survive a future you cannot see.
:::

## From idea to a tested setup

Put the whole loop together:

1. **Write the rule** as a sentence a stranger could execute — context, trigger, exit, all defined in advance.
2. **Backtest it honestly** across many instruments and a long history, costs included.
3. **Read the equity curve and drawdown**, not just the headline return — a smoother −47% beat a richer −56% for many traders' nerves.
4. **Resist tuning.** If it only works after heavy optimisation, it probably does not work.
5. **Decide what you are buying** — return, smoothness, time-out-of-market — and accept the trade-off with open eyes.

## Quick recap

- A setup is four answered questions: context, trigger, exit, size — all fixed *before* the trade.
- Define entry, exit-when-right and exit-when-wrong in advance; the calm decision beats the heat-of-the-moment one.
- Our simple 50/200 trend filter beat buy-and-hold in only 8% of 48 stocks; median +107% vs +744% — but with a shallower −47% drawdown and only ~55% time invested. A simple rule mostly underperforms holding while controlling the downside.
- The equity curve reveals the trade-off: protection from the worst declines costs you the best recoveries. Lag cuts both ways.
- Judge a system by expectancy and reward-to-risk, not win rate.
- Curve-fitting makes the past look perfect and the future fail — prefer few rules, round numbers and out-of-sample tests.

A tested setup tells you *what* to do; it cannot tell you *how much* to risk on each trade — and that single decision matters more than the rule itself. Next, we put risk first: stops, reward-to-risk, the 1-2% rule, and the brutal math of drawdown.
