Raw price is a jittery thing. A stock can close up, down, up, down across a week and leave you squinting at the candles, unsure whether the trend is your friend or your trap. A moving average answers one honest question: ignoring the day-to-day noise, which way has price been leaning lately? It does that by taking the average close of the last N days and re-drawing it every day, so the line *moves* forward with price. The jagged becomes smooth, and a direction appears.

That smoothing is the whole appeal — and the whole catch. A line built from the past can only ever describe the past a little late. The most famous moving-average signals, the "golden cross" and the "death cross," are sold as buy and sell buttons. When we measured them on 16 years of Nifty 50 data, the buttons were wired backwards from what the textbooks promise. Let us build the tool properly first, then look at what it actually delivers.

## Two ways to average: SMA vs EMA

A **Simple Moving Average (SMA)** adds up the last N closes and divides by N. Every day in the window counts equally — the close from 50 days ago matters exactly as much as yesterday's. It is calm and steady, which is also its flaw: a sharp turn today is diluted by 49 older days.

An **Exponential Moving Average (EMA)** fixes the lag by weighting recent days more heavily. Yesterday's close moves the line more than the close from a month ago, so the EMA hugs price tighter and turns sooner. The cost is that it also reacts to noise sooner — more responsiveness, more false twitches. Neither is "better"; the EMA trades lag for jumpiness, the SMA trades jumpiness for lag.

```python
from openalgo import ta

df["sma50"]  = ta.sma(df["close"], 50)
df["ema50"]  = ta.ema(df["close"], 50)
df["sma200"] = ta.sma(df["close"], 200)
```

:::key
A moving average is a *follower*, never a leader. It can only tell you what price has already done, smoothed. Treat it as a description of the trend's direction and slope — not a prediction of the next bar.
:::

## The lengths everyone watches: 20, 50, 200

Three windows have become a shared language simply because so many traders watch them:

- **20-day** — roughly a trading month. A fast line for short-term swing direction.
- **50-day** — about a quarter. The classic "intermediate trend" gauge.
- **200-day** — close to a trading year. The line institutions cite when they call a stock "above its 200-DMA" — shorthand for a healthy long-term trend.

There is nothing magic in these numbers; they work partly *because* the crowd watches them, which makes them self-fulfilling levels of attention. A 50-day average on a daily chart and a 50-day average on a weekly chart describe completely different trends, so always know which timeframe you are on.

## A line you can lean on — sometimes

In a clean uptrend, price often pulls back to a rising moving average and bounces, as if the line were a moving floor. This is the "dynamic support" idea, and it has a *small* real edge. Across the Nifty 50, when an uptrending stock dipped to its rising 50-day average, the next 10 days returned **+0.67%** on average — versus **+0.58%** for any day in an uptrend. That is an edge of less than a tenth of a percent.

:::warn
The rising-average "floor" is a tendency, not a wall. The +0.67% vs +0.58% gap is real but tiny — about a rounding error. Price slices straight through these averages all the time. A moving average is a zone of *interest*, not a guaranteed bounce, and never a place to skip your stop-loss.
:::

## Slope is the real message

Before any crossover, read the **slope**. A moving average angling up means the average buyer of the last N days is in profit and the trend leans up; a flattening average means the trend is stalling; a rolling-over average means control is shifting. The angle tells you more than the exact price touch. A stock can sit *above* its 200-day average while that average flattens — momentum draining even though price is still technically "above the line."

## The golden cross and the death cross

The headline signals are crossovers of two averages. A **golden cross** is the 50-day average crossing *above* the 200-day — supposedly bullish. A **death cross** is the 50-day crossing *below* the 200-day — supposedly bearish. The names alone tell you which one financial media gets excited about.

Here is a textbook-perfect golden cross. BEL's 50-day average crossed above its 200-day on 12 May 2025 at 322.8, and the stock gained **19.8% over the next 60 sessions**. Exactly what the story promises.

{{image: ch16-bel-golden-cross.png | BEL's 50-day average crossed above its 200-day on 12 May 2025 at 322.8 and ran +19.8% over the next 60 sessions — a textbook golden cross that actually worked. | chart}}

And here is a death cross doing its job. TCS's 50-day average crossed below its 200-day on 17 February 2025 at 3904, and the stock fell **9.7% over the next 60 sessions**.

{{image: ch16-tcs-death-cross.png | TCS's 50-day average crossed below its 200-day on 17 Feb 2025 at 3904 and lost 9.7% over the next 60 sessions — the death cross behaving as advertised. | chart}}

Two clean wins for the textbook. But two charts are an anecdote, not evidence. So we measured **every** cross across the Nifty 50 — 527 golden crosses and 529 death crosses over 16 years — and the result inverts the folklore.

{{image: ch16-cross-base-rates.png | Across 527 golden and 529 death crosses on the Nifty 50: the golden cross was positive only 59% of the time at 60 days (about 64% at 120 days), while the death cross was positive 66% of the time on both horizons — more often up than the "bullish" signal. | data}}

The golden cross was positive only **59% of the time at 60 days**, with a median gain of just **+2.7%** — and the typical winning path still made you sit through a **−7.3% drawdown** before it worked. Stretch to 120 days and the median is **+6.4%**, which is actually *below* the roughly +9% you would have earned simply holding through the same period. The signal lags so badly that by the time the 50-day has crawled above the 200-day, much of the move is already gone.

Now the punchline. The **death cross** — the one you are told means "sell" — was positive **66% of the time at 60 days**, with a median gain of **+4.8%**. The supposedly bearish signal was *more often up*, and rose *more*, than the supposedly bullish one. Why? A death cross usually fires *after* a long decline, often near a washed-out, oversold low — precisely when the next bounce is brewing.

:::fact
On the Nifty 50, the death cross beat the golden cross on both how-often-up (66% vs 59% at 60 days) and median return (+4.8% vs +2.7%). "Golden cross = buy, death cross = sell" is not a rule — on this data it is closer to backwards. The cross tells you a trend changed *recently*; it does not tell you what happens next.
:::

The baseline matters here. The average Nifty 50 day drifts **+1.48% over the next 20 days** — roughly +4.5% over 60. Measured against that drift, the golden cross's +2.7%/60d is barely an improvement, while the cost of acting on it (the lag, the −7.3% drawdown) is very real.

## When the line turns too often

The deeper problem with any trend line is **whipsaw**: in choppy, sideways markets the averages cross back and forth, handing you a string of small losses. Adaptive trend tools like Supertrend — which trails price using volatility (ATR) rather than a fixed average — try to react faster, but they pay the same tax. On BEL, the Supertrend flipped direction **25 times in two years**. Most of those flips were noise, not trend changes.

{{image: ch16-bel-supertrend.png | A volatility-based trend overlay (Supertrend) on BEL flipped direction 25 times in two years. Faster-reacting trend tools cut lag but pay for it in whipsaw — every flip in a sideways market is a potential small loss. | chart}}

```python
from openalgo import ta
# A volatility-based trailing trend line (ATR-based)
supertrend, direction = ta.supertrend(df["high"], df["low"], df["close"], 10, 3)
```

This is the **lag trade-off** stated plainly. Slow averages (the 200-day, a wide SMA) give few signals, late — you keep most of a big trend but give back a chunk at every turn. Fast averages (a short EMA, a tight Supertrend) give many signals, early — you catch turns sooner but get chopped to pieces when there is no trend. There is no setting that removes the trade-off; you can only choose which side of it to pay.

:::tip
Use moving averages for what they are genuinely good at: reading the *direction and slope* of the trend, and framing context ("price is above a rising 50-day, so I favour long setups"). Do not use a single crossover as a standalone buy or sell trigger — the base rates say it barely beats just being invested, and often lags it.
:::

:::example
A practical, honest way to use the 50/200 relationship is as a *filter*, not a *trigger*: only hunt for long entries while the 50-day is above a rising 200-day, and stand aside when it is not. As a later chapter's backtest will show, this kind of filter does not make more money than buy-and-hold — it usually makes less — but it can meaningfully cut your drawdown and time in the market. That is a risk decision, not a profit machine.
:::

## Quick recap

- A moving average smooths price into a trend line; **SMA** weights all days equally (calmer, slower), **EMA** weights recent days more (faster, jumpier).
- The watched lengths — **20, 50, 200** — matter largely because the crowd watches them. Always know the timeframe.
- A rising average acts as *loose* dynamic support (+0.67% vs +0.58% baseline on a 50-DMA dip) — a tendency, never a guaranteed floor.
- **Slope beats the exact touch**: a flattening average warns of a stalling trend before any cross.
- The **golden cross** was positive just 59% at 60 days (+2.7% median, after a −7.3% drawdown) and lagged buy-and-hold by 120 days; the **death cross** was *more* often up (66%, +4.8%). "Golden = buy, death = sell" does not survive the data.
- Every trend tool pays the **lag trade-off**: slow = late but smooth, fast = early but whipsawed (BEL's Supertrend flipped 25 times in two years).

Moving averages smooth the trend but tell you little about *momentum* — how forcefully price is moving right now. Next we put a number on that pulse with the RSI, and discover that "overbought" does not mean what you have been told.
