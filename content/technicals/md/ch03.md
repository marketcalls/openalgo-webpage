Zoom all the way in on any chart, past the trends and the levels, and you reach the atom of technical analysis: a single candlestick. It looks like a tiny box with threads poking out of each end. It is, in fact, a compressed story — a whole trading day's worth of human hope, fear and second-guessing, packed into one shape you can read in under a second. Learn to read that one shape and every chart in this course becomes a sentence built from a vocabulary you already know.

The candlestick is a Japanese invention, centuries older than the computer, and it survived because it does one thing brilliantly: it shows you not just where price ended up, but *who was in control on the way there*. A line on a chart says "the price was here." A candle says "buyers tried to push it up, sellers slammed it back from this level, and by the bell the buyers had won — narrowly." That is a different, richer kind of information, and it is free in every bar.

## Five numbers, one shape

Each bar of data carries five numbers: the **open**, **high**, **low** and **close** (the OHLC you met last chapter), plus the **volume** traded. The candlestick draws four of them as a picture and leaves volume to a separate row beneath. Those four prices define the entire shape:

- The **open** and **close** mark the two ends of the thick rectangle in the middle.
- The **high** and **low** mark the tips of the thin threads above and below.

Two numbers for the box, two for the threads. That is the whole construction. From four prices, a shape; from the shape, a story.

```python
# Every candle's geometry comes straight from the OHLC columns
df["body"]       = (df["close"] - df["open"]).abs()   # size of the rectangle
df["upper_wick"] = df["high"] - df[["open", "close"]].max(axis=1)
df["lower_wick"] = df[["open", "close"]].min(axis=1) - df["low"]
```

You will rarely compute these by hand — your charting tool draws them for you — but seeing the arithmetic makes it concrete: the candle is not a symbol someone decided meant "buy." It is a faithful, mechanical drawing of four real prices.

## Anatomy: body, wick and shadow

Give the parts their names, because the rest of the course assumes them.

{{image: ch03-candlestick-anatomy.png | The anatomy of a candle: the thick body spans the open-to-close range, the wicks (or shadows) reach up to the high and down to the low. A green body closes above its open; a red body closes below. Body size shows conviction; wick length shows rejection. | diagram}}

The **body** is the thick rectangle between the open and the close. Its length is the single most important feature of a candle: it measures how far price travelled, net, from start to finish — the *margin of victory* for whichever side won.

The **wicks** — also called **shadows** — are the thin lines extending above and below the body. The upper wick reaches up to the high; the lower wick reaches down to the low. They record the ground that price covered intraday but could not hold. A long wick is a story of a push that got rejected.

That is the entire vocabulary: body, upper wick, lower wick. Everything else — every named pattern in Module C — is just combinations of these three parts across one, two or three candles.

## Colour: bullish versus bearish

Colour answers one question instantly: did the period close higher or lower than it opened?

A **bullish candle** closes *above* its open — buyers ended in control. It is conventionally drawn green or hollow. A **bearish candle** closes *below* its open — sellers ended in control — and is drawn red or filled. The colour is not about whether the stock rose versus *yesterday*; it is strictly open-versus-close *within this one period*. A stock can gap down hard at the open and still print a green candle if buyers clawed it back by the close. That nuance — green does not always mean "up day" — is exactly the kind of thing the colour reveals and a line chart hides.

:::key
The body answers two questions at once: its **colour** says who won (close above or below open), and its **length** says by how much. A long green body is decisive buying; a long red body is decisive selling; a tiny body of either colour is a near-draw. Before you name any pattern, just read the body — it is most of the message.
:::

## Long wicks: the language of rejection

If the body is conviction, the wicks are **rejection** — and they are where candlesticks earn their reputation. A long wick means price travelled a long way in one direction during the period and was then forcefully pushed back before the close.

A **long lower wick** says: sellers drove the price down, buyers came in underneath, absorbed the selling, and lifted it back up by the bell. Buyers defended that lower zone. A **long upper wick** says the opposite: buyers pushed the price up, ran into a wall of selling, and were shoved back down. Sellers defended that upper zone. The longer the wick relative to the body, the more emphatic the rejection. A candle with a small body and a long wick is one of the most informative single bars you can see, because it shows a clear shift of control *within* the period.

:::tip
When a long wick appears, ask *where* it appeared, not just that it appeared. A long lower wick stabbing into a level where the stock has bounced before is meaningful. The identical candle in the middle of empty space, with no level nearby, is mostly noise. The shape is the same; the context is everything — a point we build an entire chapter around later.
:::

## Marubozu and doji: the two extremes

Two named single candles sit at opposite ends of the conviction scale, and they are worth knowing now.

A **marubozu** is a candle that is almost *all body* — little or no wick at either end. It opened at one extreme and closed at the other, marching in one direction the entire period with no meaningful pushback. A green marubozu is pure, one-sided buying; a red one is pure, one-sided selling. It is the chart's clearest expression of conviction: one side was in control from the first tick to the last.

A **doji** is the mirror image: a candle with almost *no body* at all, because the open and the close finished at nearly the same price. Buyers and sellers fought all period and ended in a draw. A doji is the chart's clearest expression of **indecision** — a balance of power, a pause, a question mark. After a long run in one direction, that sudden balance can hint the trend is tiring. *Hint* is the operative word.

:::warn
Resist the urge to turn a single candle into a trade. A doji does not "mean reversal" and a hammer does not "mean buy" — not on its own. One candle is a single data point in an ongoing fight, and the data shows clearly that isolated patterns barely beat doing nothing. A single candle can raise a question; only the surrounding trend, level and volume can begin to answer it. We will measure exactly how weak lone patterns are when we reach the candlestick-pattern chapters.
:::

## Size and context: a candle whispers, it does not shout

Two final habits turn candle-reading from a parlour trick into a skill.

First, **read size relative to its neighbours**, not in the abstract. A "big" candle only matters if it is big *compared with the recent bars*. A long green body that dwarfs the last ten candles signals a real surge of demand; the same body in a stock that swings that much every day is unremarkable. Always judge a candle against its own recent range.

Second, remember that **a single candle whispers**. It tells you who won one round and how decisively — genuinely useful information — but it cannot tell you the fight's outcome. The story of a chart is told by candles *in sequence*: a long green body is more meaningful after a string of weak red ones; a doji counts for more at the top of a long climb than in the middle of a quiet drift. You are learning the alphabet here. The words and sentences — two- and three-candle patterns, and patterns read in full context — come in Module C, and they only make sense once a lone candle is second nature.

## Quick recap

- A candlestick draws four of a bar's five numbers — **open, high, low, close** — as a shape; volume sits in a separate row.
- The **body** (open-to-close) shows conviction by its length and direction by its colour; the **wicks** (to the high and low) show how far price was pushed and rejected.
- **Green/hollow** closes above the open (buyers won); **red/filled** closes below (sellers won) — strictly within that one period, not versus yesterday.
- A **long wick** is rejection — buyers or sellers defending a zone; a **marubozu** is one-sided conviction; a **doji** is indecision.
- Judge candle **size against recent bars**, and treat any single candle as a *whisper* — context (trend, level, volume) decides whether it matters.
- Isolated candle patterns barely beat doing nothing; their power, where it exists, comes only from where they appear.
