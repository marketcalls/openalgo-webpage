Momentum is the speed of a trend, and the Relative Strength Index is the most popular way to put a number on it. RSI takes the recent up-closes and down-closes, weighs them against each other, and squeezes the result onto a 0 to 100 scale. Near 100, buyers have utterly dominated the lookback window; near 0, sellers have. It is a clean, bounded reading of how *forceful* a move has been.

Every chart platform draws the same two dashed lines on the RSI panel: 70 and 30. And almost every beginner is taught the same false sentence, "above 70, sell; below 30, buy." It is one of the most repeated rules in trading, and on 16 years of Nifty 50 data it is simply wrong. Not "needs nuance" wrong, *backwards* wrong. The most overbought readings produced the **best** forward returns. Let us see why.

## What RSI actually measures

RSI compares the average gain on up-days to the average loss on down-days over a lookback period, 14 days by default. The ratio of those two averages is called Relative Strength (RS), and RSI rescales it with the formula `100 − 100 / (1 + RS)` so the output is always pinned between 0 and 100. If gains and losses are balanced, RS is 1 and RSI sits near 50. As up-days dominate, it climbs toward 100; as down-days dominate, it sinks toward 0. Because the formula is bounded, RSI, unlike price, can never run off the top of the chart, which is exactly what makes it comparable across a ₹200 stock and a ₹4,000 stock on the same scale.

Note that "Relative Strength" here is internal: RSI measures a stock against *its own* recent history, not against an index or another stock. That is a common confusion worth clearing early, RSI is a self-referential momentum gauge, nothing to do with comparative strength versus the Nifty.

The conventional labels: above **70** is "overbought," below **30** is "oversold." Those words are accurate as *descriptions*, at RSI 75 the stock genuinely has been bought hard. The error is treating the description as an instruction.

:::key
"Overbought" means *strong*, not *about to fall*. "Oversold" means *weak*, not *about to bounce*. RSI measures the momentum that already happened; it does not contain a timer for when that momentum will end.
:::

## The number that debunks the rule

We bucketed every Nifty 50 day by its RSI(14) reading and measured the average return over the *next* 20 days. The baseline, the drift of any random Nifty 50 day, is **+1.48%** over 20 days. Here is what each bucket actually delivered:

| RSI(14) bucket | Avg forward 20-day return |
| --- | --- |
| Below 30 ("oversold") | +1.8% |
| 50 to 70 (normal) | +1.4% |
| 70 to 80 ("overbought") | +1.4% |
| Above 80 (extreme) | **+2.2%** (60% positive, the best) |

Read that bottom row again. The **most overbought** stocks, RSI above 80, the deepest "sell" zone in the textbook, went on to the **highest** forward return of any bucket, and were positive 60% of the time. The oversold (<30) bucket did fine too at +1.8%, but the "obvious sell" at the top was the best place to *be long*, not short.

{{image: ch17-rsi-bucket-returns.png | Nifty 50 forward 20-day returns bucketed by RSI(14): the most overbought bucket (RSI > 80) delivered the best forward return at +2.19% and was positive 60% of the time, the opposite of "70 = sell." | data}}

The reason is mechanical. A stock reaches RSI 80+ precisely *because* it is in a powerful uptrend, and strong trends tend to keep trending. Selling strength because a momentum gauge is high means betting against the very force the gauge is measuring.

:::fact
On the Nifty 50, the RSI > 80 bucket, the "extremely overbought, definitely sell" zone, had the single best forward 20-day return of every bucket (+2.2%, 60% positive). The cliché "70 = sell, 30 = buy" does not survive contact with the data.
:::

## Overbought can stay overbought

The other half of the myth is timing. Even traders who know not to short strength assume "overbought" is a fleeting, one-day flag. It is not. On this data, RSI-above-70 streaks ran as long as **43 consecutive days**, and RSI-below-30 streaks as long as **44 days**. A reading is a *state* a stock can live in for weeks, not a same-day switch to flip.

So a stock can print RSI 75 and keep climbing for a month, while you wait for the "sell signal" to pay off. And oversold cuts the same way. Here is Asian Paints driving the point home: on 25 January 2024 its RSI hit **8.7**, about as oversold as a major stock ever gets, and the textbook screamed "buy." Over the next 20 days the stock managed only **+1.2%**, *below* the +1.48% baseline, and then kept grinding lower. In that single stock, RSI dipped below 30 on **76 separate sessions**; "oversold" was not a rare bottom-fishing signal, it was the stock's normal condition during a downtrend.

{{image: ch17-asianpaint-rsi.png | Asian Paints hit RSI 8.7 on 25 Jan 2024, deeply "oversold", yet returned only +1.2% over the next 20 days and kept falling. RSI dipped below 30 on 76 sessions in this stock alone; oversold is a state, not a buy button. | chart}}

:::warn
A deeply oversold RSI in a *downtrend* is not a bargain, it is confirmation the trend is strong, to the downside. Buying it because "30 = buy" is catching a falling knife. RSI extremes need the trend's context before they mean anything.
:::

## Where RSI earns its keep: divergence and context

If the threshold rule is broken, what is RSI good for? Two things.

**Divergence.** This is RSI's most respected use. A *bearish divergence* is when price makes a higher high but RSI makes a *lower* high, the new price peak was achieved with less momentum than the last, a hint the trend is tiring. A *bullish divergence* is the mirror: price makes a lower low while RSI makes a higher low, suggesting selling pressure is fading even as price slips. The logic is intuitive: price set a new extreme, but it took *less* underlying force to get there, so the engine driving the move is quietly losing power.

Two honest cautions about divergence. First, it can persist for a long time before price actually turns, a strong trend can throw off divergence signals for weeks while it keeps running, so divergence alone is a notoriously early and unreliable entry. Second, divergence is best read at the *extremes* of a move, near established support or resistance, not in the middle of a trend where it means little. Used well, divergence does not name a date; it is an early warning that the *force* behind a move is weakening, best paired with a price trigger, a broken trendline, a reversal candle, before you act.

**Trend vs range.** RSI behaves completely differently depending on the regime:

- In a **range**, price oscillates between support and resistance, and RSI swings obediently between its extremes, here the 70/30 levels are more useful as "the band is stretched" markers (still not standalone triggers).
- In a **trend**, RSI gets pinned. An uptrend keeps RSI riding the 60 to 80 zone for weeks; a downtrend pins it 20 to 40. Applying range rules to a trending stock is exactly how the "sell at 70" mistake bankrupts a momentum trade.

Some momentum traders even flip the thresholds in a strong trend: in a confirmed uptrend they treat RSI holding *above 40* as the sign of health, and a break below 40 as the warning, ignoring the 70 line entirely. You do not have to adopt that exact rule, but it captures the right instinct, the meaning of any RSI level depends entirely on the regime it is printing in.

:::tip
Read RSI as *context*, never as a trigger. In a confirmed uptrend, an RSI dip toward 40 to 50 (not 30) often marks the pause before continuation, far more useful than waiting for a "30" that a strong stock may never print. Let price and structure give the entry; let RSI describe the momentum behind it.
:::

## Tuning the lookback

The default is 14, but the lookback is a dial. A **shorter** RSI (say 7) reacts faster and hits extremes more often, more signals, more noise. A **longer** RSI (say 21) is slower and smoother, fewer, more deliberate swings. Shortening the period is the same trade-off you met with moving averages: more responsiveness bought with more false alarms. Most traders leave it at 14 not because 14 is optimal, but because it is the shared default everyone else is reacting to. Whatever you choose, the interpretation rules above do not change, the thresholds still describe a state, not an order to act.

:::example
A workable RSI routine: first decide the trend from price and a moving average. If the trend is up, *ignore* overbought readings and instead watch for shallow RSI dips and bullish divergence as continuation cues. If the trend is sideways, treat stretched RSI as a "this leg is extended" caution near the range edges. Either way, RSI confirms; it never leads.
:::

## Quick recap

- RSI puts momentum on a **0 to 100 scale** from average up-closes vs down-closes over a lookback (default 14).
- "70 = sell, 30 = buy" is **false** on this data: the RSI > 80 bucket had the *best* forward 20-day return (+2.2%, 60% positive); oversold (<30) returned +1.8%.
- Overbought and oversold are **states, not switches**, streaks ran up to 43 (overbought) and 44 (oversold) days; Asian Paints hit RSI 8.7 and still fell.
- RSI's real value is **divergence** (momentum fading against price) and **regime context** (it rides high in uptrends, low in downtrends).
- The **lookback** trades responsiveness for noise; the interpretation never changes, RSI describes momentum, it does not predict the turn.

RSI reads momentum but says nothing about the trend it is riding. The MACD tries to do both at once, blending two moving averages into a single momentum-and-trend tool. Next we build it, read its crossover, and measure how often that famous signal is worth acting on.
