Ask a new trader what they are working on and they will tell you about entries, the perfect pattern, the magic indicator, the signal that gets them in at the bottom. Ask a trader who has survived a decade and they will talk about something far less glamorous: how little they risk, where they get out, and how they make sure no single trade can ever take them out of the game.

That is the great inversion of this chapter. Profit is optional and unpredictable; survival is mandatory and entirely in your control. You cannot make the market go up, but you can decide exactly how much you lose when you are wrong, and that one decision, made before you enter, matters more than any signal in this course. We put risk first, because the trader who is still standing in five years is rarely the best analyst. They are the one who never let a bad trade end them.

## Risk comes first

Most people build a trade in the wrong order. They find a setup, get excited, buy, and *then*, maybe, think about where to get out. By then they are emotionally committed and every stop feels like an insult to their brilliant idea.

Flip it. Decide the risk *first*, and let the risk decide everything else. The sequence that keeps traders alive is:

1. Where will I admit I am wrong and get out? (the stop)
2. How much money am I willing to lose if that happens? (the risk budget)
3. *Therefore*, how many shares can I buy? (the position size)

Notice that position size is the *last* thing you calculate, not the first. You do not decide "I'll buy 500 shares" and then hope. You decide how much you are willing to lose, mark where the trade is wrong, and let the arithmetic tell you the size. Risk is the input; size is the output.

:::key
You control three things in a trade and none of them is the outcome: where you get in, where you get out, and how much you bet. Master those and the wins take care of themselves. Obsess over the outcome and you will manage none of them.
:::

## The stop-loss: deciding where you are wrong

A stop-loss is a price at which you exit a losing trade, no questions asked. It is not an admission of failure, it is the cost of being in the game, like the small ante that lets you sit at the table. Every professional is wrong constantly; the difference is they are wrong *small*.

A stop should live where your reason for the trade is broken, not at some round number that feels comfortable. If you bought because price held a support level, the stop belongs just below that level: if support gives way, your thesis is dead and you want out. If you bought a pullback to a rising average, the stop sits below the swing low that the pullback should not breach. The market, not your wallet, decides where you are wrong.

Two cautions from earlier chapters apply directly. First, support and resistance are *zones*, not exact lines, place the stop a little beyond the level, not exactly on it, so normal noise does not knock you out of a trade that is still valid. Second, remember that oversold can get more oversold and overbought can stay overbought; a stop is what protects you when a level you trusted simply fails, as levels eventually do.

:::warn
A stop you move *further away* to avoid being hit is not a stop, it is a wish. The entire point is that you set it when you are calm and honour it when you are scared. Widening it mid-trade is how a small, planned loss becomes the giant, unplanned one that does real damage.
:::

## Reward-to-risk: only take trades that pay you to be right

Before you take a trade, measure two distances: from your entry to your stop (the **risk**, R), and from your entry to a realistic target (the **reward**). Divide them and you get the **reward-to-risk ratio**.

Say you buy at 322.8 with a stop at 300, you are risking 22.8 per share. If a sensible target is 380, you stand to make 57.2. That is a reward-to-risk of about 2.5-to-1: you risk 1 to make 2.5. This single ratio is what makes win rate almost irrelevant. As the last chapter showed, a trader who wins only 40% of the time but keeps reward-to-risk above 2:1 still comes out ahead, because the wins are bigger than the losses. Cut losers at 1R and let winners run to 2R or 3R, and the math works *for* you even when you are wrong more often than right.

The practical rule: be picky. If a setup only offers a target a hair above its stop, a reward-to-risk below 1:1, it is rarely worth taking, however pretty the chart. You would need to win the large majority of such trades just to break even, and few setups do.

## The 1-2% rule

Here is the rule that quietly does most of the work of keeping accounts alive: **never risk more than 1-2% of your trading capital on a single trade.**

Risk, here, means the money you lose if your stop is hit, not the position's full value. On a 10,00,000 account, a 1% risk budget is 10,000 per trade. That is the most a single trade is allowed to cost you. With that cap in place, a string of losses is survivable and unremarkable: even ten consecutive losers, a genuinely terrible run, dents the account by roughly 10%, not 50%. You live to trade the eleventh.

Compare that to the trader risking 20% a trade. Three losses in a row, entirely normal, as our backtests showed signals fail more often than they succeed, and they are down nearly half their account, staring at the recovery math we are about to see. The 1-2% rule is not timidity. It is what makes a losing streak an annoyance instead of an ending.

:::tip
The 1-2% is a budget, not a target. You are not trying to lose 1%, you are ensuring that *if* you do, it barely registers. Small, fixed risk is the single habit most strongly shared by traders who are still around years later.
:::

## Position sizing from the stop

Now the three pieces snap together into one formula. Once you know your risk budget and your stop distance, the number of shares is fixed, no guessing. The share count is simply your risk budget divided by the risk per share, where the risk per share is the entry price minus the stop price.

The arithmetic: 10,000 of allowed loss, divided by 22.8 of risk per share, is about 438 shares. That position is worth roughly 1,41,000, about 14% of the account, yet if the stop is hit you lose only the planned 10,000, your 1%.

The beautiful part is what happens automatically. **A tight stop lets you hold a bigger position; a wide stop forces a smaller one, and your money-at-risk stays constant either way.** A volatile stock whose stop must sit far away gets sized down; a calm stock with a close stop gets sized up. You are no longer guessing how many shares "feels right." The stop and the 1% rule decide it for you, and the decision is the same whether you are calm or terrified.

## The cruel math of drawdown

Why all this fuss over small losses? Because losses and the gains needed to recover them are not symmetric, and the gap gets vicious as losses grow. A loss of X% requires a *larger* gain to climb back, because you are now recovering from a smaller base.

| Loss taken | Gain needed just to break even |
|---|---|
| −10% | +11% |
| −20% | +25% |
| −33% | +50% |
| −50% | +100% |
| −75% | +300% |
| −90% | +900% |

Read the bottom of that table and feel it. **A 50% loss does not need a 50% gain to recover, it needs 100%, a full double, just to get back to where you started.** Lose 75% and you must quadruple what is left. This is the mathematical reason deep drawdowns are so deadly: they do not just hurt, they steepen the climb out to something most traders never achieve, and they do it while your confidence is at its lowest.

This is also the real argument *for* risk management over chasing returns. Recall the last chapter's backtest: the trend filter's median worst drawdown was −47% against buy-and-hold's −56%. From −47% you need about +89% to recover; from −56% you need about +127%. Shaving the drawdown was worth real money on the way back up, which is exactly why a smoother, shallower equity curve can beat a higher-returning but deeper one for a trader who has to live through it.

:::fact
The math of recovery is why the 1-2% rule and stops are not optional extras. A trader who never lets a drawdown get deep never faces the brutal end of that table. The whole discipline of risk management is, at heart, a campaign to keep your losses in the top two rows.
:::

## Putting it together

A complete, risk-first trade plan, in order:

1. **Find the setup** (context, trigger), from everything in this course.
2. **Mark the stop** where your thesis would be wrong, below the level, beyond the noise.
3. **Check reward-to-risk**, is the target at least ~2x the stop distance? If not, pass.
4. **Set the risk budget**, 1-2% of capital, in rupees.
5. **Size from the stop**, risk budget ÷ (entry − stop) = shares. This is the only place your position size comes from.
6. **Execute and honour the stop**, the decision was made when you were calm; follow it when you are not.

Every step except the first is about defence. That is not pessimism, it is the recognition that you cannot control whether a trade wins, only how much it can cost you when it loses.

## Quick recap

- Risk comes first: decide the stop and the loss you can accept *before* the trade, and let those decide the position size.
- A stop-loss belongs where your reason for the trade is broken, a little beyond the level, never moved further away to dodge a hit.
- Reward-to-risk above ~2:1 lets you profit even with a sub-50% win rate; skip trades that pay less than they risk.
- The 1-2% rule caps each trade's loss so a losing streak is survivable, not fatal.
- Size from the stop: risk budget ÷ (entry − stop) = shares, a tighter stop allows more shares, a wider one fewer, with rupees-at-risk held constant.
- Drawdown math is merciless: −50% needs +100% to recover. Keeping losses shallow is the whole game.

You now have the analysis, the system and the risk math. One variable remains, and it is the one that breaks most traders: the person executing the plan. Next, the last edge, psychology, discipline, and where to go from here.
