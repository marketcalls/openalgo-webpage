Here is an experiment you can run on any chart. Pick a long, boring stretch where a stock drifts sideways and pick out every hammer, every doji, every engulfing candle in it. You will find dozens. Now check what happened after each one. Most led nowhere - price wandered on as if the "pattern" had never printed. The shapes were real; the signals were noise.

That is the uncomfortable truth this chapter is built on: **a candlestick pattern, by itself, has almost no predictive power.** A hammer in the middle of a featureless drift means nothing. The identical hammer at a support level that has held three times, in a stock that's been falling into it, on a day of heavy volume, is a genuine setup. Same candle, opposite worth - and the only thing that changed is the *context* around it. Learning to read that context is the difference between collecting pretty shapes and actually trading.

## Why context is everything

A candle pattern is a snapshot of one short tussle between buyers and sellers. Context is the answer to three questions about *where* that tussle happened:

1. **At what level?** Is the pattern forming at a price the market cares about - support, resistance, a prior high or low, a round number - or in the middle of nowhere?
2. **With or against the trend?** Does the pattern point the same way as the larger trend, or is it betting against it?
3. **On what volume?** Did the pattern form on conviction (heavy participation) or on a thin, sleepy session?

A pattern that scores well on all three is worth acting on. A pattern that scores well on none is decoration. Most patterns you'll spot are somewhere in between - and learning to grade them is the skill.

## Patterns at support and resistance

The single biggest filter is **location**. Levels are where the crowd's memory lives - where buyers stepped in before, where sellers capped the price before. A reversal pattern is the chart's way of saying "the prior pressure just got rejected here." That sentence only means something if "here" is a level where rejection was *plausible* in the first place.

- A **hammer or bullish engulfing at established support** is a high-quality signal: an independent reason to expect buyers (the level) and visible evidence they showed up (the candle) agree.
- A **shooting star or bearish engulfing at established resistance** is the bearish equivalent.
- The *same* patterns in open space, with no level nearby, are low quality - there is no reason for the move to stall there beyond the candle itself, and one candle is a thin reason.

This is also why Fibonacci, support/resistance and candlesticks are taught close together: a candle pattern that forms *at* a Fibonacci retracement that *also* lines up with prior support is three independent witnesses pointing at one price. That stacking - confluence - is what turns a coin-toss into an edge.

:::key
Always identify the level *before* you look for the pattern, not after. Mark your support and resistance on a clean chart first; then a reversal candle that prints exactly at one of those lines is meaningful precisely because you weren't hunting for it. A pattern you only "found" because you were staring at the current price is the bias talking, not the chart.
:::

## Patterns with and against the trend

The second filter is **direction**. A pattern that agrees with the larger trend is playing with the wind; one that fights it is spitting into the wind.

In a healthy uptrend, the high-percentage play is to use *bullish* reversal patterns at pullback lows - a hammer where the rising trend dips to support - to rejoin the trend, not to pick a top with a bearish pattern on every minor stall. Counter-trend reversals do happen, and the big ones are spotted exactly this way, but they fail far more often than continuation-friendly signals. A bearish engulfing in a roaring uptrend usually marks a pause, not the end.

This is also the trap in single candles that we met two chapters ago: a long-lower-wick candle is a *hammer* (bullish) in a downtrend but a *hanging man* (bearish) in an uptrend. The candle cannot be graded until you know the trend it sits in. Context doesn't just strengthen the signal - it can flip its meaning entirely.

:::tip
Default to trading reversal patterns that point *back into* the prevailing trend after a pullback, and treat patterns that fight a strong trend with suspicion. "The trend is your friend" is a cliche, but the data behind it is not: signals aligned with the larger move clear a lower bar to work than signals trying to call its top.
:::

## Volume confirmation

The third filter is **conviction**, and volume is how you measure it. A reversal pattern formed on heavy volume means many participants were involved in the change of control; the same pattern on thin volume may be a handful of trades that won't stick.

The cleanest evidence for this comes from breakouts, a close cousin of candle patterns. A 20-day price breakout - "the level has given way, the move is on" - **fails about 56% of the time**, sliding back below the breakout within five days. That is *worse than a coin toss*. But filter for breakouts on more than **1.5 times** average volume and the failure rate falls to about **47%**, with better follow-through. Volume doesn't make the signal a sure thing; it shifts it from a losing bet toward a slight edge. The same logic applies to a candle pattern: an engulfing on a volume spike is worth materially more than the identical shape on a quiet day.

:::fact
A 20-day breakout fails about 56% of the time on its own; adding a volume filter (over 1.5x average) cuts the failure rate to roughly 47% and improves returns. The number to remember is not 56 or 47 - it is the *idea*: even a respected signal can fail more often than it works alone, and confirmation is what tilts the odds. Treat candlestick patterns with exactly the same humility.
:::

## Why most patterns fail

It is worth being blunt about the base rate. Most candlestick patterns, taken in isolation, do not give you a meaningful edge over simply being invested. Across years of Nifty 50 data, the average stock drifts up about **+1.48% over any 20-day window** just by existing in a long-term uptrend. A pattern has to beat *that* to be worth the effort and the trading costs - and most, on their own, barely do.

The reason isn't that the patterns are fake. It's that markets are mostly noise, candles are tiny samples, and any shape will appear thousands of times by chance. The patterns that *do* earn their keep are the minority that form at the right place, in the right direction, with the right conviction. Context is not a nice-to-have garnish on the pattern - context is where essentially all of the edge lives. The candle is the trigger; the level, trend and volume are the reason.

## A confirmation checklist

Turn all of this into a routine you run *before* acting on any candlestick pattern. If a setup can't tick most of these boxes, pass on it - there will always be another.

- **Level:** Is the pattern forming *at* a meaningful support/resistance, prior high/low, round number or Fibonacci level? (No level -> skip.)
- **Trend:** Does the signal align with the larger trend, or is it a high-conviction reversal at the *end* of an extended move? (Mid-trend counter-trade -> skip.)
- **Volume:** Did the pattern form on above-average volume, or on a thin session? (Thin -> downgrade.)
- **Location in the swing:** Is price at a pullback extreme (a logical turning point) or stretched far from any reference? (Mid-air -> skip.)
- **Confluence:** How many *independent* reasons point at this price? One is weak; three is a setup.
- **Risk first:** Is there a sensible place for a stop just beyond the pattern's extreme, and does the reward justify it? (No clean stop -> skip.)

:::warn
The checklist exists to make you say *no*. A candle pattern is a reason to *investigate*, never an automatic reason to trade. The discipline is in walking away from the dozens of patterns that don't clear the bar - because the cost of trading every shape you see is a thousand small losses that no winning setup can repay.
:::

## Avoiding pattern-spotting bias

A final, very human warning. Once you learn these patterns, your brain starts seeing them *everywhere* - especially in the direction you already want price to go. This is **confirmation bias** wearing a chartist's hat: you want the stock to rise, so you notice the hammer and overlook the three failed hammers beside it; you stretch the definition until any candle qualifies.

The defences are concrete. Mark your levels and trend on a **clean chart first**, before looking for any pattern, so the structure isn't bent to fit your hope. Apply the **same definition every time** - a hammer's lower wick is at least twice its body, or it isn't a hammer. And keep a **journal**: when you log every pattern you traded and how it resolved, the patterns that don't actually work stop fooling you, because the record argues back. The chart doesn't care what you want. Your job is to read what is there, not to draw what you wish were.

:::key
The pattern is the *least* important part of a good setup. Where it forms, which way the trend runs, and whether volume confirms it carry the edge. Master the context and average patterns become useful; ignore the context and even the best patterns become a slot machine. Read the whole chart, not the candle.
:::

## Quick recap

- A pattern's worth is decided by **context**, not its shape: the same hammer is a signal at support and noise in open space.
- Grade every pattern on three filters - **level**, **trend alignment** and **volume** - and prize **confluence** when independent reasons stack on one price.
- Most patterns fail on their own; they must beat the market's natural **+1.48% / 20-day drift**, and confirmation (as with breakouts: ~56% failure alone, ~47% with volume) is what shifts the odds.
- Run a **confirmation checklist** designed to make you say *no*, and always define risk before acting.
- Beware **pattern-spotting bias** - mark levels on a clean chart first, use fixed definitions, and let a journal argue back.
