One candle whispers; two or three candles can argue a case. When you let a few bars speak together, a fuzzy hint sharpens into something closer to a statement: yesterday sellers were in charge, today buyers completely overpowered them, and the handover of control is visible in the shapes themselves. That hand-off, caught across two or three sessions, is what multi-candle patterns try to capture.

They are more reliable than a single candle for a simple reason: they require *more agreement* before they qualify. An engulfing pattern is not one good day; it is a bad day followed by a day so strong it erases the previous one. But "more reliable" is a relative phrase, and this is where most candlestick books oversell. Even the best of these patterns is a modest tilt in the odds, not a prophecy, and the proof of that is that the same shape that nails a famous turn on one chart fizzles on the next ten. We'll meet the patterns, then look hard at one real example and at what reliability honestly means.

## Engulfing: one candle swallows the last

The **engulfing** pattern is the workhorse of the group. It is two candles where the second body completely covers, engulfs, the first.

- A **bullish engulfing** appears in a downtrend: a small red (down) candle, then a larger green (up) candle whose body opens *below* the prior close and closes *above* the prior open. The day's buyers didn't just win, they erased the previous day's selling entirely.
- A **bearish engulfing** is the mirror in an uptrend: a small green candle swallowed by a larger red one. Sellers overwhelmed the prior session.

The logic is intuitive. For the second candle to engulf the first, one side had to show up with real size and reverse the whole prior session. That visible *change of control* is why engulfing patterns carry more weight than, say, a lone doji.

## The harami: a sudden loss of momentum

The **harami** ("pregnant" in Japanese) is the engulfing pattern reversed in size: a large candle followed by a small one whose body sits *entirely inside* the previous body. After a long green candle in an uptrend, a small candle huddled inside it says the buying surge has abruptly stalled. After a long red candle in a downtrend, a small inside candle hints the selling has paused.

A harami is a weaker signal than an engulfing, it shows *hesitation*, not a takeover. Nobody seized control; the dominant side simply ran out of breath. It is a flag to watch for confirmation, rarely a reason to act alone.

## Morning star and evening star: a three-candle turn

The **stars** are three-candle patterns, and they tell a more complete story of a reversal:

- A **morning star** (bullish, at a bottom) is: a long **red** candle (the downtrend in force), then a small-bodied candle that gaps or stalls lower (selling momentum fading, the "star"), then a long **green** candle that pushes well back into the first candle's body (buyers seizing control). Like dawn after night.
- An **evening star** (bearish, at a top) is the mirror: a long green candle, a small indecisive candle, then a long red candle driving down into the first body.

The middle candle is the pivot, it is the moment of balance between the old trend and the new. The third candle is the confirmation that control actually changed hands. Because they demand that full three-act structure, stars are among the more respected candlestick reversals.

:::key
Notice the pattern in the patterns: the more candles a signal requires, the more *agreement* it encodes, and the more weight it carries. A single doji is indecision; an engulfing is a takeover; a morning star is a takeover with a confirming follow-through. None of them is a guarantee, but a three-candle star asks the market to prove more before it qualifies.
:::

## Three white soldiers and three black crows

These are momentum, not reversal, signatures. **Three white soldiers** are three consecutive long green candles, each opening within the prior body and closing near its high, a steady, convincing advance, often seen as a downtrend ends and buyers take sustained control. **Three black crows** are the bearish mirror: three long red candles marching down, a sign of persistent, heavy selling.

The honest caution: by the time you have *seen* three big candles in one direction, a good chunk of the move is already behind you. These patterns confirm that a trend has strength; they are a poor place to enter late and chase.

## Tweezer tops and bottoms

A **tweezer** is two (or more) candles that share almost the same high (a **tweezer top**, bearish) or almost the same low (a **tweezer bottom**, bullish). The matching extremes show price tested a level twice and was rejected both times, a small double-test of support or resistance compressed into adjacent candles. On their own they are weak; at a level that already matters, they add a little confirmation.

## A real bullish engulfing, and why one example proves nothing

Now the part that keeps us honest. Here is a genuine, textbook-clean bullish engulfing on State Bank of India. On **30 December 2025**, at a swing low around **973.5**, after a pullback, SBIN printed a green candle that engulfed the prior day's red one. Over the next **10 sessions it rose about +5.7%**. The pattern worked.

{{image: ch10-bullish-engulfing.png | State Bank of India, daily: a bullish engulfing at a swing low on 30-Dec-2025 near 973.5, the green candle's body swallows the prior red one. Price rose about +5.7% over the next 10 sessions. One clean example is an illustration, not a base rate | chart}}

It is a beautiful illustration, and that is *all* it is. One winning example tells you the pattern *can* work; it tells you nothing about how *often* it works. This is the trap that candlestick lore is built on: the books show you the chart where the morning star nailed the bottom, and quietly skip the dozens where the identical shape appeared and price kept falling. A single labelled chart is selection bias dressed up as evidence.

:::warn
Never let a single perfect example set your expectations. For *any* pattern, you could find a chart where it worked gorgeously, that is true even of patterns with no edge at all, simply because markets generate thousands of candles. The question that matters is never "did it work here?" but "across hundreds of occurrences, how often did it work, and by how much versus just being invested?"
:::

## What "reliability" honestly means

So how reliable *are* multi-candle patterns? Modestly. They genuinely improve on single candles because they demand more confirmation, and a real change of control is real information. But the edge is small and fragile, and it lives almost entirely in *context*, which is the whole point of the next chapter.

Consider an analogy from measured data. A 20-day price breakout, another "the move has turned" signal, **fails about 56% of the time**, slipping back below the breakout level within five days. Add a volume filter (more than 1.5 times average volume) and the failure rate drops to about **47%** with better returns. Two lessons transfer directly to candle patterns. First, even a respected technical signal can fail *more often than it works* on its own. Second, *confirmation*, here, volume, is what shifts the odds from a coin toss toward a slight edge.

:::tip
Use multi-candle patterns as a *trigger to look*, never as a complete reason to trade. The pattern says "control may have changed here." Before you act, demand that it appear at a level that matters, in the direction the larger trend allows, ideally with a volume pickup. The pattern is the spark; context is the fuel, and without fuel, the spark dies.
:::

:::fact
The reason a bullish engulfing on heavy volume, at tested support, after a clean downtrend is worth more than the same candle floating in mid-air is not mysticism. It is that several independent conditions now agree, and agreement is what nudges a coin-toss signal toward a genuine edge. The candle pattern contributes one vote, an important one, but only one.
:::

## Quick recap

- **Engulfing** patterns (a candle that swallows the prior body) show a visible *change of control* and carry more weight than any single candle.
- The **harami** is the reverse, a small inside candle showing momentum *stalling*, a weaker, watch-and-wait signal.
- **Morning** and **evening stars** are three-candle reversals; the small middle candle is the pivot, the third candle the confirmation.
- **Three white soldiers / black crows** confirm momentum but appear late; **tweezers** mark a twice-tested level.
- One clean example (SBIN's +5.7% bullish engulfing) is an **illustration, not a base rate**, and even respected signals like a breakout fail more often than they work alone.
- Reliability is **modest** and lives in **context**; treat every pattern as a trigger to look, confirmed by level, trend and volume.

That last point deserves a chapter of its own, next we'll see exactly why a hammer in the middle of nowhere is noise, and the same hammer at strong support is a signal.
