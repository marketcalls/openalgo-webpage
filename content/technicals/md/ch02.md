Open any trading platform for the first time and the chart can feel like a wall of noise, a jagged red-and-green forest with numbers crawling up the side and dates along the bottom. Experienced traders look at the very same screen and read it like a sentence. The difference is not talent or a faster eye. It is knowing what each mark on the chart *is*, and learning to ignore the ninety percent that does not matter today.

A price chart only ever shows two things: **price**, on the vertical axis, and **time**, on the horizontal one. Everything else is a choice about how to draw the price, as a line, as a bar, or as a candlestick. Those three styles plot identical data and yet tell the story with very different richness. Get them straight and the wall of noise turns into information.

## Three ways to draw the same price

Imagine a single trading day for a stock. It opened at one price, traded as high as another, dipped as low as a third, and finally closed at a fourth. That is four numbers for one day. The three chart styles differ entirely in how many of those four numbers they bother to show you.

{{image: ch02-chart-types.png | The same price history drawn three ways, a line connecting closes, an OHLC bar showing all four prices, and a candlestick that fills the open-to-close range as a body. Each adds more of the day's story. | diagram}}

A **line chart** is the simplest: it plots only the closing price of each period and connects the dots. It throws away three of the four numbers. That sounds wasteful, but the close is the most important price of the day, it is where the market settled after everyone had their say, so a line chart is wonderful for seeing the big-picture direction without distraction. It is the wrong tool the moment you need to know *how* the price got there.

A **bar chart** (often called an OHLC bar) keeps all four numbers. Each period is a vertical stick spanning the low to the high, with a small tick on the left for the open and a tick on the right for the close. It is complete and compact, and it was the professional's chart for decades.

A **candlestick** chart shows the exact same four numbers as a bar, but draws the distance between the open and the close as a filled or hollow rectangle, the **body**, with thin lines above and below for the extremes. That single design choice, which we will dissect next chapter, makes the balance of buyers and sellers jump off the screen. It is why almost every trader today reads candles.

## OHLC: the four numbers in every bar

Whatever style you choose, the underlying data for each period is the same four prices, always in the same order:

| Field | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Open** | The first traded price of the period |
| **High** | The highest price reached during the period |
| **Low** | The lowest price reached during the period |
| **Close** | The last traded price of the period |

This is the famous **OHLC**. On a daily chart each bar is one trading day; on a weekly chart, one week. The four numbers are not decoration, they are the entire factual record of what happened. When you pull data with a tool like OpenAlgo, these are exactly the columns you get back, one row per period.

There is a fifth column too, **volume**, the number of shares traded in the period. Price tells you *what* happened; volume tells you *how convincingly*. We will give volume a chapter of its own, but keep it in the corner of your eye from the start.

## Reading the axes

Two axes, two jobs. The **vertical (price) axis** on the right or left tells you the rupee level of every point on the chart. The **horizontal (time) axis** along the bottom tells you when each bar occurred. They sound trivial, and that is exactly why they trip people up, the axes are where a chart quietly misleads you, a problem we devote a whole chapter to later. For now, two habits: always check what period one bar represents (a daily bar and a 5-minute bar look identical at a glance), and always read the price scale before reacting to a "huge" move, because a chart squeezed into a narrow range can make a one-percent wiggle look like a crash.

:::tip
Before you form any opinion about a chart, answer three questions out loud: *What stock is this? What is one bar, a day, a week, a minute? What is the price range top to bottom?* Skipping this is the single most common way beginners misread a perfectly normal chart as dramatic.
:::

## Why candlesticks win

If a bar and a candle carry identical numbers, why has the candle taken over? Because of how fast the human eye reads it. A candlestick uses **colour and a solid body** to encode the one thing traders care about most: did buyers or sellers win this period, and by how much?

When the close is above the open, the body is drawn hollow or green, buyers were in control. When the close is below the open, the body is filled or red, sellers won. The *length* of the body shows the margin of victory at a glance, and the thin wicks above and below show how far price travelled before being pushed back. You can scan fifty candles in a second and feel the rhythm of a battle. The same fifty OHLC bars require you to compare left-tick and right-tick positions one by one. Same data, far lower effort, and in a fast market, effort is everything.

:::key
A line chart shows you *where price went*. A candlestick shows you *how hard it was fought over to get there*. That extra layer, open versus close, and the rejection stored in the wicks, is why candlesticks dominate modern charting and why the next chapter is devoted entirely to reading a single candle.
:::

Read it the way the checklist taught you. The stock is RELIANCE; each bar is one trading day; the price scale runs from roughly 1,253 at the bottom to 1,612 at the top, a span of under 30%, so this is a normal, liquid large-cap doing normal things, not a crisis. Now the story: price pushed up to a high near **1,612**, then spent the back half of the window working lower, ending around **1,318**, meaningfully below where it peaked. No prediction required to see it; the chart simply records that buyers ran out of room near the top and sellers had the upper hand into the close of the period. A line chart of the same data would show you that drift down. The candles add the texture, which days were fought hard, which gave up easily, that we will learn to read next.

## Common chart-reading mistakes

A few errors trip up almost everyone at the start. Name them now and you will catch yourself doing them.

- **Reacting before reading the scale.** A move that looks violent is often one percent on a compressed axis. Read the price range first.
- **Confusing the timeframe.** A daily and an intraday chart look the same; the conclusions are not. Always know what one bar is.
- **Treating the latest bar as the whole story.** A single red candle is one day in a year. Zoom out before you decide anything.
- **Seeing patterns in pure noise.** The eye is a pattern-making machine and will find a "head and shoulders" in random wiggle. Demand context, a later chapter is entirely about this trap.
- **Forgetting volume exists.** Price without volume is half the picture. Glance at the bars underneath.

:::warn
The chart is a record, not a forecast. Everything to the left of the latest bar already happened; everything to the right is unknown. Reading a chart well means describing what *has* occurred accurately and honestly, not narrating a confident story about what comes next.
:::

## Quick recap

- A chart plots **price (vertical)** against **time (horizontal)**; everything else is a choice of how to draw the price.
- **Line** charts show only the close (great for direction), **bar** charts show all four OHLC prices, and **candlesticks** show the same four but make the buyer-versus-seller balance visible at a glance.
- Every bar carries **Open, High, Low, Close**, plus **Volume**, and on a daily chart one bar is one trading day.
- **Candlesticks dominate** because colour and body length let the eye read the outcome of each period instantly.
- On the real RELIANCE chart, price ran to about **1,612** then faded to close near **1,318**, strong stocks give gains back too.
- The classic mistakes are reacting before reading the **scale**, confusing the **timeframe**, and over-weighting the **latest bar**, fix these first.
