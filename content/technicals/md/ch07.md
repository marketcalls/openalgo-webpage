Markets have a memory, and it is made of people. A price where a crowd of buyers once stepped in, or where a wall of sellers once dumped stock, gets remembered, by the people who traded there, by the people who wished they had, and by the people who got trapped. Months later, when price returns to that same level, those memories turn into orders, and the level holds again. That is the entire mechanism behind support and resistance: not magic lines, but human memory priced into the order book.

The trap is to treat these levels as precise walls that price bounces off forever. They are not. A good level is a zone where the odds of a turn improve, and *every single one of them eventually fails*, that is how trends are born. This chapter is about marking the levels that genuinely matter, understanding why they form, and respecting the one truth most courses skip: support and resistance exist to be tested, retested, and finally broken.

## What support and resistance actually are

- **Support** is a price zone where buying has previously been strong enough to halt a decline. Demand overwhelms supply, and price bounces.
- **Resistance** is a price zone where selling has previously been strong enough to halt an advance. Supply overwhelms demand, and price stalls.

That is it. Support is a floor where buyers keep showing up; resistance is a ceiling where sellers keep showing up. Both are simply *memory of where supply and demand last fought*, and the more often and more recently they fought there, the more the level matters.

:::key
Support and resistance are not about the line being "strong." They are about a price level where enough traders share the same memory that their orders cluster there. The level holds because people *act* on the memory, until enough of them give up or get overrun.
:::

## The psychology behind a level

Why do orders cluster at a remembered price? Three groups of people, all looking at the same number:

- **The ones who bought there** and watched it work. When price comes back to that level, they buy more, it "worked last time."
- **The ones who missed it.** They watched price leave without them and swore they would buy if it ever came back. Now it has.
- **The ones who got trapped.** They bought too high, sat through a loss, and just want to get out "at breakeven." When price returns to their entry, they sell, creating resistance exactly where they bought.

That third group is the engine of **role reversal**, the most useful idea in the chapter. Notice that all three groups are reacting to the same level for different reasons, and their combined orders are what make the bounce or rejection happen. The level is self-fulfilling precisely because everyone can see it.

## Round numbers and prior highs and lows

Where do these levels come from? A few reliable sources:

- **Prior swing highs and lows.** The most important. A previous peak becomes resistance; a previous trough becomes support. These are the levels the structure itself remembers.
- **Round numbers.** 100, 500, 1000, 1500. Humans place orders at round figures, stops, targets, alerts all cluster there, so price often reacts at psychologically "big" numbers even with no prior history.
- **Prior consolidation zones.** A region where price spent a lot of time tends to act as a magnet and a barrier later; lots of volume changed hands there, so lots of memories live there.

:::tip
The best levels are the ones marked by *both* a structural reason and a round number, a prior swing high that also sits near 1500, say. When two independent reasons point to the same zone, more traders see it, more orders cluster, and the level carries more weight. We will call that overlap "confluence" again in the Fibonacci chapter.
:::

## Role reversal: the floor becomes the ceiling

The single most practical behaviour of these levels is that **once broken, they swap roles.** Broken resistance becomes support; broken support becomes resistance.

{{image: ch07-role-reversal.png | Role reversal: a resistance ceiling that price finally breaks above tends to become a support floor on the pullback, the same level, its role flipped, because the traders trapped above it are now made whole and defend it. | diagram}}

The mechanism is the trapped-trader story again. While a level is resistance, sellers who bought below are happy and the rally stalls. Once price *breaks above* and holds, those earlier buyers are now in profit, the doubters who sold are now wrong, and a fresh group wants to buy any dip back to the breakout point. So the old ceiling becomes the new floor. This is why a **retest** of a broken level, price coming back to touch it from the other side, is one of the cleaner, lower-risk entries in technical analysis: you are buying at a level the market has just demonstrated it will defend.

## Draw zones, not lines

Beginners draw support and resistance as a single, pixel-perfect line and then feel betrayed when price overshoots it by a few rupees. Professionals draw **zones**, a band a little wider than a single price, because that is how levels actually behave.

A level is a region of agreement, not a number. Wicks poke through it, closes pull back inside it, and different traders remember slightly different prices. Mark the zone from the *cluster* of highs or lows, not the single most extreme tick. A useful, mechanical starting point is to let a recent swing high and low define a band, say the highest high and lowest low of the last 20 sessions, and then let price tell you which edges matter. OpenAlgo's library can work those range boundaries out for you, but treat them as zones to watch, not exact lines to trade blindly.

## Why levels eventually fail, tested and broken

Here is the honest part nobody frames properly: **a level's whole job is to be broken eventually.** Support and resistance are not permanent walls; they are pressure points that hold until the supply/demand balance changes, and then they give way. Watching *how many times* a level is tested before it breaks tells you more than the level itself.

{{image: ch07-reliance-support-resistance.png | RELIANCE retesting its key levels: resistance near 1577 was tested on 14 separate sessions and support near 1291 on 13 sessions. Each touch is the market probing the level, and a level probed that many times is being worn down, not reinforced. | chart}}

On this RELIANCE chart, resistance near **1577 was tested on 14 separate sessions**, and support near **1291 on 13 sessions**. Read that carefully: those are not 14 successful "bounces" proving the level is invincible. They are 14 *attacks*. Each retest chips away at the supply sitting there, absorbing the sellers, until one day the orders are exhausted and price breaks through. A level tested over and over is not getting stronger, it is being **worn down**, and a much-tested ceiling that finally cracks often releases a sharp move because so much pent-up demand was sitting just above it.

:::warn
Do not confuse "tested many times" with "will hold forever." Repeated tests are a countdown, not a guarantee. The more often price grinds against a level, the more you should prepare for the break, and the breakout, not the bounce, is frequently the bigger trade. The next chapter shows why even that breakout fools you most of the time.
:::

:::fact
Levels are zones, not lines, and they are temporary. RELIANCE's resistance at ~1577 (14 tests) and support at ~1291 (13 tests) held repeatedly, until they didn't. That is the normal life cycle of every support and resistance level: form, get tested, get retested, and eventually give way to a new trend.
:::

## Putting it together

Mark the zones where price has a memory, prior swings, round numbers, busy consolidations, and draw them as bands, not razor lines. Expect broken levels to flip roles, and treat the retest as your higher-quality entry. Above all, hold the two ideas at once: a level improves your odds of a turn, *and* it will eventually fail. The trader who respects the level but is mentally prepared for its break is the one who neither fights the bounce nor gets blindsided by the breakout.

## Quick recap

- **Support** = a floor where buyers repeatedly step in; **resistance** = a ceiling where sellers repeatedly step in. Both are crowd memory of where supply and demand last fought.
- The **psychology** is three groups acting on one price: those who bought there, those who missed it, and the trapped traders waiting to get out at breakeven.
- **Role reversal:** broken resistance becomes support and vice versa; the **retest** of a broken level is a cleaner, lower-risk entry.
- Levels come from **prior swing highs/lows, round numbers, and old consolidation zones**, strongest where two reasons overlap (confluence).
- Draw **zones, not lines**, a band around the cluster of highs or lows, because wicks overshoot and traders remember slightly different prices.
- Every level eventually **fails by design**: RELIANCE's ~1577 resistance was tested on **14 sessions** and ~1291 support on **13** before giving way. Repeated tests are a countdown, not a fortress.
