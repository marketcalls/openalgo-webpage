Most indicators try to tell you *which way* price is going. Bollinger Bands do something humbler and more honest: they tell you *how much price is moving*. They wrap a moving average in two elastic lines that breathe in and out with volatility, squeezing tight when a stock goes quiet, flaring wide when it gets loud. That single idea, drawn well, quietly solves a problem most beginners never name: knowing when the market is coiled and when it is already running.

The trap is that the bands *look* like they give buy and sell signals. The upper line seems to scream "too high, sell"; the lower line seems to whisper "too low, buy". Sixteen years of Nifty 50 data says that reading is wrong often enough to bankrupt you. A close above the upper band on our universe still returned **+0.58% over the next 10 days**, positive, not negative. The bands measure volatility. They do not measure direction. Confuse the two and you will sell every winner the moment it gets strong.

## What the bands actually are

Bollinger Bands, created by John Bollinger in the 1980s, are three lines:

- A **middle band**, usually a 20-day simple moving average, the same smoothed trend line you met in the moving-averages chapter.
- An **upper band**, the middle band *plus* two standard deviations of price.
- A **lower band**, the middle band *minus* two standard deviations of price.

Standard deviation is just a measure of how spread out recent prices are. When a stock has been swinging hard, standard deviation is large, so the bands sit far apart. When a stock drifts sideways in a tight range, standard deviation shrinks and the bands close in. The width of the channel *is* the volatility, drawn to scale.

That ±2 standard deviation default is not magic. Statistically, if price moves were perfectly random and bell-curved, roughly 95% of closes would fall inside two standard deviations. Markets are not perfectly random, but the rule of thumb survives: most days, price lives inside the bands, and the band edges are where price is statistically *stretched*, not where it is *finished*.

OpenAlgo's library can calculate the three bands for you from a stock's closing prices, using a 20-day average and two standard deviations.

:::key
The middle band is the trend; the gap between the outer bands is the volatility. Bollinger Bands are two indicators in one, a moving average you already understand, plus a live volatility gauge wrapped around it. Read them as a *condition*, never as a *command*.
:::

## The squeeze: when quiet precedes loud

The most useful thing the bands tell you is when they get narrow. A **squeeze** is a period where volatility has collapsed, the bands pinch together because recent closes have barely moved. Quiet markets do not stay quiet forever. Pressure builds, and when it releases, the move that follows is often sharp. The squeeze does not tell you *which direction* the release will go, only that the calm is unusual and a bigger move is statistically overdue.

You can read the squeeze straight off the chart by eye, but the precise way to measure it is **bandwidth**:

> bandwidth = (upper band − lower band) ÷ middle band

A small bandwidth number means a tight squeeze. On the chart below, BEL's bandwidth compressed to roughly **4.7% on 4 September 2024**, an unusually tight coil after a long quiet stretch, before the bands expanded again as the next move developed.

Notice what the squeeze did *not* do: it did not point up or down. It said "this stock is unusually quiet, and quiet does not last." That is genuinely useful, it tells you *when* to pay attention, but it is only half a trade. You still need price itself, or another tool, to tell you which way to lean.

:::fact
A squeeze is a statement about volatility, not direction. The bands can pinch tight and then break *down* just as easily as up. Traders who assume a squeeze always resolves upward are reading a direction that simply is not there.
:::

## Walking the band

Here is where most beginners go wrong. In a strong trend, price does not politely return to the middle after touching an outer band. It *clings* to the band and rides it, a behaviour traders call **walking the band**. In a powerful uptrend, a stock can close near or above the upper band day after day, because the trend is so strong that "two standard deviations above average" keeps getting redefined upward.

If your rule is "sell when price touches the upper band," walking the band is the rule that destroys you. You would have sold the strongest part of the strongest moves, again and again, while the stock kept climbing. The band touch was not an exit signal. It was the signature of strength.

:::warn
"Price touched the upper band" is not a sell signal, and "price touched the lower band" is not a buy signal. In a trend, repeated touches of one band confirm the trend's power, the opposite of a reversal. Treating a band touch as a fade is one of the most expensive beginner mistakes in technical analysis.
:::

## Why a band touch is not a signal

We can put a number on this. Across the Nifty 50 universe over 16 years, days where price **closed above the upper band** went on to return **+0.58% over the next 10 days**, *positive*, and almost exactly in line with the average return of an ordinary day inside an existing uptrend. In other words, a close outside the upper band gave you no edge in *either* direction. It certainly was not the reliable top that the textbook picture implies.

Compare that to the course baseline: any random Nifty 50 day has averaged about **+1.48% over the next 20 days**. A band touch did not beat that baseline and did not invert it. It is a measure of *where price is relative to its recent range*, nothing more. If the bands gave free sell signals, the forward return after an upper-band close would be sharply negative. It is not.

:::example
On BEL's chart, watch what happens after the September 2024 squeeze resolves: as the move gets going, price repeatedly pushes the upper band rather than collapsing off it. Anyone who shorted the first upper-band tag, because the textbook said "overbought", was fighting the very strength the band was advertising. The band edge marked conviction, not exhaustion.
:::

## Bandwidth as a volatility cycle

Step back and the bands reveal a rhythm: **volatility cycles between quiet and loud, then back to quiet.** Bandwidth makes that cycle visible as a single line you can watch:

- **Low and falling bandwidth**, a squeeze; the stock is coiling; expect the calm to break eventually.
- **Rising, wide bandwidth**, volatility has expanded; a move is underway; the easy, explosive part may be maturing.
- **Very wide, then contracting**, the move is cooling; the stock is returning toward balance.

This is why Bollinger Bands pair so naturally with the volatility chapter idea of **ATR** (average true range), both answer "how much is this thing moving right now?" rather than "which way?". Knowing the volatility regime helps you size positions and set stops sensibly: a tight-squeeze stock needs a different stop than one whose bands are blown wide open.

## Pairing bands with other tools

Because the bands are silent on direction, they only become a decision tool when you combine them with something that *does* speak to direction. Sensible pairings:

- **Bands + trend.** Use the slope of the middle band (the 20-day average) to know which way the wind blows, then use band touches as evidence the trend is strong, not as fades.
- **Bands + a level.** A squeeze that resolves *through* a known support or resistance zone is far more meaningful than one that resolves into open space. The level tells you direction; the band told you the move was coming.
- **Bands + volume.** An expansion out of a squeeze backed by a genuine surge in volume is more convincing than a quiet drift outside the band, exactly the conviction question the next chapter tackles.

What the bands should *never* be paired with is another volatility indicator doing the same job. Stacking Bollinger Bands, Keltner Channels and an ATR panel together is not three confirmations, it is the same idea, drawn three times, fooling you into false confidence. We will name that trap directly in the combining-indicators chapter.

:::tip
A clean way to use the bands: let the *middle band's slope* tell you the trend, let a *squeeze* tell you a move is brewing, and let *price breaking a real level* tell you the direction. The bands set the stage; price and structure pick the play.
:::

## Reading the bands honestly

Put together, here is the honest job description for Bollinger Bands. They are a **volatility instrument wearing a trend line down the middle.** They excel at one thing, showing you when a stock is unusually quiet (squeeze) or unusually stretched (band touch) relative to its own recent behaviour. They are useless, even dangerous, as standalone buy and sell triggers, because price walks the band in exactly the trends you most want to hold, and a band touch carried no negative edge in the data at all.

Use them to answer "is this market coiled or running, calm or stretched?", and then let price, levels and volume answer "which way, and how convincingly?" That division of labour is the whole skill.

## Quick recap

- Bollinger Bands are a 20-day moving average plus and minus two standard deviations, the middle line is trend, the gap between the outer lines is volatility.
- A **squeeze** (narrow bands / low bandwidth) flags an unusually quiet market and a likely bigger move, but says nothing about direction. BEL squeezed to ~4.7% bandwidth on 4 September 2024 before expanding.
- In a trend, price **walks the band**: repeated touches confirm strength, they do not signal a reversal.
- A band touch is not a signal. A close above the upper band still returned **+0.58% over 10 days** in our data, positive, not a sell.
- Bandwidth turns volatility into a watchable cycle: quiet → loud → quiet.
- Pair the bands with a direction tool (trend, a level, volume), never with another volatility indicator that says the same thing.

Bands tell you *how much* price is moving. The next chapter adds the other half of conviction, *how many people are behind the move*. We turn to volume, the market's fuel gauge.
